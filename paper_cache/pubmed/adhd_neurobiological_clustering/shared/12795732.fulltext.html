Insomnia and psychiatric disorders are established comorbid conditions inflicting significant impairment to daily function [1]. Sleep disturbance is commonly reported in psychiatric disorders and may be a precipitating factor signaling symptom onset [2]. Understanding the bidirectional mechanisms connecting poor sleep to psychiatric traits provides an opportunity to define novel avenues for therapeutic intervention given sleep is a modifiable risk factor.

Genomic studies can provide insights into the shared biological mechanisms that raise individual risk for sleep and psychiatric disorders. In addition to large-scale genome wide association studies (GWAS) revealing loci for various common complex traits, they also offer a key opportunity to assess and detect genetic relationships between nominated traits. In the current issue of SLEEP, Xue and colleagues [3] expand on prior GWAS reports [4–6] by performing a cross-disorder analysis of public domain data to pinpoint the shared genetic risk loci for insomnia with 12 psychiatric conditions. Consequently, the study reports robust evidence of a shared genetic etiology between these two trait areas. Furthermore, through integration with cell-specific datasets, the authors implicate potential cell types responsible for mediating the risk conferred by these shared loci.

To quantify the genetic overlap between insomnia and each of the psychiatric conditions, the authors performed bivariate MiXeR analysis identifying substantial genetic overlap ranging from 28.1% to 83.7%. Consistent with prior studies [4–6], insomnia was positively correlated with each of the psychiatric conditions, with the largest proportion of overlap being observed with anxiety (rho beta = 0.921). This finding supports the hypothesis that insomnia is strongly correlated with mental health transdiagnostically rather than being limited to specific diagnoses [2]. The genetic associations derived from this MiXeR approach were higher than genetic correlations previously reported [4–6] given this bivariate model examines the proportion of shared variants irrespective of the direction of variant effects [7]. As a result, this method allows for variants to be causally associated with two traits even if they exhibit opposing effects.

Across 7 of the 12 psychiatric conditions, insomnia shared a total of 97 lead single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs). This included a region on chromosome 5 which has already been previously implicated in the shared genetic basis of sleep and psychiatric disorders [8, 9], with the lead SNP coinciding with a long non-coding RNA. Notably, the effect allele for the most significant SNP at this locus, rs77960, increases the risk for insomnia while it decreases the risk for ADHD. Indeed, non-protein coding RNAs are increasingly being recognized for their roles in the maintenance of cell-specific gene programs [10] making this an intriguing locus for follow-up.

Consistent with loci derived from GWAS, the majority of insomnia risk SNPs reside in non-protein coding regions [11]; however, 87.6% were located in regions of accessible chromatin, indicating they likely serve in a regulatory context. Pathway enrichment was subsequently performed to determine the biological context of the genes implicated by these shared variants. This analysis demonstrated an important role for GABAergic synapses in the observed shared genetic etiology, which is notable given that most hypnotic medications work through GABAergic mechanisms. Other enriched pathways similarly pinpointed were dendritic formation and synaptic signaling, emphasizing a role for neural development and maintenance in these disorders.

The public accessibility of large single-cell datasets has proven to be a useful resource for interrogating the functional significance of disease-associated genes. Xue and colleagues integrated the vast single-cell datasets available in the CellXGene resource [12] with the genes implicated through their SNP-based analysis. Looking across 36 different brain cell types, the authors identified eight key enriched cell types—four GABAergic and four glutamatergic. GABAergic cortical interneurons were significantly implicated in the shared genetic basis of insomnia and multiple psychiatric conditions. It is interesting to note that the authors found trait pairs were influenced by different subtypes of GABAergic neurons, while some trait pairs, such as insomnia and bipolar disorder, were additionally enriched in glutamaterigic cell types. This suggests that there is no singular mechanism underlying the neural dysfunction that contributes to these shared pathologies, and therapeutics to correct sleep may need to target distinct biological pathways depending on the specific psychiatric condition. Given that GABA and glutamate are the primary inhibitory and excitatory neurotransmitters, respectively, the genomic links between insomnia and psychiatric disorder may operate through altered excitatory/inhibitory (EI) balance. EI balance modulates the intrinsic excitability of neurons and is increasingly seen as an important etiologic factor in psychopathology [13], but it remains unexplored in relation to insomnia.

Increasingly, studies have sought to resolve the shared genetic basis between sleep and psychiatric disorders [4, 6, 9], homing in on the central cellular mechanisms that go awry in the development of these pathologies. Xue and colleagues importantly uncovered both shared and distinct genetic modifiers linking insomnia with specific psychiatric conditions. From a therapeutic perspective, it is important to recognize the distinct cellular mechanisms across disorders to mitigate negative side effects resulting from broadly acting treatments such as GABA agonists. Instead, future therapeutics may benefit from targeting subsets of GABAergic cells by acting on sub-types with specific genetic programs. One analysis performed by Xue et al. identified the most influential genes driving the associations within GABAergic subpopulations. This type of analysis can implicate the specific cell sub-types that are most vulnerable to disease. Transcriptional regulators, such as those encoded by TCF4 and RBFOX1, emerged as key factors driving associations in GABAergic subtypes. Indeed, orthologs to these genes have been shown to regulate sleep [14, 15] and development of inhibitory neurons [16, 17] across species, thus demonstrating critical conserved roles in cellular programming.

Although this study further highlighted the genetic programs underlying shared molecular mechanisms between sleep dysregulation and psychiatric traits, it was based on public domain data solely and did not conduct follow-up functional experiments. It demonstrates the utility of the huge wealth of genomic data now publicly available, though, such studies would be enhanced by key validation approaches in appropriate models. Collectively, this work provides additional loci for future functional assessment and sheds light on the cellular contexts in which these loci may exert their effects.
