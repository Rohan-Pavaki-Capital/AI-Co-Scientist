Little is known about the immune responses during acute asthma exacerbation. In this study, we examined immune responses in children following an acute asthma exacerbation.

Methods:

We evaluated pro-inflammatory cytokine levels and gene expression profiles in blood samples from pediatric patients admitted for acute asthma exacerbation. Viral PCR was performed to differentiate between viral or non-viral associated exacerbations.

Results:

Following informed consent, clinical data were obtained from 20 children with asthma (median [interquartile range, IQR] age 11.5 [8.0, 14.2]) years and 14 healthy age-matched controls (10.5 [7.0, 13.0]). Twelve had positive nasopharyngeal Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) for viral infection (11 Rhinovirus and 1 Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)). Nine were in the pediatric intensive care unit (PICU) and among them five required Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP). Mean (±SD) days on systemic steroids before drawing blood sample was 2.5 ± 1.6. Twelve had history of environmental allergies with 917 (274, 1,396) IU/ml total IgE (median (IQR)). Compared to controls, IL-1RA and IL-10 levels were significantly increased and TNFα significantly decreased in asthma subjects (p<0.05 for all). RNA-seq analysis revealed 852 differentially expressed genes in subjects with asthma. Pathway analysis found up-regulated genes and pathways involved in innate immune responses in subjects with asthma. Significantly reduced genes included pathways associated with T helper cell differentiation and activation.

Conclusions:

In acute asthma exacerbation, innate immune pathways remained increased while adaptive immune responses related to T helper cells are blunted and are independent of trigger or asthma severity. Our novel findings highlight the need to identify new therapies to target persistent innate immune responses to improve outcomes in acute asthma.

Asthma is the most common chronic disease in children and is associated with significant morbidity1. In the United States alone, it affects more than six million children2. As a leading cause of hospitalization in children, acute asthma exacerbation remains a significant problem that can cause significant morbidity and potentially be life threatening. An acute exacerbation can be induced by a variety of insults including respiratory infection, allergens, air pollution, or cigarette smoke. Heightened airway inflammation in response to infection or environmental exposure plays a pivotal role in bronchoconstriction that leads to acute symptoms and diminished lung function. While patients with asthma experience similar symptoms, underlying causes of asthma are highly heterogeneous with recent studies identifying distinct asthma phenotypes and endotypes that reflect different types of inflammatory responses3. Thus, it is important to understand how different triggers, whether viral or environmental, influence airway inflammation in acute exacerbation.

Asthma pathophysiology is centered on type 2 inflammation which is mediated by T helper 2 (Th2) cells, type 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2), and eosinophils4. Additional type 1 and type 17 inflammatory pathways, which involve Th1/17 cells and neutrophils, can also be involved and are associated with more severe disease5,6. The innate and adaptive immune responses during acute asthma exacerbation are not well understood, especially in children. With the rapid onset of symptoms and deterioration in lung function, acute exacerbation likely involves cellular and molecular pathways that are distinct from stable asthma. Recently Ramphul et al. reported that sputum from children with acute asthma had increased neutrophils and Th cell cytokine/chemokines levels, which were distinct from children with stable asthma7, but it is not known how inflammatory responses in the acute phase are impacted by exacerbation triggers (viral vs. non-viral) and asthma severity8–11.

In this study, we aimed to understand the immune response in acute asthma by examining systemic inflammation in blood samples from children hospitalized with acute asthma exacerbation. We assessed systemic inflammatory responses by measuring plasma pro-inflammatory cytokine levels and performing RNA-sequencing on whole blood samples. Our analysis revealed a gene expression profile that favors innate immune pathways, while pathways related to T cell activation were suppressed. Further understanding of these mechanisms may lead to identifying new biomarkers and developing more targeted therapies to improve outcomes and recovery after an acute exacerbation in children.

Methods:Enrollment:

This was a single center, prospective cross-sectional study. Institutional IRB approval was obtained (STUDY00000423) and written informed consent and/or assent was received prior to participation. Following informed consent, we enrolled 20 children ages 5–18 years old admitted to our tertiary care children’s hospital for acute asthma exacerbation. Clinical data was collected and analyzed in 20 children but RNA-seq data was available in 19 children because of missing blood sample in one remaining patient.

Inclusion and Exclusion CriteriaInclusion Criteria:

Children (age 5–18 years) previously physician-diagnosed with asthma admitted at tertiary care pediatric hospital with acute asthma exacerbation.

Controls:

Age matched healthy subjects without history of asthma, eczema, food allergies or allergic rhinitis were enrolled as controls.

Exclusion Criteria:

Children with other pulmonary diseases such as cystic fibrosis, bronchopulmonary dysplasia, sickle cell disease, any previous lung surgery, lung transplant, etc. Patients were also excluded if they had history of cardiac disease, auto-immune disease, connective tissue disease or any known immune deficiency.

Exclusion criteria for healthy control subjects included history of asthma or allergic disease (such as eczema, allergic rhinitis or food allergies), cardiovascular and/or any pulmonary disease other than asthma, prematurity, and bronchopulmonary dysplasia. Healthy subjects with prolonged use of medications such as antihistamines and corticosteroids were also excluded.

Study Endpoints:Primary Endpoints:

To define immune profiles (cytokine and gene expression) in pediatric patients with acute asthma exacerbation and compare to healthy control subjects.

Secondary Endpoints:

To determine impact of trigger (viral and non-viral) or severity of underlying asthma (mild persistent vs. moderate-severe persistent as defined by GINA guidelines) or severity of acute exacerbation (PICU admission vs. non-PICU admission) on immune profiles in children with acute asthma exacerbation.

Study Intervention:

After informed consent, we enrolled patients with different levels of asthma severity ranging from mild persistent asthma to severe persistent asthma. Demographic information including gender, race, exposure to smoking, family history of asthma, asthma severity and control in previous year, and Asthma Control Test (ACT) were obtained by questionnaire from all patients. Viral PCR (polymerase chain reaction) was done with FARVP (film array respiratory viral panel) from nasal secretions (nasal swab). Blood was collected, processed, and stored for later analysis. Data from hospital admission was collected from electric medical records (EMR). Demographic data and blood were also obtained from healthy controls without asthma.

Blood was drawn from patients for CBC with differential, and total and allergen specific IgE for common environmental allergens (house dust mites, cat, dog, cockroach and Alternaria). Cytokine levels were also measured in whole blood samples by multiplex ELISA plates following manufacturer’s protocols.

Viral PCR was done to determine if a viral infection was associated with the asthma exacerbation.

From healthy controls, blood was drawn for CBC with differential, total IgE, cytokine levels, and gene expression profiles.

ELISA:

Pro-inflammatory cytokines were measured by Meso Scale ELISA U-Plex assay (Meso Scale Diagnostics, Rockville, MD). Our cytokine panel included CCL11, CCL17, CXCL10, GM-CSF, IFN-γ, IFN-β, IL-1α, IL-1β, IL-1RA, IL-4, IL-5, IL-6, IL-8, IL-10, IL-13, IL-17A, IL-22, IL-23, TNF-α, MCP-1, TGF-β1, TGF-β2, and TGF-β3. Following manufacturer’s protocol12, custom U-Plex plates were coated with the appropriate linkers and antibodies for spot within each well. Fifty microliters of sample and serially diluted calibrator standards were loaded in duplicate onto the plate. Samples were allowed to incubate at 4⁰C overnight. The following day, detection antibodies were added and then plates were read on the MSD QuickPlex SQ 120MM Reader. Cytokine concentrations were calculated using the standard curve in the MSD Discovery Workbench analysis software.

RNA Isolation and Sequencing:

Approximately 5 mL blood was collected into Tempus™ Blood RNA Tubes (cat. no. 432792, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA) and then aliquoted into 2 mL cryovials and stored at −80⁰C. One patient in the acute asthma group was unable to provide enough blood for RNA-seq analysis, thus RNA-seq was performed on 19 acute asthma and 14 control samples. For RNA isolation, 2 mL blood was thawed on ice and RNA was extracted using the Tempus™ Spin RNA Isolation Kit (cat no. 4380204, Thermo Fisher Scientific), purified, and concentrated using RNA Clean and Concentrator kit (cat. no. R1013, Zymo Research, Irvine, CA). cDNA libraries were generated from 1 μg total RNA using the Stranded mRNA Prep, Ligation kit (cat. no. 20040532, Illumina, San Diego, CA) and IDT for Illumina RNA UD Indexes Set A, Ligation (cat. no. 20040553, Illumina). Pooled libraries were sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 at 2×150dp, 300 cycles. Data files have been deposited to NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus with accession number GSE256534.

Bioinformatic Analyses:

RNA-seq data was processed and analyzed using the Nationwide Children’s Hospital Franklin Cluster. Fastq files were trimmed with the BBDuk tool (ftm=5, ftl=1, ktrim=r, qtrim=r, trimq=10, k=23, mink=11, hdist=1, maq=10, minlen=25) from the BBTools suite13. FASTQC14 was used to verify fastq files before and after trimming. Reads were aligned with Hisat215 (-p=5, -k=2) against the GRCh38 genome16 downloaded from Gencode17. RSeQC18 was used to verify strandedness. Ten hemoglobin gene transcripts that are abundant in whole blood were excluded prior to downstream bioinformatic analyses19,20. Samtools21 was used to convert and sort bam files. Features were counted using HTSeq22 (-r=pos, -t= exon, -i=gene_id, -s=yes). Python programming language23 was used to collect, manage, and summarize data as needed. Differential analysis was performed with edgeR24 and annotated with the Bioconductor.org.Hs.eg.db package25. Significant genes were defined as having a log2 fold change = |1.5| and False Discovery Rate (FDR) <0.05. Differential analysis results are summarized in the differential analysis Supplementary File 1. Further analysis and visualization of data was performed using ggplot226, EnhancedVolcano27, ComplexHeatmap28, PCATools29, and matrixStats in the R environment30.

Ingenuity Pathway Analysis was used to identify enriched pathways among differentially expressed genes (IPA, Qiagen, Inc., https://www.qiagenbioinformatics.com/products/ingenuitypathway-analysis). Genes identified to be significantly different between asthma vs. healthy control samples were analyzed using the Core Analysis tool in IPA. We defined differentially expressed genes as log2 fold change |>1.5| and adjusted p-value (FDR) <0.05. Canonical pathways with p-value <0.05 (or >1.3 −log (p-value)) were considered significantly enriched. Z-scores were used to determine predicted pathway up- or down-regulation. Pathways enrichment results are in Supplementary File 2.

Statistical Analysis

Clinical data were summarized using frequency (percentage) for categorical variables, mean (standard deviation [SD]) for normally distributed continuous variables and median (interquartile range [IQR]) for skewed continuous variables.

Subjects with an acute asthma exacerbation and healthy controls were compared by demographic characteristics, total IgE, complete blood count measures, and inflammatory cytokines using the Wilcoxon rank sum, chi-square, or Fisher’s exact test, as appropriate. Among only subjects with asthma, we compared cytokine levels by various characteristics including gender, race, severity of underlying asthma, severity of acute exacerbation (PICU admission, use of CPAP/BiPAP), and viral infection using the Wilcoxon rank sum or Kruskal-Wallis tests depending on the number of groups. Non-parametric tests were selected due to the small sample size (N=14 controls, N=20 subjects with asthma) and failure to satisfy the normality assumption. In addition to presenting raw p-values, we calculated adjusted p-values to account for multiple testing using the Benjamini and Hochberg correction.

Two-sided p-values < 0.05 were considered statistically significant. Statistical analyses for clinical data were performed using R version 4.0 with reproducible programming in R and bioinformatic analyses were performed in R version 4.0 (R Core Team, Vienna, Austria)30 with reproducible programming in R programming language.

Results:Demographics:

Our cohort included 20 children, median age 11.5 (8.0, 14.2) years, 6 males and 14 females admitted to a tertiary pediatric hospital for an acute asthma exacerbation. Most subjects (14; 70%) identified as Caucasian. Data was compared with 14 healthy controls with a median age of 10.5 (7.0, 13.0) years, of which 9 were male and 8 (57%) identified as Caucasian. Subjects with acute asthma were more likely to have a history of exposure to smoking and a family history of asthma (p<0.05 for both) (Table 1). Median total IgE was significantly higher in subjects with asthma compared to controls (917 (274, 1396) IU/ml versus 31 (25, 144) IU/ml, p<0.05). Similarly, mean white blood cell count and neutrophil count was significantly higher in patients compared to controls, while median lymphocyte, monocyte, eosinophil, and basophil counts were significantly higher in controls compared to subjects with asthma (p<0.05 for all) (Table 1).

Among subjects with asthma, median age of onset of chronic asthma symptom was 2.5 (1.0, 4.2) years and median age of asthma diagnosis was 3.5 (2.0, 5.0) years. Sixteen (84%) subjects had moderate/severe asthma. All subjects with asthma received systemic steroids, with systemic steroids being started an average of 2.5 ± 1.6 days before the blood sample was drawn. Median ACT score at hospital admission was 16.0 (12.8, 17.2). Median number of hospital days for asthma exacerbation was 2.0 (2.0, 3.2). Eleven (55%) patients required supplemental oxygen. Nine patients required a pediatric intensive care unit (PICU) stay (45%) and among them 5 required CPAP/BiPAP support (Table 2). Viral PCR was done to differentiate between viral or non-viral associated exacerbations. Twelve (60%) had positive nasopharyngeal PCR for viral infection (11 Rhinovirus and 1 Respiratory syncytial virus, RSV) (Table 2) and 12 had a history of environmental allergies.

Cytokine analysis

Compared to controls, IL-1RA and IL-10 levels were significantly increased and TNFα significantly decreased in subjects with asthma (q<0.05 for all) (Table 1). Levels of GM-CSF, IFN-γ, IL-1β, IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, IL-17A, TGF-β1, and TGF-β2 were below limit of detection. Median CCL11, CCL17, CXCL10, IL-1α, IL-6, IL-8, IL-22, IL-23, MCP-1, TGF-β3 and TGF-β2 levels were not significantly different between controls and patients with acute asthma (Table 1). We did not observe significant effects of gender, race, severity of underlying asthma (mild/moderate compared to severe persistent asthma), severity of acute exacerbation, presence of virus, or atopy (total IgE levels) on cytokine levels (Supplement Tables (1–6). Those who were admitted to the PICU for acute asthma exacerbation had lower median CCL17 compared to healthy controls (22 (11, 28) versus 53 (47, 69) pg/ml, respectively, q<0.05).

RNA-seq analysis

To identify the differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in acute asthma, we performed RNA-sequencing analysis using peripheral blood samples from 19 children hospitalized for asthma and 14 healthy controls. Principal component analysis indicated clustering among control samples, while greater variation was observed among the samples from subjects with asthma (Figure 1A).

We observed that asthma and positive viral test were influencing factors within the first principal component (PC1) that accounted for ~63% of the variance in gene expression (Figure 1B and 1C). Meanwhile age, asthma severity, PICU, and corticosteroids taken did not impact gene expression (Figure 1). In differential expression analysis, we observed 852 genes were differentially expressed with 648 genes increased and 204 genes decreased in subjects with asthma compared to controls (Figure 2A and Supplementary File 1). Unbiased hierarchical clustering shows distinct gene expression patterns between patients with acute asthma and controls (Figure 2B). Pathway enrichment analysis on differentially expressed genes in subjects with asthma revealed pathways involved in innate and adaptive immunity. Core analysis in IPA generated 229 canonical pathways with p-values <0.05 (or >1.3 −log (p-value)) (Supplementary File 2). Results for enriched canonical pathways are listed in the pathway analysis supplementary file 2 and notable pathways are summarized in Table 3. Top significantly enriched pathways that were predicated to be up-regulated are related to innate immune responses including neutrophil degranulation and extracellular trap signaling, phagosome formation, IL-1 family and toll like receptor (TLR) signaling, and pyroptosis (Figure 2C and Table 3). Pathways predicted to be down-regulated included immunoregulatory interactions between lymphoid and non-lymphoid cells and costimulation by the CD28 family, which are related to T cells and adaptive immune responses. Within these enriched pathways were T cell receptor signaling and costimulatory genes (CD3E, CD28, CD40LG, PD-L1, CD247) (Figure 2C and Table 3).

Discussion:

In this study, we evaluated both cytokine levels and gene expression in blood to understand patterns of inflammation in children with asthma during hospitalization for acute exacerbation. We compared them with healthy non-asthmatic controls. We also looked at the impact of potential confounding factors such as severity of underlying asthma or severity of acute asthma exacerbation and presence of virus (viral vs. non-viral). Subjects with an acute asthma exacerbation exhibited an overall increase in white blood cell count that was composed of neutrophils, lymphocytes, and monocytes, while eosinophils were significantly reduced compared to healthy controls (likely a result of systemic corticosteroid treatment in the subjects with asthma).

Compared to healthy controls, subjects with an acute asthma exacerbation had significantly increased levels of IL-1RA and IL-10. These are both cytokines involved in regulation of the immune response. By binding to IL-1R1, IL-1 receptor antagonist (IL-1RA) inhibits signaling activation by both IL-1α and IL-1β31. IL-1RA can also decrease release of IL-6 and IL-8 in human bronchial epithelial cells32. IL-10 is another important cytokine known for its ability to antagonize and inhibit pro-inflammatory responses by stimulating IL-1RA production and inhibiting NFκB signaling33. In asthma, IL-10 secretion by regulatory T cells and type 2 innate lymphoid cells are an important mechanism to suppress type 2 inflammation33. These observations suggest IL-1RA and IL-10 may be important in dampening inflammation, however additional studies are required to understand their role in acute asthma exacerbation.

Studies examining inflammatory markers in acute asthma exacerbation in adults34,35 and in children36,37 have revealed variable inflammatory profiles with either increased eosinophils or neutrophils, together with increased Th1 or Th2 cytokines. In a recent study38 comparing mediators in serum from stable visits, pediatric and adult patients with acute asthma exacerbation on systemic steroids had increased levels (>1.5 fold) of serum IL-18, CXCL10, and CXCL11. Many Th2 cytokines (IL-4, IL-9, IL-17, IL-13, IL-33, TSLP) were below the limit of quantitation both when subjects had stable asthma and an exacerbation, which is consistent with our findings.

Currently, associations between gene expression patterns in PBMCs and immune phenotypes in asthmatic patients are being explored, but there is limited understanding of immune responses in acute asthma exacerbation especially in pediatric populations. Further, it is not known how acute asthma exacerbation is affected by asthma severity and viral infection. In our current study, whole blood RNA sequencing analysis revealed significant changes in gene expression in subjects with an acute asthma exacerbation compared to controls. Pathway enrichment analysis showed inflammatory pathways up-regulated in subjects with an acute asthma exacerbation. Several of these pathways had significantly increased genes that are involved in IL-1R, IL-18R, TLR, pyroptosis and inflammasome signaling pathways. These innate immune pathways, including the NLRP3 inflammasome, have important roles in asthma pathogenesis. The NLRP3 inflammasome complex includes caspase-1 and adaptor proteins to regulate IL-1β production and pyroptosis, a type of programmed cell death39. In allergic airway inflammation, NLRP3 is required for IL-1β production, eosinophil infiltration, and airway hyperresponsiveness40. As a caspase regulated process, pyroptosis mediates airway epithelial cell death41 and pro-inflammatory responses in lung macrophages42. Recently Wei et al.43, observed increased expression levels of genes involved in IL-1 signaling pathway (IL-1RN, IL-1RAP, IRAK3, and PELI1) in children with persistent asthma and those with acute asthma exacerbation. Despite evidence for IL-1 signaling in our gene expression data, we observed a lack of IL-1α and IL-1β protein levels in plasma which may be attributed to increased IL-1RA protein levels. The significance of IL-1 signaling pathway is well established in autoimmune diseases15. Whether the IL-1 signaling pathway is similarly important in asthma is less clear, although in a mouse model of asthma, the IL-1R antagonist (akin to IL-1RA), anakinra, has been shown to reduce allergic airway inflammation44. In humans, IL-1RA can reduce LPS-induced airway inflammation45 but to our knowledge has not been tested in acute asthma exacerbation.

We also observed a reduction in expression of genes and pathways involved in T cell adaptive immunity. Notable significantly reduced genes were enriched for pathways involved in T cell costimulation and differentiation (CD3E, CD40LG, TBX21, GATA3) and ICOS-ICOSL signaling in T helper cells. These findings are reflected in a lack of detection of Th effector cytokine levels (e.g. as IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, IL-17A) and decreased blood lymphocyte counts in subjects with an acute asthma exacerbation. While the suppression in T cell responses can be attributed to several mechanisms, we observed enrichment for PD1-PD-L1 signaling with significant increases in PD-L1 (CD274) gene expression. T cells express PD1 which is activated by PD-L1 co-stimulation by myeloid-lineage cells, inactivating T cell effector function and inducing apoptosis. As an important immune regulatory mechanism, recent studies have suggested that targeting PD1 can reprogram T cells and alleviate allergic airway inflammation in mice46. Yet it is unknown whether PD1-PDL1 signaling has a regulatory role in dampening T cell responses in acute asthma exacerbation.

Our blood cell count, cytokine, and RNA-seq data suggest that patients with acute asthma have increased systemic inflammation, which may be associated with local inflammation in the lung. Circulating blood eosinophil counts positively correlate with exhaled nitric oxide (FeNO), an established biomarker for type 2 airway inflammation47. Other systemic biomarkers, such as blood neutrophil to lymphocyte ratios, have been shown to correlate with sputum eosinophil counts and have the potential to predict acute exacerbations in subjects with severe asthma48,49. However, to our knowledge this has not been evaluated with whole blood RNA-seq analysis. Despite correlations between systemic and airway pro-inflammatory biomarkers, immune cell populations in the blood and airway are likely distinct in their composition and function due to differences in the environment. Thus, elevations in systemic inflammation may not fully reflect inflammatory processes taking place in the airway.

Despite respiratory viral infections being a common trigger of asthma exacerbations, our analysis showed there was little difference in inflammatory profiles in blood from patients with acute asthma who were either virus positive or virus negative. Additionally, we did not observe significant effects of gender, race, severity of underlying asthma, total IgE levels, severity of acute exacerbation or even presence or absence of viral infection on cytokine or gene expression levels. These findings suggest that the systemic inflammatory response in an acute asthma exacerbation is not affected by either the asthma severity (intermittent vs. persistent) or the presence of a positive test for a respiratory virus.

This study has several limitations including small sample size, the cross-sectional design, and longitudinal cohort studies with larger sample size are needed. Our study design did not include a stable asthma without acute exacerbation control group, limiting our ability to determine if results are attributed to asthma-related responses or hospitalization. Gene clustering analysis studies in adults with acute exacerbation show gene expression signatures that are distinct from subjects with stable asthma50, further highlighting the need to understand potential differences between acute exacerbation and stable asthma in children. Additionally, changes in gene expression do not always correlate with changes at the protein level. Caution is needed in generalizing our results, because validation analysis was not performed in a different cohort. Further, external validation is necessary in a larger cohort of asthmatics. We examined an extensive panel of mediators in plasma samples, which allowed us to explore both innate and adaptive immune responses. However, we acknowledge a need for more in-depth examination into immune cell populations and pathways. Additionally, more sensitive assays of inflammatory mediators are needed as we were not able to note high levels of T2 mediators either due to the assay sensitivity, pediatric age group, or because of systemic corticosteroid therapy.

Conclusions:

Our data show that pathways involved in innate immune responses remained increased in children hospitalized for an acute asthma exacerbation. Our study has revealed increased IL1-RA levels in children with acute asthma and upregulation of innate immune responses including IL-1 pathways and TLR signaling pathways while pathways associated with adaptive immune responses were downregulated. These pathways remained altered despite treatment with corticosteroids during the hospital stay. Further understanding of activation of innate immune responses in acute asthma may offer insight into how to identify biomarkers for acute asthma and optimize therapeutic interventions.

Key Message

Immune responses in children hospitalized for an acute asthma exacerbation remain largely unknown. This study identified increased expression of genes known to regulate innate immune responses, while genes that regulate adaptive immune responses were decreased in children with acute asthma exacerbation. These novel findings implicate innate immune pathways in acute asthma and may be a therapeutic target in managing acute asthma exacerbation in children.
