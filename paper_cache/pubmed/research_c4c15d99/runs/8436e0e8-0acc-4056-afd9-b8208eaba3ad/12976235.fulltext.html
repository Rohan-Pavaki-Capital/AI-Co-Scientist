Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and cancer are among the most prevalent age-related diseases. Despite previous research into their potential relationship, the nature of their association remains poorly understood. This study aims to examine the clinical characteristics of AD and various cancers using data from the Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) database, investigate the causal relationship between AD and cancers through Mendelian randomization (MR) analysis, and identify potential shared underlying mechanisms through transcriptomic profiling.

Methods

Clinical data from AD patients were retrieved from the Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) database, and survival analysis was conducted using Kaplan-Meier curves. For the two-sample Mendelian randomization (MR) analysis, data were obtained from genome-wide association study (GWAS) databases. Multiple MR approaches, including inverse-variance weighted, MR-Egger, and weighted median methods, were applied, along with assessments of heterogeneity and sensitivity to ensure the robustness and reliability of the results. Transcriptomic data for AD, colorectal cancer (CRC), and breast cancer (BC) were downloaded from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified through differential expression analysis, followed by functional enrichment analysis using Gene Ontology (GO) and the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway analysis.

Results

A total of 42,768 cancer patients who died from AD were included from the SEER database. Survival analysis revealed a more favorable prognosis (p < 0.01) in patients younger than 65 years. Asian or Pacific Islander patients exhibited better survival outcomes compared with White patients. Regarding tumor sites, patients with uterine corpus cancer had the best prognosis, while lung cancer patients had the poorest outcomes. Patients who received surgery, radiotherapy, or chemotherapy showed significantly improved survival compared to those who received no cancer treatment. Higher household income and being married were also associated with better prognosis, although no significant difference was observed by gender. MR analysis demonstrated a significant positive causal relationship between AD and CRC, and a weak inverse relationship between AD and BC, suggesting that increased genetic susceptibility to AD is associated with elevated CRC risk and reduced BC risk. Intersection analysis of DEGs revealed that shared DEGs between AD and BC were enriched in GO terms related to amino acid transport regulation, organic acid transport regulation, positive regulation of vesicle docking, and myo-inositol transmembrane import. Shared DEGs between AD and CRC were enriched in presynaptic actin cytoskeleton organization, proteasome ubiquitin-independent protein catabolic process, negative regulation of cellular amide metabolic process, and adenylate cyclase binding. KEGG enrichment analysis indicated that AD and BC may share the synaptic vesicle cycle pathway.

Conclusion

Our study reveals significant subgroup heterogeneity among cancer patients who died from AD. MR analysis demonstrates that AD increases the risk of CRC while showing weak evidence for a decreased risk of BC. These associations may be mediated by mechanisms involving amino acid transport regulation, myo-inositol transmembrane import, and synaptic vesicle cycling. These findings offer new perspectives on the AD-cancer relationship and may guide future investigations into shared mechanisms.

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is one of the most common diseases in the elderly and the leading cause of dementia.AD is a neurodegenerative disorder characterized by neuronal and synaptic loss, cerebral atrophy, and eventual mortality. In recent years, the incidence of AD has continued to rise. Alzheimer’s Disease International estimated the global prevalence of dementia at approximately 50 million people, projected to triple by 2050, with the majority residing in low- and middle-income countries [1], imposing a significant economic burden.BC is the most prevalent malignancy among women worldwide, and CRC ranks as the third most common cancer and the second leading cause of cancer-related mortality.

AD, BC, and CRC are all age-related diseases; however, their interrelationships remain incompletely understood. Some studies suggest an inverse causal relationship between AD and cancer. For instance, ZeHua Dong et al. found that AD may reduce cancer risk, with very low-density lipoprotein(VLDL) serving as a critical intermediate variable linking AD and cancer [2]. A meta-analysis revealed a weak inverse association between AD and cancer that confounders, diagnostic bias, or competing risk bias could not explain [3]. At the cellular level, AD is associated with increased cell mortality, whereas cancer cells evade apoptosis to resist cell death [4, 5]. Furthermore, genes involved in cell proliferation and apoptosis, such as ARPC4, VLDLR, DAB1, and PAR-4, contribute to AD progression through diverse mechanisms but exhibit protective roles in cancer [6–8]. Paradoxically, AD and cancer share certain features: microRNAs (miRNAs), endogenous small non-coding RNAs, regulate miRNA-target networks that critically link cancer and neurodegenerative disease [9, 10]. Additionally, hallmarks of cancer—including inflammatory responses, evasion of growth suppressors, replicative immortality, angiogenesis, and genomic instability—are also implicated in AD pathogenesis [11]. These findings highlight the complexity of molecular pathways underlying AD and cancer, yet existing evidence remains insufficient to fully elucidate their precise relationship.

MR, a method employing genetic variants as instrumental variables to infer causal relationships between exposures and outcomes, enables precise evaluation of potential causality [12, 13]. In this study, we first analyzed clinical characteristics of AD patients from the SEER database. To mitigate confounding effects, we applied MR to investigate causal relationships between AD and cancers, including CRC, BC, prostate cancer, and lung cancer. Finally, transcriptomic data from the GEO database were utilized to identify differentially expressed genes (DEGs) and explore shared molecular mechanisms between AD and CRC/BC.

MethodsWorkflow

The workflow is illustrated in Fig. 1.

Overview of study design

Clinical feature analysisData source

Data for Alzheimer’s disease (AD) patients were obtained from the Clinical records of cancer patients who died from Alzheimer’s disease (AD) between 2000 and 2021 were extracted from the Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) database. Extracted variables included age, sex, race, tumor site, surgical status, radiotherapy status, chemotherapy status, median household income, and marital status. Inclusion was restricted to patients whose cancer type ranked among the top 10 most prevalent cancers within the cohort of patients who died from AD. Patients with incomplete clinical information were excluded, yielding a final analytical cohort of 42,768 patients.

Statistical analysis

Pie charts were used to illustrate the proportion of cancer types in this population. Intergroup comparisons were performed using chi-square tests. Kaplan-Meier survival curves evaluated survival outcomes across patient subgroups, with statistical significance defined as P < 0.05.

Mendelian randomization (MR) analysisData source

The MR analysis was designed to satisfy three core assumptions: (1) genetic variants are strongly associated with the exposure (AD); (2) genetic variants are independent of confounders affecting the exposure-outcome relationship; and (3) genetic variants influence the outcome solely through the exposure.

All GWAS summary data utilized in the present MR analysis were sourced from the IEU OpenGWAS database. These datasets underwent standardized processing by the platform and were uniformly subjected to a minimal covariate adjustment scheme, thereby effectively mitigating bias arising from inconsistent adjustments across studies. Furthermore, we avoided adjusting for any postoperative or disease progression-related variables, which circumvented the risk of collider bias. GWAS data for AD (ebi-a-GCST005921, exposure) included 71,880 cases and 383,378 controls (7,746,640 SNPs). For outcomes, BC GWAS data (ukb-b-13584) comprised 35,102 cases and 388,356 controls (9,851,867 SNPs), and CRC GWAS data (ieu-b-4965) included 5,657 cases and 372,016 controls (11,738,639 SNPs). Identifiers for other cancer GWAS (prostate, bladder, melanoma, lung, uterine, renal, non-Hodgkin lymphoma) are listed in Supplementary Table 1.

Statistical analysis

SNPs strongly associated with AD (P < 5 × 10⁻⁸, genome-wide significance threshold) were selected. Linkage disequilibrium (LD) was removed via clumping (LD threshold: r² < 0.001, clump distance = 10,000 kb). To ensure that our genetic instruments were robust and to avoid bias from weak instruments, we calculated the F-statistic for each SNP using the formula: F = (beta/se)^2. The F-statistics for the nine independent SNPs used as instruments for AD ranged from 30.09 to 3905.77,which confirms that our genetic instruments were strongly associated with the exposure (AD). Five MR methods were applied: inverse variance weighted (IVW), MR-Egger, weighted median, simple mode, and weighted mode for robust analysis of causality. Sensitivity analyses included Cochran’s Q test for heterogeneity, funnel plots, and leave-one-out analysis. To account for multiple testing across the nine cancer outcomes, a False Discovery Rate (FDR) correction was applied. A causal effect was deemed significant if the FDR-corrected P-value was < 0.05 in at least one of the five MR methods.

Analyses were conducted using R (v4.4.2) with the TwoSampleMR package (v0.6.9).

Transcriptomic analysisData source

Microarray expression datasets for AD (GSE132903: 97 AD cases, 98 controls), BC (GSE45827: 35 tumor tissues, 24 normal tissues), and CRC (GSE23878: 130 tumor tissues, 25 normal tissues) were downloaded from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database.

Principal component analysis (PCA) and DEG identification

Each dataset was normalized independently using the normalizeBetweenArrays function from the R limma package with the “quantile” method. PCA was performed to visualize sample distribution. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified for AD, BC, and CRC using thresholds of |log2(Fold Change)| >1 and an adjusted P-value < 0.01.

Functional enrichment analysis

Overlapping DEGs between AD-BC and AD-CRC were identified using Venn diagrams. Biological functions and pathways associated with these overlapping genes were annotated through Gene Ontology (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) enrichment analyses.

All transcriptomic analyses were implemented in R (v4.4.2).

ResultsBaseline data

Among 42,768 patients, 31,022 underwent surgery (72.53%) and 11,746 did not (27.47%) (Table 1). The surgical group had a lower proportion of individuals aged ≥ 65 years (93.93% vs. 96.11%, P < 0.001) and a higher proportion of females (66.93% vs. 22.89%, P < 0.001). Racial distribution differed significantly (P < 0.001), with Whites comprising 90.79% in the surgical group versus 86.11% in the non-surgical group. Fewer surgical patients received radiotherapy (16.88% vs. 40%, P < 0.001), while chemotherapy was more common in the surgical group (10.19% vs. 7.28%, P < 0.001).Cancer site distribution varied markedly: breast cancer predominated in the surgical group (34.7% vs. 7.59%), whereas prostate cancer was more frequent in the non-surgical group (68.06% vs. 8.79%). Pie charts identified the top three tumor sites as breast cancer (27.3%), prostate cancer (25.1%), and colorectal cancer (15.5%) (Supplementary Fig. 1).

Survival analysis

A better prognosis was associated with age < 65 years, surgical intervention, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, median household income >$70,000, and married status (P < 0.001). Mendelian randomization analysisMR analysis revealed causal relationships between AD and CRC/BC

Using SNPs as instrumental variables in a two-sample MR framework with AD as the exposure, a significant positive causal relationship was observed between genetic susceptibility to AD and increased risk of CRC (PMR Egger = 0.0763, PWeighted median = 0.0027, PIVW = 0.0003, PSimple mode = 0.6718, PWeighted mode = 0.0230; 2). This causal effect remained statistically significant after FDR correction (FDR.PIVW = 0.0015). The scatterplot visually supported an upward trend in CRC risk with increasing genetic predisposition to AD (Fig. 3). Cochran’s Q test (P > 0.05) indicated no heterogeneity among the SNPs, and the MR-Egger intercept test (P > 0.05) confirmed the absence of significant horizontal pleiotropy. Funnel plot symmetry suggested minimal potential bias, and leave-one-out sensitivity analysis demonstrated that the causal estimate remained consistent after sequentially excluding each SNP, confirming the robustness of the finding.

Discussion

In this study, we systematically investigated the relationship between Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and cancers by integrating data from the SEER database, Mendelian randomization (MR), and transcriptomic analyses for the first time. Using a large-scale SEER dataset, we analyzed cancer patients who died from AD. Kaplan-Meier survival curves demonstrated that age < 65 years, surgical intervention, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, median household income >$70,000, and married status were associated with improved prognosis (P < 0.001). Our MR analysis revealed a robust positive causal effect of AD on CRC risk, which remained significant after strict multiple testing correction. In contrast, evidence for a causal effect of AD on BC risk was attenuated after multiple testing correction. Specifically, in the AD–breast cancer analysis, only the weighted median method suggested a significant association, whereas IVW and MR-Egger did not show significant causal effects. These observations suggest that the causal relationship between AD and BC remains hypothetical and requires further validation.

Additionally, no significant causal relationships were observed between AD and other cancers (e.g., prostate, lung, or bladder cancers). Furthermore, when cancers were treated as exposures and AD as the outcome, no significant causal effects were detected. To explore potential shared mechanisms, GO and KEGG enrichment analyses indicated that AD–CRC interactions may involve presynaptic actin cytoskeleton organization, proteasome ubiquitin-independent protein catabolism, negative regulation of cellular amide metabolism, and adenylate cyclase binding. Conversely, AD–BC associations may relate to the regulation of amino acid transport, organic acid transport, positive regulation of vesicle docking, myo-inositol transmembrane import, and synaptic vesicle cycling.

Although epidemiological studies suggest that two-thirds of AD patients are female—correlating with aging and the APOE4 genotype [14]—our analysis found no significant sex-based prognostic difference. Cancer treatment significantly influenced AD prognosis: surgical patients exhibited better outcomes [15], and chemotherapy was associated with reduced AD risk [16], consistent with our findings. Unmarried or partnerless AD patients showed poorer prognosis, potentially linked to elevated plasma biomarkers such as amyloid-β40 and tau protein [17].

The causal relationship between Alzheimer’s disease and cancer has long been a topic of intense research, with previous studies predominantly suggesting an inverse association. For example, a retrospective study from Ruijin Hospital (Shanghai) reported an 18% reduction in overall cancer risk among AD patients, including lung, prostate, and testicular cancers [18]. A recent review by Mikyoung Park highlighted that cancer involves dysregulated cell proliferation, whereas AD is linked to excessive neuronal loss, potentially mediated by shared molecular mechanisms such as p53, cyclin D, cyclin E, and protein phosphatase 2 A [5]. Additionally, a retrospective study of over 1 million Italian residents demonstrated that cancer and AD dementia incidence both increase exponentially with age but inversely correlate—elderly cancer patients exhibit reduced AD dementia risk and vice versa [19]. These findings collectively indicate a strong, predominantly inverse association between AD and cancers. In our study, the observed inverse causality between AD and BC aligns with prior evidence. However, the positive association between AD and CRC—suggesting AD may increase CRC risk—contrasts with earlier reports. This discrepancy may arise from confounding factors in previous studies, such as underdiagnosis of AD in cancer survivors with severe comorbidities [20] or reduced cancer screening efficacy (e.g., mammography, colonoscopy) in cognitively impaired populations [21].

Shared mechanisms between AD and CRC may involve inflammatory pathways [22]. Amyloid-β (Aβ) deposition and neurofibrillary tangles promote chronic neuroinflammation via activated microglia releasing pro-inflammatory cytokines, driving AD pathogenesis [23, 24]. Similarly, chronic inflammation mediated by immune cells is a hallmark of tumorigenesis [11, 25]. The gut-brain axis further supports this link: gut microbiota alterations correlate directly or indirectly with amyloidosis [26]. For example, Escherichia coli endotoxins enhance Aβ aggregation in vitro [27], while gut microbial taxa such as Actinobacteria, Coriobacteriaceae, and Betaproteobacteria are associated with increased AD risk [28]. The complex association between Alzheimer’s disease and cancer may also be attributed to the regulatory effects of non-coding RNAs. Dysregulation of specific ncRNAs, including snoRNAs and snRNAs, may disrupt mitochondrial homeostasis, thereby exacerbating neurodegeneration in AD [29]. Simultaneously, these regulatory abnormalities can modulate the tumor microenvironment through the neuro-immune-cancer axis. A recent study identified three immune-related differentially expressed genes—specifically CCL4, IL17C, and TEK—that can recruit immune cells to the brain, thereby promoting a neuroinflammatory environment. These infiltrating cells secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines that exacerbate neuronal damage and compromise blood-brain barrier integrity. This persistent neuroinflammatory state may systematically influence the peripheral tumor microenvironment, ultimately suppressing anti-tumor immunity [30].

We further attempted to bridge the MR results with transcriptomic findings by exploring whether the instrumental variable SNPs for AD were in linkage disequilibrium with DEGs in AD and CRC/BC. However, we did not identify any significant AD-associated SNP that also functioned as a significant eQTL for these DEGs in major brain tissues. This suggests that DEG expression changes may not be directly driven by top AD genetic risk variants, but are more likely downstream effects of the AD pathological process or linked to cancer risk through other indirect mechanisms such as epigenetic regulation.

While acknowledging the limitation of using different tissues, the purpose of our cross-tissue transcriptomic analysis was not to directly compare expression levels between organs, but to identify shared systemic molecular perturbations and pathways that might underlie the genetic correlation between AD and cancers observed in our MR analysis. For instance, our study suggests that multiple cellular transport proteins—including amino acid transporters, ion transporters, and transmembrane transporters of pentoses and fucose—may mediate interactions between AD and cancers. Notably, the expression direction of the overlapping gene ENC1 aligns coherently with our MR results: ENC1 was downregulated in AD brain tissue but upregulated in both colorectal and breast cancers. This inverse expression pattern is consistent with the opposing causal effects identified by MR: genetic predisposition to AD (potentially mediated by low ENC1) increases CRC risk, whereas it may weakly decrease BC risk. Prior studies also link ENC1 to cognitive decline [31] and overexpression in multiple cancers (breast, colorectal, lung, cervical) [32–36]. For instance, ENC1 facilitates colorectal carcinoma tumorigenesis and metastasis via the JAK2/STAT5/AKT axis-mediated epithelial-mesenchymal transition and stemness [37]. Moreover, ENC1 is significantly upregulated in breast cancer, and its expression is associated with sensitivity to radiation therapy [32]. Additionally, ENC1 has recently been emphasized as a metastasis-related biomarker, a suitable prognostic marker, and an attractive therapeutic target in breast cancer [38]. This positions ENC1 as a compelling molecular hub, where its dysregulation in opposite directions in the brain and peripheral tissues may underpin the inverse relationship between AD and cancer. The SLC5A3 gene (sodium/myoinositol cotransporter), upregulated in both AD and BC, indicates shared involvement of actin cytoskeletal processes. Recent AD susceptibility genes such as CASS4 participate in actin dynamics, while SLC24A4 (encoding a solute carrier family sodium/potassium/calcium exchanger) may influence AD pathogenesis via tau-mediated pathways [39]. SLC5A3 has also been implicated in cervical cancer and non-small cell lung cancer progression [40, 41]. RGS4, a regulator of G-protein signaling, modulates synaptic transmission, neurogenesis, and cell growth by inhibiting adenylate cyclase (AC) and phospholipase C activity [42]. Paradoxically, RGS4 promotes BC invasion and metastasis by blocking G-coupled receptor signaling [43]. Our data show RGS4 downregulation in AD but upregulation in BC.

Synaptic vesicle cycling may represent another shared regulatory mechanism. Synaptic vesicles mediate activity-dependent neurotransmission through fusion, retrieval, and recycling [44]. In AD, synaptic dysfunction—an early neuropathological event—correlates with neurodegeneration and synaptic loss [45, 46]. Recent work identified four persistently downregulated synaptic vesicle cycling genes in AD [47]. In cancer, synaptotagmin-13 (SYT13) promotes BC progression via FAK/AKT signaling [48], while synaptotagmin knockdown suppresses CRC cell growth [49].

Our study has several limitations. First, the patient data extracted from the SEER database consisted solely of cancer patients who died from Alzheimer’s disease, lacking detailed individual-level information, which complicated prognostic analysis. Second, the Mendelian randomization analysis was conducted exclusively in European populations, potentially limiting generalizability to other ethnic groups such as Asian or African cohorts regarding AD–cancer relationships. Third, while functional enrichment analysis was employed to explore potential mechanisms between AD and CRC/BC, no further experimental validation or exploration of the risk genes linking these diseases was performed. Finally, the transcriptomic analysis utilized datasets with limited sample sizes, particularly for AD, resulting in a relatively small number of identified DEGs. This may affect the robustness of the overlapping genes and functional enrichment results. Therefore, the mechanistic insights derived from the overlapping DEGs should be regarded as preliminary and require validation in larger, well-powered studies.

Conclusion

Our study reveals significant subgroup heterogeneity among cancer patients who died from AD. MR analysis demonstrates that AD increases the risk of CRC while showing weak evidence for a decreased risk of BC. These associations may be mediated by mechanisms involving amino acid transport regulation, myo-inositol transmembrane import, and synaptic vesicle cycling. These findings offer new perspectives on the AD-cancer relationship and may guide future investigations into shared mechanisms.
