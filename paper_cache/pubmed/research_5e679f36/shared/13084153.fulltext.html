Chronic kidney disease (CKD) affects ∼15% of US adults and over 840 million people worldwide. Environmental contaminants, including pesticides and metals, are increasingly recognized as disease contributors, yet mechanisms and consequences of long-term, low-level mixture exposures remain poorly defined. Our prior work identified glyphosate and metals (cadmium, arsenic, lead, and vanadium) in drinking water from agricultural regions with high CKD prevalence and showed that early-life co-exposures disrupt kidney development. Here, using adult zebrafish as a mechanistic model, we tested whether chronic, low-level exposure to glyphosate, metals, and their combination impairs kidney function and structure. We exposed zebrafish for 10 and 60 days to glyphosate (10 ppb), metals (2 ppb Cd, 4 ppb As, 5 ppb Pb, 15 V), or glyphosate + metals and evaluated low-molecular-weight proteinuria, histopathology, metabolomics, mitochondrial function, mitochondrial copy number, and mitophagy in the kidney. Chronic exposure to glyphosate and metals produced distinct yet overlapping kidney toxicity signatures, including tubular injury, altered metabolism, and impaired mitochondrial function. Co-exposures generated the most severe effects, with mitochondrial beta oxidation, respiration, and mitophagy as sensitive targets. These findings demonstrate that glyphosate and metals at levels found in drinking water damage kidney function over time, with co-exposure worsening outcomes compared with individual chemicals. Our study identifies mitochondria-rich proximal tubules as critical targets of chronic glyphosate–metal exposure, providing mechanistic insight into how environmental contaminants contribute to CKD risk. This work advances understanding of disease etiology in environmental nephropathies and highlights environmental factors as important drivers of kidney health.

The trajectory of kidney health depends on the preservation of the ∼1 million nephrons established at birth, as nephrons possess limited cellular repair capacity. Although some damage accumulates with age, exogenous injuries can accelerate these deleterious changes, increasing the likelihood of developing chronic kidney disease (CKD) (Hommos et al. 2017). CKD entails progressive, irreversible loss of renal function, eventually leading to end-stage renal disease. CKD burdens ∼15% of US adults, over 840 million people globally, and disease incidence continues to rise (Francis et al. 2024).

Exogenous factors, such as exposure to environmental toxins, may affect the trajectory of kidney health through life and contribute to the rising prevalence of kidney diseases (Vervaet et al. 2017; Lash 2019; Sanders and Jayasundara 2023; Francis et al. 2024). Nephrotoxic environmental contaminants filter through the kidney over the lifetime, and the damaging effects may begin long before chronic disease develops (Barnett and Cummings 2018). For example, there is evidence of ongoing renal injury in children who live in areas where environmentally driven CKD affects adults (Gunasekara et al. 2022; Strasma et al. 2025). To address the risk of environmental contaminants for kidney health, it is essential to understand what subcellular processes are targeted and how chronic dysfunction of those processes diminishes renal function over time.

The mitochondria-rich proximal tubule is a sensitive target of environmental toxins. As the primary site of transporter-mediated resorption and endocytosis, the proximal tubule experiences a high energetic demand and is densely packed with mitochondria. Renal tissue is surpassed in mitochondrial content and energy consumption only by the heart (Bhargava and Schnellmann 2017). Furthermore, extensive uptake processes facilitate the intracellular accumulation of even low levels of contaminants over time (Barbier et al. 2005). Nephrotoxicants that adversely affect mitochondrial function, including pesticides and metals, may pose particular risks to proximal tubule health (Barnett and Cummings 2018; Lash 2019).

Our previous analyses of drinking water collected from populations disproportionately impacted by increasing CKD prevalence indicated that the herbicide glyphosate and a mixture of the metals cadmium (Cd), arsenic (As), lead (Pb), and vanadium (V) may pose a risk to kidney health (Babich et al. 2020; Ulrich et al. 2023). Glyphosate, the active ingredient in RoundUp, is one of the most widely used pesticides in the world (Benbrook 2016). Evidence of glyphosate toxicity and widespread human exposure continues to emerge, including a recent study indicating an association between glyphosate exposure and poor kidney health outcomes in US populations (Gillezeau et al. 2019; Costas-Ferreira et al. 2022; Grau et al. 2022; Ulrich et al. 2023; Hu et al. 2025). The metals Cd, As, Pb, and V are known mitochondrial and nephrotoxicants (Rehder 2013; Treviño et al. 2019; Kshirsagar et al. 2022; Upamalika et al. 2022; Turkington et al. 2025). Contamination of drinking water with glyphosate and these nephrotoxic metals is pervasive in agricultural areas in the United States and globally and may represent a risk to kidney health (Lash 2019; Balali-Mood et al. 2021; Kshirsagar et al. 2022; Price et al. 2025). Cd, As, and Pb are quantifiable in the urine of 83.8%, 99%, and 94.9% of women aged 15 to 44 years in the United States (NHANES 2003 to 2016), whereas glyphosate has been detected in 70% to 99% of general populations in the United States, Canada, and Europe (Gillezeau et al. 2019; Sanders et al. 2019; Watson et al. 2020; Grau et al. 2022). These widespread exposures underscore the public health relevance of understanding their nephrotoxic effects.

Glyphosate can interact with metals to form glyphosate–metal complexes that increase glyphosate’s half-life from 90 days to 7 years in water and 47 days to 22 years in soil (Mertens et al. 2018). Environmental and biological persistence increases the risk of human exposure and adverse health outcomes to these pollutants. Previously, we detected biological interactions between glyphosate and a metal mixture during early development, where co-exposure to glyphosate + metals targeted distinct developmental processes compared with the individual exposures (Babich et al. 2024). Although we showed that co-exposure preferentially targets kidney development, we did not resolve whether long-term adult exposure to glyphosate–metal interaction affects tissue partitioning of these toxicants or broader consequences for kidney health.

Building on our previous developmental studies and environmental exposure analyses that linked glyphosate and metal mixture exposure to adverse kidney health outcomes, we tested the hypothesis that long-term, low-level exposure to glyphosate, metals, and their combination impair kidney function and structure and that co-exposure increases adverse kidney effects. We leveraged the adult zebrafish (Danio rerio) as a higher-throughput mechanistic model organism to enable chronic exposure studies and maintain the biological fidelity of a whole organism system. The zebrafish kidney shares key nephron functions with mammalian kidneys, with conserved molecular pathways and cellular responses to toxicants that enable translation of findings across species (Nishimura et al. 2015; Poureetezadi and Wingert 2016; Foreman 2019; Ding et al. 2021; Vorhees et al. 2021). We evaluated zebrafish kidney toxicity at 10 and 60 days following exposures to glyphosate (10 ppb; well below the 700 ppb drinking water threshold), a metal mixture (CdCl2 [2 ppb], AsNaO2 [4 ppb], PbCl2 [5 ppb], and V2O5 [15 ppb]) at or below regulatory limits, and the combined glyphosate + metal mixture. We quantified low-molecular-weight (LMW) proteinuria, histopathological changes, metabolomics changes, as well as kidney-specific mitochondrial function, copy number, and mitophagy. We further tested the hypothesis that mitochondria are a sensitive target of exposure to metals and glyphosate that may contribute to proximal tubular dysfunction. Ultimately, our goal is to reveal the subcellular processes targeted by ubiquitous environmental contaminants, such as low levels of glyphosate and metals, and their impact on the development of renal dysfunction over time.

Materials and methodsAnimal husbandry

A colony of Ekkwill zebrafish (Ekkwill Waterlife Resources, Ruskin, Florida) and transgenic lines is maintained at Duke University in recirculating AHAB systems (Pentair Aquatic Ecosystems, Apopka, Florida) at 28 °C with a 14:10 h light:dark cycle and a pH of 7.2 to 7.4. Colony fish were fed twice per day, with live Artemia nauplii (90% GSL strain; Reed Mariculture, Campbell, CA) and Zeigler’s Adult Zebrafish diet (Pentair Aquatic Ecosystems). For the chronic exposure experiments, adult (8 to 14 mo.) zebrafish were transferred to custom-built standalone recirculating aquarium systems. Fish were randomly assigned to 1 of 4 independent systems, each corresponding to a treatment group. Each system contained either clean zebrafish water (60 μg/l ASW, pH = 7.2 to 7.4), glyphosate (10 ppb Glyphosate, Sigma-Aldrich analytical standard, Germany), the metal mixture (2 ppb CdCl2, 4 ppb NaAsO2, 5 ppb PbCl2, 15 ppb V2O5, Sigma-Aldrich), or a co-exposure to glyphosate + metals (10 ppb glyphosate, 2 ppb CdCl2, 4 ppb NaAsO2, 5 ppb PbCl2, 15 ppb V2O5). All systems were maintained at 28 °C, with a 14:10 h light:dark cycle, without mechanical or biological filtration, and zebrafish were fed daily according to the colony regimen. Each week, a 33% exposure system water was removed and replaced with clean water containing fresh exposure solution at the nominal dose to prevent accumulation of biological waste. Temperature, pH, nitrate, nitrite, total hardness, and carbonate hardness were monitored twice weekly (API Freshwater Master Test Kit and API 5-in-1 Test Strips, API Fishcare, Chalfont, PA). Additional details regarding exposure system reagents and water quality parameters can be found in Table S1. All experiments using transgenic zebrafish lines with fluorescent reporters were performed in hemizygous animals. All zebrafish care and protocols were approved by and conducted in accordance with Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) at Duke University (#A069-22-04).

Quantification of metal burden in tissues

Adult Ekkwill zebrafish (n = 3 to 4 each sex from a single tank) were exposed to either the metal mixture or glyphosate + metal mixture for 60 days as previously described and euthanized by rapid cooling. The whole kidney, liver, and remaining carcass were dissected, weighed, and flash-frozen. Tissues were digested in 100% trace metal-grade nitric acid, and samples were analyzed for metal contaminants (Li, Be, Al, V, Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, As, Se, Rb, Sr, Mo, Ag, Cd, Sn, Sb, Cs, Ba, W, Hg, Tl, Pb, and U) by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) using an Agilent 8900 triple quadrupole mass spectrometer (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA) in helium and oxygen modes. The ICP-MS was calibrated using NIST-traceable standards, and calibration was verified using second source standards after the calibration standard and every 10 samples.

Only those metals that were quantified above the minimum detection limit in more than 80% of samples were included for further analysis, which resulted in the exclusion of Ag, Al, Be, Cr, Ni, Tl, and U. Concentration values were normalized to total tissue weight. To determine the distribution of each metal across the kidney, liver, and carcass compartments, the ratio of tissue concentration to total body burden was computed. Kruskal–Wallis tests were performed in R (version 4.4.2) to evaluate whether the sex of the fish significantly impacted metal distribution across tissues. Then, to assess whether the dosed metals (As, Cd, Pb, V) accumulate in the fish relative to their abundance in the exposure water, the total body burden of each metal in the fish was compared with the exposure water. Finally, to evaluate the impact of glyphosate on metal burden, the concentration of each metal was compared between the 2 exposure groups using a Kruskal–Wallis test for each tissue.

LMW proteinuria assay

Proximal tubule function was assayed using the zebrafish transgenic line Tg(fabp10a: nanoLuc-RAPD3), abbreviated Tg(NL-D3), constitutively expresses nanoluciferase fused to the high-affinity megalin-binding domain of Receptor Associated Protein (total 35.5 kDa) (Naylor et al. 2022). The concentration of NL-D3 lost to the urine can be quantified as a measure of LMW proteinuria. Three cohorts of adult Tg(NL-D3) fish were exposed for 60 days in groups of 2 female and 2 male fish in each tank to facilitate recognition of individual fish (total n = 6 for each sex). Every 10 days (i.e. 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, and 60 days of exposure), fish were transferred into individual beakers containing 100 ml of clean, control housing water and held overnight at 26 °C. Prime water conditioner (25 μl/l, Seachem Laboratories, Madison, Georgia) was added to prevent ammonia toxicity from overnight accumulation. After 16 h, beaker contents were stirred, and three 80 μl samples of overnight water were collected and placed into wells of a 384-well plate (Greiner Bio-one, Monroe, North Carolina). Luminescence of these samples and 6 blanks (clean, control housing water) was measured using the Nano-Glo Luciferase Assay Kit (Promega, Fitchburg, Wisconsin) according to manufacturer instructions. Raw luminescence values were blank-subtracted for each assay and statistically compared using a Kruskal–Wallis test with Dunn’s post-hoc test. There was no statistically significant exposure tank effect on luminescence within each timepoint, so data from the 3 independent exposure cohorts were pooled for statistical comparison.

Histopathology

After 10 and 60 days of exposure, adult Ekkwill zebrafish (n = 4 to 6 each sex across 3 exposure cohorts for each timepoint) were euthanized, and the entire dorsal abdomen was excised to ensure that the kidney remained undamaged and in its original position. Immediately upon excision, this portion was submerged in >10 times the volume of 10% neutral buffered formalin (VWR International, Radnor, Pennsylvania) and fixed overnight on an orbital shaker at room temperature. Fixed specimens were decalcified using methods detailed in Copper et al. (2017). Briefly, they were first rinsed twice with 20× specimen volume of 1× PBS followed by immersion in 20× volume of 0.35 M, pH 7.8 EDTA (Fisher Scientific, Hampton, NH) for 7 days. Specimens were rinsed twice with 20× volume of 70% ethanol (EtOH) and then incubated overnight in 70% EtOH at 23 °C. Specimens from the same treatment group were pre-embedded together in 1% agarose to facilitate similar depth and orientation during sectioning. They were then dehydrated by passage through a graded EtOH series, cleared with xylene (VWR International), and embedded in paraffin (Electron Microscopy Sciences, Hatfield, Pennsylvania). Serial frontal sections were cut using a Microm HM355S rotary microtome (Microm GmbH, Walldorf, Germany) at 5 µm thickness and mounted on glass histology slides (Epredia, Kalamazoo, Michigan). Slides were then routinely stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) and coverslipped.

Slides were examined with a Nikon Eclipse E600 compound light microscope (Nikon Instruments Inc., Melville, New York). A qualitative evaluation was made of each section, comparing morphology to control fish and published images (Menke et al. 2011; Wolf et al. 2015). All slides were examined for content and quality of kidney sections by an independent experimenter to maintain the blindness of the primary experimenter. An expert qualitative assessment of all slides was performed to identify pathological features and potential artifacts in tissues to enable subsequent quantitative analysis of identified features. Detailed findings from the qualitative analysis can be found in the online supplementary material, Appendix S1.

A subset of 5 to 6 nonconsecutive slides, each containing 4 to 7 consecutive sections, were scanned for each group at 40× magnification by the Duke Biorepository & Precision Pathology Center using an Aperio GT450 Scanner (Leica Biosystems, Deerfield, Illinois). To quantify the abundance of proximal tubule alterations and neonephrogenesis, total kidney area and the identified pathological features were manually outlined using QuPath Software (Version 0.5) (Bankhead et al. 2017). Each slide was presented with the sample identity masked, in a randomized order, twice in the image set for duplicate annotation. To compile the quantitative analysis, the total area of kidney tissue and the area of each feature type were computed for each fish on each slide, then the average fractional area was taken for that individual based on measurements from 5 to 6 nonconsecutive slides. Statistical comparison of the percentage abundance of each feature was performed using a Kruskal–Wallis test with Dunn’s post-hoc test. Because the exposure tank did not significantly affect the area of any measured feature within each timepoint, data from 3 independent exposure cohorts were pooled for subsequent analyses.

Oil Red O stain

Adult Ekkwill zebrafish (n = 3 to 4 per sex) were exposed to each treatment for 10 and 60 days across 3 exposure cohorts for each timepoint. Kidney tissue was dissected and transferred into Optimal Cutting Temperature Cryosectioning Medium (Sakura Finetek, Torrance, California) and then flash-frozen. Cryosections of 6 μm were prepared, mounted, and then stained with Oil Red O and hematoxylin by the University of Pittsburgh Histology Core Facility, using the Oil Red O stain procedure developed by Newcomer Supply Laboratory. Qualitative evaluation was performed to evaluate the presence, distribution, and relative intensity of neutral lipid staining within proximal tubule epithelial cells. This examination was performed with a Nikon E600 compound light microscope, and 40× representative images were captured using a Nikon DMX1200 digital camera and Nikon NIS-Elements 3.10.01 software (Nikon Instruments Inc.).

Metabolomics analysis

Adult Ekkwill zebrafish (n = 3 to 4 each sex from a single tank) that were exposed to each treatment for 60 days were euthanized by rapid cooling, and the kidneys were immediately dissected into pre-chilled 100% isopropanol (Sigma, UHPLC-MS grade) at a ratio of 1 mg tissue/5 μl isopropanol. The tissue was homogenized via sonication (Qsonica, Newtown, Connecticut) on ice for 1 to 3 min, with a periodicity of 10 s on–20 s off, then immediately flash-frozen and stored at −80 °C. Quantification of metabolites was performed at the Duke Proteomics & Metabolomics Core Facility using the MxP Quant 500 kit (Biocrates, Innsbruck, Austria), which provides coverage of 630 metabolites across both UPLC-MS/MS and FIA-MS/MS modalities. All solvents were UPLC-MS grade, and further sample processing and analytical quality control were performed in accordance with the analytical method validated by the kit manufacturer. Additional information about the analytical workflow can be found in the online supplementary material, Appendix S2.

All data quality control processes and statistical analyses were performed in R (version 4.4.2). To ensure high data quality, only analytes that were quantifiable above the limit of detection in 80% of samples and those that had less than 20% variability in SPQC were included in the dataset for further analysis. Differentially abundant metabolites were determined by computing a 2-way ANOVA using log-transformed analyte concentrations against treatment and sex as variables, followed by Benjamini–Hochberg multiple comparison correction. Only metabolites that were significant for either treatment or treatment by sex interaction were selected as significant. Separately, a quantitative enrichment analysis was performed to compare concentrations of metabolites against the Small Molecule Pathway Database (SMPDB) metabolite sets (MetaboAnalyst 6.0) (Pang et al. 2024). As recommended, a custom reference metabolome composed of all metabolites with HMDB IDs that are included in the MxP Quant 500 kit was used. Based on the processes identified as significantly impacted by the exposure, concentrations of individual analytes contributing to those processes were evaluated using a Kruskal–Wallis test with Dunn’s post-hoc test.

Mitochondrial respiration

Mitochondrial respiration was measured in live, intact kidneys ex vivo using the Seahorse XFe24 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, California). Adult zebrafish (n = 4 to 6 each sex) were exposed to each treatment for 10 and 60 days. Kidney tissue was dissected into 2.0 ml 28 °C Seahorse XF DMEM Medium, pH 7.4 (103575, Agilent Technologies) with 5 μM Sodium Pyruvate added (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, Massachusetts). After the instrument was calibrated, all tissues were transferred into 0.6 ml fresh media in individual wells of a 24-well Seahorse islet plate (Agilent Technologies) and secured under the provided screen. Then, oxygen consumption (pmol O2/min) was measured for 4 cycles, each cycle consisting of 3-min mix, 1-min wait, and 2-min measure windows. Oxygen consumption rate (OCR) was highly stable over this time period, and oxygenation remained between 70 and 140 mmHg in each well throughout the assay. Therefore, OCR was analyzed as the average of all 4 measurement windows. Immediately after the final measurement, tissues were flash-frozen in DNA/RNA Shield (Zymo Research, Irvine, California) for mitochondrial copy number analysis. Each assay plate contained samples from all 4 treatment groups, and this experiment was repeated for 2 to 3 independent exposure cohorts of zebrafish at each timepoint. OCR measurements were stratified by sex for statistical analysis due to a significant treatment-by-sex interaction. As there was no statistically significant effect of exposure cohort within each timepoint, data from all cohorts were pooled for subsequent analyses. Statistical differences were evaluated using a Kruskal–Wallis test and Dunn’s post-hoc analysis.

Mitochondrial copy number

Mitochondrial copy number was determined using a dual probe-based qPCR approach that compares the number of mitochondrial genomes to the number of nuclear genomes in each sample. Tissues used in the respiration assay were thawed after storage in DNA/RNA Shield (Zymo Research, Irvine, California), lysed with a hand-held homogenizer in Zymo Solid Tissue Buffer, and whole gDNA purified using Zymo DNA Mini+ 96-well kit (Zymo Research). FAM-mt-nd1 and SUN-nuc-actin1b (PrimeTime qPCR Probes, IDT, Coralville, Iowa) were used to target mtDNA and nuclear DNA, respectively. Each qPCR reaction used Luna Universal Probe qPCR Master Mix (New England Bio Labs, Ipswich, Massachusetts), mt-nd1 probe (ACGAGCATCATACCCACGATTCCG) and primers (FW: CGTTTACCCCAGATGCACCT, RV: GTGCGATTGGTAGGGCGATA) and gDNA-actin1b probe (AGCAAGAGAGGTATCCTGACCCTCA) and primers (FW: GGTGAGCTGGTGCTTTAAT, RV: ACAATACCGTGCTCAATGG), and real-time qPCR data were acquired using a qTower3 instrument (Analytik Jena, Germany). All qPCR was performed using the same master mix, reaction efficiency on each plate was validated by an mtDNA standard curve, and every DNA sample was run in triplicate. Mitochondrial copy number was computed by mtCN = 2×2(Ctnuclear—CtmtDNA) for each technical replicate well, then averaging this ratio for each sample if the coefficient of variation of all technical replicates was less than 0.2. A comparison of copy number between groups was performed using a Kruskal–Wallis test.

Mitophagy

The zebrafish transgenic line Tg(cox8-eGFP-mCherry), abbreviated Tg(MitoQC), constitutively expresses the tandem reporter cox8-eGFP-mCherry and produces red-green fluorescence in all mitochondria (Wrighton et al. 2021). As mitochondria are digested in acidic lysosomes, eGFP fluorescence is lost, and post-mitophagic mCherry-rich vesicles remain. Tg(MitoQC) fish were exposed to each treatment group for 10 and 60 days. Whole kidneys of Tg(MitoQC) individuals were dissected directly into 2% paraformaldehyde in PBS, then fixed overnight at 4 °C with rocking. The PBS solution was preadjusted to pH = 7.0 to avoid artificially unquenching the fluorescence of any eGFP molecules that had been acidified in the lysosome and preserve the pH-sensitive readout of the reporter (McWilliams et al. 2016). Kidneys were then washed 5× for 30 min each, then overnight in PBS at room temperature with rocking. Subsequently, fixed tissues were embedded in 5% low-melt agarose in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), vibratome-sectioned at 150 μm thickness (Leica, Germany), and mounted on slides in mounting medium (10 mM Tris-base, pH = 8.0 in 90% glycerol and 1% N-propyl gallate, Sigma-Aldrich) (Levic et al. 2021). This sectioning and mounting strategy facilitated confocal imaging of mitophagic activity across the entire area of multiple dorsal sections, as it was designed to remove capture-area bias and preserve endogenous fluorescence of the reporter. A total of 3 to 5 zebrafish of each sex were assayed for each treatment group in each experiment, and this experiment was repeated for 2 to 3 independent exposure cohorts of zebrafish at each timepoint.

Confocal images were captured on a Zeiss 880 Axioscope (Carl Zeiss Microscopy, White Plains, New York) using a 20× objective lens. The entire area of each tissue section was captured by tile stitching using Zen Software (version 2.3, Carl Zeiss Microscopy). Fluorescent image settings were chosen to avoid pixel saturation in any channel and exclude autofluorescence by using a wild-type kidney prepared alongside those of the reporter line. Due to the high intensity of mCherry signal in post-mitophagic punctae of some treatment groups, mCherry signal in normal mitochondria was comparatively dim and was below the detection range to avoid oversaturation of the mitophagic particles.

Images were processed and quantified using ImageJ (v1.54p) (Schindelin et al. 2012; Schneider et al. 2012). As in McWilliams et al. (2016), mitophagic activity was identified by calculating the ratio of mCherry/eGFP fluorescence intensity at each pixel and identifying punctae based on appropriate thresholds of size 1 to 100 μm and circularity of 0.2 to 1. To calculate the density of mitophagosomes in each tissue, the total kidney area was calculated based on the total area of eGFP signal in each tiled image. The entire area of 2 to 3 sections of kidney tissue at least 150 μm apart was captured for each fish, and the density of mitophagic activity was calculated across all sections. Statistical comparisons were performed using a Kruskal–Wallis test and Dunn’s post-hoc test for each timepoint. As there was no statistically significant effect of exposure cohort within each timepoint, data from all cohorts were pooled for statistical analyses. To estimate the acidification rate of mitochondrial punctae, a second analysis was performed that identified punctae based on raw mCherry fluorescence, using the same particle criteria of size 1 to 100 μm and circularity of 0.2 to 1. The mean mCherry fluorescence and mean GFP fluorescence of each particle was calculated, then the ratio of mCherry/GFP fluorescence was computed. Given that the acidified punctae contained little to no GFP fluorescence, any GFP fluorescence value less than 0.01 was set to 0.01 to avoid ratios approaching infinity.

ResultsGlyphosate impacts the accumulation of metals in tissues during co-exposure

We previously identified biological interactions between glyphosate and the metal mixture during early development. To discern whether the biological consequences of co-exposure may be driven by differences in the distribution of toxicants, we measured metal concentrations in the kidney, liver, and remaining carcass of zebrafish exposed to either the metal mixture alone or the glyphosate + metals co-exposure for 60 days. Across fish exposed to metals in the presence or absence of glyphosate, the metals Cd, Pb, and V primarily accumulated in the kidney, whereas As predominantly accumulated in the liver and carcass (Fig. 1A). There was no statistically significant effect of sex on how Pb or V was distributed across the bodily compartments (P = 0.361 and P = 0.483, respectively) (Fig. S1A). There was a lower burden of Cd in the liver of male fish (P = 0.025), meaning more of the total burden of Cd was concentrated in the kidney in male fish and less in the liver (P = 0.024 and P = 0.002, respectively) (Fig. S1A and B). As is preferentially distributed into the liver in female fish compared with the carcass compartment, reflecting a higher liver burden of As in female fish (P = 0.025).

A) Mean distribution of the burden of As, Cd, Pb, and V among whole kidney, liver, and remaining carcass in all individuals (n = 6 zebrafish each sex). B) Mean tissue burden of fish exposed to metals in the presence of glyphosate relative to metals alone. Asterisks represent significant (P < 0.05) impact of glyphosate computed by the Kruskal–Wallis test for each tissue and hash represents P < 0.10.

To understand whether glyphosate changes the tissue distribution or burden of metal species, we compared the concentration of metals in each tissue in the presence and absence of glyphosate co-exposure (Fig. 1B). Glyphosate increased the total body burden of As (P = 0.088), increased the total body burden of Pb in female fish (P = 0.049), and decreased the total body burden of V (P = 0.028) (Fig. S1B). On a tissue-specific level, glyphosate increased the Cd burden in the kidney tissue (P = 0.042) and decreased the burden of V in all tissue compartments by 50% to 70% (P = 0.011 to 0.028). There was no interaction between glyphosate and sex for Cd (P = 0.112), As (P = 0.365), or V (P = 0.089).

All exposures compromise proximal tubule uptake function over time

To discern whether glyphosate and metals could pose a risk to kidney function at drinking water concentrations, we assessed proximal tubule function over the course of the chronic exposure using the zebrafish Tg(NL-D3) (Fig. 2A). We observe that glyphosate, metals, and the glyphosate + metals co-exposure led to an increase in urinary luminescence after 50 and 60 days of exposure, illustrating that tubular reuptake function was impaired (Fig. 2B). At 60 days, exposed fish had a median 3- to 6-fold increase in LMW proteinuria compared with controls, and the most impacted individuals had 20 to 40 times more NL-D3 lost in the urine. There was no statistically significant effect of sex or biological replicate on urinary luminescence.

Glyphosate is a significant driver of cytoplasmic vacuolation in renal epithelial cells

We investigated histological morphology of kidney tissue at 10 and 60 days of exposure to determine whether structural changes were present and if they corresponded to known pathological indicators. We performed a qualitative analysis of all sections to identify key structural features, followed by a blind quantitative analysis of those features. Detailed findings from the qualitative analysis can be found in the online supplementary material, Appendix S1.

Quantitative analysis of histologic sections showed there was extensive cytoplasmic vacuolation in proximal tubules of fish exposed to glyphosate and co-exposure to glyphosate + metals for 60 days (P = 0.008) (Fig. 3A and B). Vacuolation was not detected in fish exposed to the metal mixture or in any group at 10 days of exposure (P = 0.072). In addition, there was no significant effect of sex detected at any timepoint (P = 0.128) (Fig. 3B). Cellular pathogenesis was in the form of large, rounded vacuoles that pushed nuclei to the outer margins of the cells. This lesion is indicative of intracellular accumulation of material such as lipid species, aqueous solutes, or cellular material within proximal tubule epithelial cells.

We also observed a cytoplasmic alteration that shared some features with the pattern of subcellular vacuolation but differed in morphology, especially in the glyphosate + metals co-exposed fish at 60 days. The cytoplasm appeared lacy and had clear gaps in H&E intensity, but the margins of each gap were not as clearly defined as in the vacuolated cells (Fig. S2A). Additionally, the size of each gap in the cytosol was much smaller and more diffuse in the lacy cytoplasm feature compared with the vacuolated cells. Given that 2 types of cytoplasmic alterations were observed, each was quantified separately for precision. However, it is possible that these features represent a related pathology, such as differential severity or more isometric vacuolation, because both features are significantly increased in co-exposure at 60 days (Fig. S2B).

It is important to acknowledge that artifactual cytoplasmic vacuolation can result from autolysis of kidney tissue and be falsely identified as a pathological feature. Autolysis can occur rapidly in the time between euthanasia and tissue fixation or when examining tissues from moribund individuals. We are confident that the vacuolation we observed in this work was not artifactual, as all individuals were active and oriented, with no signs of malaise or stress, and tissues were fixed within 1 min of euthanasia. There was also no confluent loss of cytoplasmic or nuclear detail that would indicate changes were the result of autolysis. In addition, we quantified tissue features in a blinded manner and identified significant vacuolation in only glyphosate and glyphosate + metals exposed fish (Fig. 3B).

Using H&E stain, the observed vacuolation presents as subcellular compartments that are neither basophilic nor eosinophilic. Although we are unable to discern the molecular contents of the vacuoles from the H&E stain, vacuolation could represent the accumulation of lipids or aqueous contents such as distended organelles or dysregulated intracellular fluid homeostasis, as in hydropic degeneration. Given that proximal tubule vacuolation is often attributed to the accumulation of lipid species, we performed an Oil Red O stain on kidney sections at 10 and 60 days of exposure to glyphosate, the metal mixture, and the glyphosate + metals co-exposure to visualize lipid stores within the tissue. However, we found no evidence that the observed vacuoles in proximal tubules are composed of accumulated lipid species (Fig. S3). Intracellular Oil Red O stain resembled small (∼1 to 5 μm) deposits, diffusely spread throughout the proximal tubule as compared with the patterning of the vacuoles, which dominated the cytoplasm of all proximal tubule cells within the nephron. Furthermore, we did not observe striking differences in Oil Red O stain between exposure groups by qualitative analysis.

In addition, we quantified the generation of new nephrons, a process termed neonephrogenesis, throughout the tissue. Neonephrogenesis is characterized by dense basophilic clusters of tubule cells that will develop into additional nephrons (Fig. 3C). Zebrafish maintain a population of stem cells within kidney stroma that can generate nephrons de novo as a part of tissue growth or regeneration after nephron injury (Diep et al. 2011; Kamei et al. 2019). At 60 days, there was a mild, significant increase in neonephrogenesis in the tissue of fish exposed to glyphosate + metals (Fig. 3D). This may reflect a state of recovery to compensate for damaged nephrons, as proximal tubule function is affected at this timepoint. However, much existing understanding of neonephrogenesis is in the context of regeneration from acute nephrotoxic insults, and it is not known how chronic, low-level exposure may stimulate the process. Additionally, there was an overall increase in neonephrogenesis at 10 days, including in the control fish, compared with the 60-day timepoint (P < 0.001). This is likely explained by tissue expansion stimulated in the fish upon transfer from colony housing to slightly decreased density in exposure system housing, as kidney and bodily growth are associated with housing density (Zhou et al. 2010). There was no significant effect of sex on the incidence of neonephrogenesis (P = 0.561).

Glyphosate + metals co-exposure disrupts mitochondrial beta oxidation

The incidence of LMW proteinuria and subcellular vacuolation at 60 days of exposure both suggest low-grade, chronic renal injury to the proximal tubule. To investigate which processes are impacted subcellularly, we quantified 630 metabolites in kidney tissue across broad molecular classes and pathways. The glyphosate + metals exposure produced the most unique metabolomic profile compared with the control, whereas the individual glyphosate and metals exposures resulted in an intermediate response with comparatively few differentially abundant metabolites (Fig. 4A and B).

To identify which biological processes may be especially affected by co-exposure to glyphosate + metals, we performed a quantitative enrichment analysis of all measured metabolites compared with SMPDB metabolite sets (Pang et al. 2024). The glyphosate + metals co-exposure disrupted multiple facets of mitochondrial metabolism in kidney tissue. The primary process affected was mitochondrial beta-oxidation of fatty acids (FAO), with consequences for energy balance across gluconeogenesis, glycolysis, and pyruvate metabolism (Fig. 4C). The quantitative enrichment analyses for exposure to the metal mixture and glyphosate alone can be found in Fig. S4.

The observed changes in substrates for mitochondrial beta-oxidation directly pointed to a chokepoint for energy generation, as mitochondria in the renal proximal tubule rely on beta-FAO to fuel the Krebs cycle and generate ATP (Uchida and Endou 1988; Simon and Hertig 2015; Bhargava and Schnellmann 2017). With co-exposure to glyphosate + metals, both acylcarnitines and fatty acids were diminished, whereas triacylglycerols were substantially increased on a class level (P = 0.002, P = 0.064, and P = 0.048, respectively) in the kidneys (Fig. 4D). Each metabolite class represents a distinct pool of substrate for FAO. Acylcarnitines represent the immediate pool of substrate for FAO, fatty acids serve as intracellular precursors to acylcarnitines and are a nexus point for nutritional signaling in the cytosol, and triacylglycerols are the predominant form of lipid storage and transport between organs (Houten and Wanders 2010). The decreased abundance of FAO substrates observed from glyphosate + metals co-exposure in combination with increased lipid storage is paradoxical in healthy tissues, as lipid storage is programed to be favored when FAO substrates are abundant.

To discern whether diminished levels of acylcarnitines could reflect cellular adjustment to higher energetic demand rather than metabolic dysfunction, we examined carnitine metabolism. Carnitine shuttling is an important checkpoint in mitochondrial metabolism of fatty acids and involves the transport of free fatty acids into the mitochondria via conjugation to carnitine (Takemura et al. 2020). In kidneys of glyphosate + metal-exposed fish, there is a nearly 50% reduction in carnitine concentration (P = 0.013) (Fig. 4E). Furthermore, there is an evident increase in the ratio of esterified carnitines to free carnitine in all treatment groups, indicating that the tissue is experiencing carnitine deficiency relative to demand (P = 0.051) (Fig. 4F). For carnitine individually and acylcarnitines as a class, there was a significant effect of sex on metabolite concentration, but no significant interaction between exposure condition and sex was detected by 2-way ANOVA (P = 0.761 and P = 0.966).

Co-exposure to glyphosate + metals decreases mitochondrial respiration without changing mitochondrial copy number

Given the clear signature of mitochondrial dysregulation from the metabolomics analysis and multiple lines of evidence for proximal tubule reuptake dysfunction, we sought to detect a change in mitochondrial function in the tissues directly. First, we measured oxygen consumption in whole, live kidney tissue ex vivo. We identified a significant interaction between sex and exposure condition and subdivided the dataset by sex to perform statistical comparisons. The kidneys from female fish suffer a 50% median decrease in OCR after 60 days of exposure to glyphosate + metals and a 40% median decrease from exposure to glyphosate alone (P = 0.024), with a similar trend observed at 10 days in female fish (P = 0.191) (Fig. 5A). However, the kidney tissue from male fish exhibited no significant changes in oxygen consumed from treatment at either 10 days (P = 0.235) or 60 days (P = 0.233).

To determine whether the observed change in mitochondrial function was driven by mitochondrial content, we quantified the abundance of mitochondrial genomes in the kidney tissue using a subset of those assayed for respiration. There was no significant difference in mitochondrial copy number from exposure (P = 0.413) or by sex (P = 0.080) (Fig. 5B). The observed deficit in mitochondrial function is not explained by the abundance of mtDNA, which suggests that the mitochondria present in the kidney tissue are respiring less effectively after exposure to glyphosate and glyphosate + metals.

Chronic exposure to glyphosate + metals suppresses mitophagy in renal tubules

Due to the evidence of mitochondrial dysfunction, we investigated whether mitophagy, the process by which damaged mitochondria are degraded in the lysosome, was induced. Proximal tubule cells maintain high baseline levels of mitophagy, as reactive oxygen species (ROS) are produced during oxidative phosphorylation, and oxidatively damaged mitochondrial components must be cleared and replaced to maintain efficient fatty acid oxidation, generation of ATP, and other mitochondrial functions (McWilliams et al. 2016; Tang et al. 2020; Ma et al. 2022). We utilized the transgenic zebrafish Tg(MitoQC) as a robust in vivo reporter to interrogate mitophagic activity in the kidney (Fig. 6A and B). The tandem reporter cox8-GFP-mCherry produces red-green fluorescence in all mitochondria; as mitochondria are digested in acidic lysosomes, GFP fluorescence is quenched, and post-mitophagic mCherry-rich vesicles remain.

At 10 days of exposure, there was no significant change in the percentage area of kidney tissue with detectable mitophagy in any treatment (P = 0.477) (Fig. 6C). However, by 60 days, there was a near-complete ablation of detectable mitophagy events across all treatment groups, most severely in the glyphosate + metals co-exposure (P = 0.043). It is especially clear from the distribution of mitophagosome density within the tissue that mitophagic events are sparse at 60 days (P < 0.001) (Fig. 6D). There was no significant effect of sex on mitophagic activity detected (P = 0.361).

Critically, there was an increased frequency of GFP+/mCherry+ punctae within kidney tissue at 60 days (Fig. 6E). The persistence of GFP fluorescence in mCherry+ punctae indicates that mitochondria may be accumulating in unacidified mitophagosomes. The total density of mCherry+ punctae (both GFP+ and GFP−) was not significantly reduced by glyphosate + metals co-exposure (P = 0.200), which suggests that isolation of mitochondria into mitophagosomes may not be impacted as much as the acidification of mitophagosomes by fusion with lysosomes (Fig. 6F). The failure to clear dysfunctional mitochondria by mitophagy may be responsible for the observation that mitochondrial genomes per cell do not change, despite consequences for mitochondrial respiration.

DiscussionGlyphosate and metals interact and are nephrotoxicants at low levels

Overall, our results demonstrate nephrotoxicity of co-exposure to glyphosate + metals at very low levels, relevant to drinking water concentrations. We show that co-exposure produces pathological change, disrupts mitochondrial metabolism, and impairs mitochondrial function and mitophagy more severely than exposure to the same concentrations of glyphosate and the metal mixture individually. Glyphosate also impacts the fate of metal species within the fish over the course of chronic exposure. Altogether, our findings demonstrate that glyphosate and metals disrupt mitochondrial function and impair proximal tubule health.

Glyphosate affects the metal burden within tissues

The tissue distribution patterns of the dosed metal species, including preferential accumulation of Cd (II) in the kidney, are consistent with existing literature (Miramand et al. 1992; Barbier et al. 2005; Thijssen et al. 2007; Faroon et al. 2012; Ambrosio et al. 2014; Lanphear et al. 2018; Balali-Mood et al. 2021). We demonstrate that glyphosate increased the burden of Cd in the kidney and decreased the burden of V, but the mechanism of this effect is not clear. Both Cd and V readily enter the proximal tubule epithelium by receptor-mediated reuptake of plasma proteins with bound metals or by direct interaction of the metal ion with renal transporters. Cd accumulates due to uptake by divalent metal transporters and zinc/iron proteins, whereas V is taken up by citrate, lactate, organic anion transporters, and phosphate or sulfate anion channels (Ballatori 2002; Johri et al. 2010; Thévenod 2010; Leggett and O’Connell 2018; Treviño et al. 2019). Although the transport mechanisms of glyphosate into proximal tubule cells are not yet known, glyphosate has been shown to interact with organic anion transporters and modify the uptake of other substrates (Hsueh et al. 2016). The increased uptake of divalent Cd but not pentavalent V suggests that chelation between glyphosate and divalent species may be important in mediating biodistribution in this exposure, which has been demonstrated in invertebrate models (Tsui et al. 2005; Hansen and Roslev 2016).

Co-exposure worsens kidney health outcomes compared with individual chemicals

Although the mechanism of chemical and biological interaction warrants further investigation, we highlight that co-exposure to glyphosate + metals worsens kidney health outcomes across multiple endpoints (Table 1). It is possible that the kidney tissue is exposed to a higher concentration of glyphosate in addition to a higher burden of metals in the co-exposure, as chelation of metals stabilizes glyphosate in aqueous solutions (i.e. reduced glyphosate clearance during co-exposure) (Bento et al. 2016; Mertens et al. 2018). However, the stability and persistence of glyphosate–metal complexes under physiological conditions remain uncertain, making it difficult to distinguish whether the observed changes in metal biodistribution result from complexation in the exposure medium, altered transport kinetics, or interactions at the cellular level. Second, glyphosate and toxic metals may exert renal toxicity via common mechanisms, and co-exposure may be additive (Upamalika et al. 2022). Exposure to glyphosate alone largely affected metabolite concentrations in the same direction as the co-exposure, though the impact of the co-exposure was more pronounced. Similarly, the quantitative enrichment analysis of metabolites in metal-exposed fish identified many of the same processes as the co-exposure, but with a weaker magnitude of change. Both the metabolome and our previous larval transcriptome demonstrate potential for additive effects of glyphosate and metals across multiple pathways shared between both individual and co-exposure.

The proximal tubule is a major target of glyphosate and metals nephrotoxicity

We found that extended exposure to glyphosate + metals had consequences for renal function as indicated by the loss of LMW proteins into the urine. LMW proteinuria is used as a clinical diagnostic criterion for proximal tubule nephropathy in patients and is a known outcome of nephrotoxicity from Cd, Pb, and other environmental contaminants (Thijssen et al. 2007; Johri et al. 2010; Cabral et al. 2012; Chaumont et al. 2012; Zhao et al. 2020; Kolli et al. 2023; Little et al. 2023). The 3- to 5-fold magnitude of increased LMW proteinuria that we observed is clinically significant: In humans, 2- to 3-fold elevations of urinary β-microglobulin above normal levels are considered indicative of early tubular dysfunction, whereas increases exceeding 10-fold may indicate more severe tubular damage (Kowal and Zirkes 1983; Tolkoff-Rubin et al. 1988; Magdo et al. 2007; EFSA 2009). Here, for the first time, we experimentally demonstrated that drinking water with relevant concentrations of glyphosate and metals can induce LMW proteinuria.

Glyphosate + metals co-exposure targets the proximal tubule on a pathological level as indicated by intracellular vacuolation, which reflects sub-cytotoxic cellular dysfunction (Bendele et al. 1998; Fogo et al. 2017). Vacuole formation could result from the accumulation of lipids, as is associated with hyperlipidemia and nephrotic syndrome (Agrawal et al. 2018; Lin and Duann 2020). Despite the identification of ∼30% increase in total triacylglycerols by metabolomics, we do not detect large deposits of lipids in proximal tubule cells. Vacuoles can also arise from osmotic swelling of autophagosomes containing undigested cargo due to dysfunction of the autophagolysosomal system or mild hydropic changes, which can arise from osmotic imbalances and may represent distended mitochondria, distended Golgi complex cisternae, or expanded endoplasmic reticulum (Miller and Zachary 2017). Vacuoles of autophagolysosomal etiology are often associated with mitochondrial dysfunction and have been identified in mouse models of chronic exposure to Cd and As, though at higher concentrations (Tsukamoto et al. 1983; Thijssen et al. 2007; Li et al. 2017; Ding et al. 2020; Li et al. 2021). The observed decrease in proximal tubule uptake function and mitophagy supports the hypothesis that the vacuoles are a product of autophagolysosomal dysfunction, as these processes rely on transport and lysosomal digestive capacity. Finally, vacuolation may reflect altered homeostasis of aqueous solutes. Glyphosate may interfere with the function of glycine through molecular mimicry, which is important for osmolar regulation and protection from mitochondrial stress (Lang et al. 1998; Kumar et al. 2023).

Glyphosate + metals co-exposure disrupts mitochondrial metabolism

Chronic exposure to glyphosate + metals disrupted the cornerstone of mitochondrial energy production in the proximal tubule, fatty acid oxidation (FAO), as identified by enrichment analysis. Due to high energetic demand, proximal tubule epithelial cells rely exclusively on FAO to produce ATP (Uchida and Endou 1988; Bhargava and Schnellmann 2017). Our findings show that acylcarnitine species and free fatty acids were diminished, but triacylglycerols were increased in the kidney tissue from exposure to glyphosate + metals. These results reflect dysregulation of lipid substrate utilization, wherein proximal tubule mitochondria experience FAO fuel shortage despite the abundance of lipids sequestered as triacylglycerols. Consistent with impaired FAO at 60 days, co-exposure to glyphosate + metals caused a decrease in the OCR of whole kidney tissue, particularly in females and marginally in male fish. Oxygen consumption primarily reflects oxidative phosphorylation at the mitochondrial electron transfer chain, in which the products of FAO are used to generate ATP. The more pronounced reduction in the OCR of female kidneys may reflect sex-specific differences in the regulation of mitochondrial function in response to stress. Renal mitochondria in females have been shown to exhibit tighter coupling between mitochondrial energy output and antioxidant defenses (Sultanova et al. 2020; Ide et al. 2022; Soto Miranda et al. 2024; Yang et al. 2025). It is possible that the greater decrease in OCR may reflect an adaptive response to limit ROS generation by electron leakage or increase coupling efficiency to maintain ATP production under constraint.

The nephrotoxic effects of glyphosate + metals may be driven by interconnected mechanisms of mitochondrial and lysosomal dysfunction

Our findings suggest convergent disruption of mitochondrial function, lysosomal processes, and lipid metabolism from chronic, low-level exposure to glyphosate + metals. Specifically, we measured decreased receptor-mediated uptake in the proximal tubule, a metabolic profile characterized by decreased acylcarnitines and free fatty acids alongside increased triacylglycerols, impaired mitochondrial respiration, and suppressed acidification of mitophagosomes. These findings collectively indicate impairment of energy production and degradative processes.

There are 2 major facets of mitochondrial dysfunction that may underlie the effects of glyphosate + metals co-exposure on mitochondrial health—insufficient production of ATP and excess generation of ROS. Uptake processes are energy-intensive and downregulated in multiple high-energy cell types under ATP-limited conditions, which may correspond to the decrease in proximal tubule reuptake function we observed (Devuyst and Luciani 2015; Herzig and Shaw 2018; López-Hernández et al. 2020; Lash 2021). Excess ROS reduces the efficiency of ATP production and can induce the sequestration of oxidized fatty acids into triacylglycerols or the production of vacuolation by hydropic degeneration (Bobulescu 2010; Meyer et al. 2013). The observed increase in triacylglycerols could reflect a protective response to the breakdown in mitophagy that we identified, as mitophagy is responsible for the clearance of damaged mitochondria. Substantial evidence in existing literature shows that Cd, As, Pb, V, and glyphosate all reduce efficient ATP production and increase mitochondrial ROS by multiple mechanisms (Barbier et al. 2005; Son et al. 2011; Meyer et al. 2013; Mesnage et al. 2015; Dong et al. 2018; Gao et al. 2019; Neto da Silva et al. 2020; Faria et al. 2021). At higher doses, Pb and As have been shown to impair carnitine shuttling and increase triacylglycerols in the kidney (Liu et al. 2025; Yu et al. 2025). As such, mitochondrial energy production might be a common target of these metals and glyphosate when in mixture, even at very low levels.

However, mitochondrial function is extensively interlinked with lysosomal function, and our evidence implicates lysosomal dysfunction in numerous ways. The decrease in free fatty acids we measured is consistent with impaired lysosomal liberation of fatty acids from endocytosed lipoproteins. The digestion of endocytic cargo in lysosomes is necessary for intracellular production of carnitine when uptake of free carnitine is limited, the release of fatty acids from lipoproteins, and efficient reuptake function in the proximal tubule (Longo et al. 2016; Scerbo et al. 2017; Baek et al. 2022). Our results suggest that all of these processes may be impacted by exposure to glyphosate + metals. We also measured a deficit in acidification of mitophagosomes that could reflect a specific blockade of acidification or fusion machinery or a broader lysosomal dysfunction. The observed vacuolation in proximal tubule cells is consistent with accumulated undigested autophagolysosomal cargo resulting from this lysosomal impairment. Cd, As, Pb, and glyphosate can all suppress lysosomal acidification, autophagosomal fusion, and lysosomal biogenesis (Kimura et al. 2016; Lee et al. 2017; Liu et al. 2017a; Pi et al. 2017; Dodson et al. 2018; Chargui et al. 2021).

These converging lines of evidence support an integrated model of nephrotoxicity driven by self-reinforcing disruption of mitochondrial and lysosomal function in the proximal tubule. Mitochondrial impairment limits ATP production and may generate excess ROS, compromising energy-intensive receptor-mediated uptake and suppressing lysosomal acidification required for cargo digestion and fatty acid liberation. This lysosomal dysfunction further limits substrate availability for mitochondrial fatty acid oxidation. The resulting deficit in mitophagy prevents the clearance of damaged mitochondria, perpetuating mitochondrial dysfunction. This framework may explain how chronic, low-level co-exposure produces nephrotoxicity through coordinated disruption of cellular energetics and lipid metabolism.

Mitochondrial health underlies the trajectory of kidney health

Our findings highlight the role of mitochondrial dysfunction and disruption of FAO metabolism as a driver of adverse kidney health outcomes (Granata et al. 2009; Simon and Hertig 2015; Console et al. 2020; Takemura et al. 2020; Chen et al. 2021). FAO impairment and the elevation of triacylglycerol storage promote fibrosis and progressive nephron loss (Hodgkins and Schnaper 2012; Kang et al. 2015; Yamamoto et al. 2017). Similarly, mitophagy underlies the cellular capacity for robust mitochondrial function (Pickles et al. 2018). Failure to clear damaged mitochondria contributes to fibrosis in multiple forms of renal injury and etiologies of CKD (Kawakami et al. 2015; Zhan et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2017b; Zuo et al. 2020; Tang et al. 2021; Su et al. 2023). The fact that co-exposure to glyphosate + metals impacted mitochondrial health and the mitochondrial–lysosomal axis with relevance to kidney health warrants a closer examination of the role of long-term exposure to low-level chemical mixtures in the development of kidney diseases.

Our pathological and mechanistic findings support a link between nephrotoxic exposure and environmentally driven CKD

The herbicide glyphosate and the metals Cd, As, Pb, and V are widespread drinking water contaminants, and were selected as high-priority nephrotoxicants to investigate at drinking water concentrations based on their association with Chronic Kidney Disease of Unknown Origin (CKDu) in Sri Lanka (Levine et al. 2016; Gunatilake et al. 2019; Babich et al. 2020; Ulrich et al. 2023). Existing evidence corroborates CKDu as a proximal tubule nephropathy and suggests that mitochondrial and lysosomal dysfunction underlie CKDu pathology. Our previous work identified increased abundance of LMW proteins in the urine of patients with CKDu, and here we demonstrate chronic exposure to glyphosate + metals induces LMW proteinuria (Kolli et al. 2023). In addition, the urinary proteome of CKDu patients substantially overlaps with CKDs induced by mitochondrial deficiencies as compared with CKDs of other etiologies (Kolli et al. 2023). Renal biopsies from CKDu patients reveal swollen mitochondria and dysmorphic autophagic-lysosomal aggregates in tubular epithelial cells as a consistent feature, related to the vacuolation produced by exposure to glyphosate + metals in this study (Vervaet et al. 2020). Furthermore, the impairment of renal mitophagy from exposure to glyphosate + metals represents a state of great vulnerability to other injuries, such that the cumulative burden of repeated renal stress from heat, exertion, or additional stressors may shift an individual’s renal health trajectory toward progressive kidney failure. We hope that this work contributes to evidence for a plausible link between chemical exposure and the development of environmental nephropathies such as CKDu.

Limitations and future directions

To better resolve the role of glyphosate and metals in kidney diseases, future studies should determine the transport mechanisms of glyphosate in the proximal tubule and investigate tissue accumulation of glyphosate in the presence and absence of metals. Future work could further clarify mixture dynamics, the role of glyphosate–metal complexation, and the specificity of the interactive effects of glyphosate with metals. Mechanistically, it will be valuable to discern whether mitochondrial dysfunction involves ROS imbalance and whether ATP production is affected. Furthermore, future work resolving molecular contents and the mechanistic origin of the proximal tubule vacuoles will provide substantial insight into the underlying subcellular dysfunction and the role of mitochondrial–lysosomal dysfunction. Studies of lysosomal health during chronic exposure in toxicant-induced renal dysfunction also remain important areas to explore. Finally, our findings establish the need for additional model systems that can build causal relationships between these contaminants and the onset of CKD. Nonetheless, this work experimentally demonstrates that chronic exposure to contaminants of concern in agricultural communities affected by the increasing prevalence of CKD has consequences for kidney health, even at drinking water concentrations, and provides mechanistic insight into the role of mitochondrial and lysosomal function in the progression of renal dysfunction.
