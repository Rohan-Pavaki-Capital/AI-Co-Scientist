Tic disorder (TD) frequently co-occurs with attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) and is associated with greater symptom severity and limited treatment options. Qiangzhi Fang is a Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) herbal formula that has been clinically used for neurobehavioral disorders, but its experimental evidence and mechanisms remain insufficiently characterized.

Objective

To evaluate the behavioral effects of Qiangzhi Fang in a drug-induced rat model of TD comorbid with ADHD and to explore potential striatal molecular mechanisms.

Methods

A drug-induced TD+ADHD rat model was established in male Sprague-Dawley rats. Animals were assigned to control, model, and Qiangzhi Fang treatment groups (low, medium, and high doses). Behavioral assessments included stereotypic behavior scoring (tic-like behaviors), locomotor activity (hyperactivity), anxiety-related behavior, and spatial learning and memory. Striatal expression of tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) and phosphorylated Ca2⁺/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II (p-CaMKII) was examined using Western blotting and real-time PCR.

Results

Qiangzhi Fang treatment ameliorated TD- and ADHD-like behavioral abnormalities, including reduced stereotypic movements, decreased hyperactivity, and improved spatial learning and memory performance, with the most pronounced effects observed at higher doses. At the molecular level, treatment was associated with downregulation of striatal TH and p-CaMKII expression. These markers reflect alterations in dopamine synthesis and synaptic signaling; however, dopamine levels, receptor subtypes, and neural circuitry were not directly assessed.

Conclusion

Qiangzhi Fang improved behavioral phenotypes in a preclinical rat model of TD comorbid ADHD and was associated with changes in striatal TH and p-CaMKII expression. These findings suggest a possible neuromodulatory role of Qiangzhi Fang, although the mechanistic evidence remains preliminary. This study may provide an experimental basis for further investigation of herbal formulations in TD and ADHD.

Tic disorder (TD) is a neuropsychiatric disorder that typically manifests during adolescence, with a higher prevalence in males than in females. It is clinically characterised by the presence of rapid, repetitive and arrhythmic motor or vocal tics involving one or more muscle groups. As the disease progresses, the symptoms gradually worsen and recur, seriously affecting children’s learning, daily life and social interaction, resulting in physical and mental health problems.1 The greatest risk associated with Tourette’s syndrome is the potential for complications. It has been found that more than 50% of children with Tourette syndrome will present with at least one behavior disorder. In particular, in patients with combined attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), the prevalence increases to 66%.2 Clinical studies have shown that children with TD comorbid ADHD exhibit more persistent symptoms, greater impulsivity, and more pronounced cognitive deficits than those with either disorder alone.3,4

The etiology and pathogenesis of TD co-occurring with ADHD are complex, and current research suggests that perinatal, environmental, genetic and pathophysiological factors (eg, neurobiochemical and neuroanatomical) are all associated with its development.5 Accumulating neuroimaging evidence from magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT) studies has consistently implicated dysfunction of the cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) circuitry, with particular involvement of the striatum and frontal cortex.6 Monoamine transmitters such as norepinephrine (NE) and dopamine (DA), as well as gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) and glutamate (Glu), play central roles in regulating CSTC circuits. Dopaminergic hyperactivity within the striatum is thought to contribute to tic generation, while dysregulated dopamine signaling in frontal-striatal networks is closely linked to attentional deficits and impulsivity in ADHD. Disruption of neurotransmitter balance within these pathways may impair inhibitory control over motor and behavioral outputs, thereby contributing to the core symptoms of TD and ADHD.7

Dopaminergic signaling in the striatum is critical for motor coordination, reward processing, and executive function. Tyrosine hydroxylase (TH), the rate-limiting enzyme in dopamine synthesis, is commonly used as an indicator of dopaminergic activity in experimental models of tic disorders and ADHD-like behaviors.8 In parallel, calcium/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II (CaMKII), particularly its phosphorylated form (p-CaMKII), is essential for synaptic plasticity, learning, and memory in the striatum. Aberrant CaMKII phosphorylation has been implicated in neurodevelopmental disorders characterized by impaired motor control, learning, and attention.9,10 Therefore, striatal TH and p-CaMKII represent biologically relevant molecular targets for investigating the mechanisms underlying TD comorbid ADHD.

Current treatment strategies for TD and ADHD rely primarily on pharmacological interventions, including dopamine receptor antagonists, psychostimulants, and α-adrenergic agonists. Although these therapies can alleviate symptoms in some patients, their clinical utility is often limited by adverse effects, variable efficacy, and high relapse rates, particularly in individuals with comorbid TD and ADHD.11 Hence, there is growing interest in complementary and alternative therapeutic approaches that may offer improved safety and long-term effectiveness.

Traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) emphasizes holistic regulation of physiological functions and has long been applied in neuropsychiatric disorders. In TCM theory, TD and ADHD are considered to involve dysregulation of cognitive control and behavioral restraint, concepts that partially overlap with modern neurobiological models of executive dysfunction. Qiangzhi Decoction is a compound herbal formula composed of Morinda officinalis (Ba Ji Tian), Aucklandia lappa (Mu Xiang), processed Polygala tenuifolia (Yuan Zhi), Poria cocos, Pinellia ternata, Dioscorea opposita (Yam), and Atractylodes macrocephala. Increasing pharmacological evidence suggests that several of these herbs exert neuroprotective, anti-inflammatory, and neuromodulatory effects. For example, extracts of Polygala tenuifolia have been shown to enhance cognitive function and regulate dopaminergic neurotransmission, while Morinda officinalis exhibits neuroprotective and anti-fatigue properties.12–14 Poria cocos and Atractylodes macrocephala have been reported to modulate neuroinflammation and neurotransmitter balance in the central nervous system.15

Based on these pharmacological properties and the established role of striatal dopaminergic and synaptic signaling in TD comorbid ADHD, we hypothesized that Qiangzhi Decoction may improve behavioral abnormalities by regulating dopamine synthesis and synaptic plasticity. Therefore, the present study aimed to systematically evaluate the behavioral effects of Qiangzhi Decoction in a validated rat model of TD comorbid ADHD and to explore its potential molecular mechanisms through behavioral, biochemical, and gene-expression analyses, with a focus on striatal TH and p-CaMKII.

Materials and MethodsExperimental Animals

Thirty-six specific pathogen-free (SPF) male rats were used in this study, including 30 spontaneously hypertensive rats (SHR) and 6 Sprague-Dawley (SD) rats, all aged 3 weeks. SHR rats were selected because they are a well-established animal model exhibiting ADHD-like phenotypes, including hyperactivity, impulsivity, and attention deficits, whereas SD rats served as neurologically normal controls. Only male rats were used to avoid the potential confounding effects of estrous cycle-related hormonal fluctuations on behavior and neurochemical outcomes. All animals were obtained from Spectrum Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China; License No. SCXK (Beijing) 2019–0010). Animals were housed under controlled conditions (22 ± 2°C, 55 ± 10% humidity, 12 h light/dark cycle) with free access to food and water. Rats were acclimatized for 7 days prior to experimentation. All experimental procedures were approved by the Ethics Committee for Laboratory Animals of Contemporary Medicine (Approval No. MDL2023-08-05-01) and were conducted in accordance with the National Institutes of Health Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals.

Experimental Design, Grouping, and Randomization

After acclimatization, rats were randomly assigned using a random number table into six groups (n = 6 per group): (1) Control group (SD rats); (2) Sham-operated group (SHR rats); (3) TD+ADHD model group (SHR rats); (4) Qiangzhi Fang low-dose group (2.5 g/kg); (5) Qiangzhi Fang medium-dose group (5 g/kg); (6) Qiangzhi Fang high-dose group (10 g/kg). Behavioral testing and data analysis were performed by investigators blinded to group allocation. Body weight was measured once weekly throughout the experimental period to assess general health status and to adjust drug dosages accordingly. No animals were excluded due to abnormal weight loss or illness.

Preparation and Administration of Qiangzhi Fang

Qiangzhi Fang (Patent No. ZL 201310000777.3) consists of Morinda officinalis (Ba Ji Tian, 9 g), Aucklandia lappa (Mu Xiang, 6 g), processed Polygala tenuifolia (Yuan Zhi, 9 g), Poria cocos (24 g), Pinellia ternata (9 g), Atractylodes macrocephala (18 g), and Dioscorea opposita (Chinese yam, 24 g). All herbs were sourced from the pharmacy of the Affiliated Hospital of Shandong University of Traditional Chinese Medicine and complied with the 2020 Chinese Pharmacopoeia. Herbs were decocted twice with distilled water, filtered, combined, and concentrated to yield a solution equivalent to 2 g raw herb/mL. The medium dose was set according to the human-rat equivalent dose, with low and high doses set at 0.5× and 2× this dose, respectively. Qiangzhi Fang was administered once daily by oral gavage at a volume of 10 mL/kg for 28 consecutive days. Control and sham groups received equivalent volumes of distilled water.

Construction of the TD+ADHD Rat Model

SHR rats underwent stereotaxic surgery under isoflurane anesthesia (induction 5%, maintenance 0.5–1%). After fixation in a stereotaxic apparatus, burr holes were drilled targeting the left dorsolateral striatum using coordinates referenced to the Paxinos and Watson rat brain atlas: AP +0.15 mm, ML +2.5 mm, DV −4.5 mm (relative to bregma).

A guide cannula was implanted 2 mm above the target site and fixed with dental cement. After a 7-day recovery period, TD-like behaviors were induced by daily intrastriatal microinjection of bicuculline (1 μg/μL, 0.5 μL total volume, 0.5 μL/min) for 28 days. Bicuculline, a GABA_A receptor antagonist, reliably induces tic-like motor behaviors by disinhibiting striatal circuitry, while SHR rats provide an established ADHD-like behavioral background. Sham-operated rats received equivalent saline injections.

Behavioral Assessments

Behavioral testing was conducted in a fixed order with≥24 h intervals to minimize stress effects.

Stereotypy and Locomotor Activity

Stereotypic and locomotor behaviors were assessed immediately after bicuculline injection. Scoring criteria followed established methods by Diamond and Singer. Rats scoring ≥2 on both scales were considered to meet TD model criteria.

Open-Field Test

Rats were placed in the center of a square open-field arena (80 × 80×40 cm) for 5 min. Total distance traveled, center-zone time, and movement speed were recorded using automated tracking software.

Elevated Plus Maze

The apparatus consisted of two open arms and two closed arms elevated 50 cm above the floor. The number of open-arm entries and time spent in open arms were recorded over 5 min to assess anxiety-related and impulsive behaviors.

Morris Water Maze

Spatial learning and memory were evaluated using a Morris water maze. Escape latency was recorded over 6 days of training, followed by a probe trial on day 7 to assess platform crossings and time spent in the target quadrant.

Western Blot Analysis

Striatal tissues were rapidly dissected on ice and homogenized in RIPA lysis buffer containing protease and phosphatase inhibitors. Lysates were centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 15 min at 4°C, and supernatants were collected. Protein concentrations were determined using a bicinchoninic acid (BCA) protein assay kit. Equal amounts of protein (30 μg per lane) were separated by 10% SDS-PAGE and transferred onto nitrocellulose (NC) membranes. Membranes were blocked with 5% non-fat milk in Tris-buffered saline containing 0.1% Tween-20 (TBST) for 1 h at room temperature and incubated overnight at 4°C with the following primary antibodies: Anti-tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) antibody (rabbit polyclonal, 1:1000, Abcam, ab112, UK); Anti-CaMKII antibody (rabbit monoclonal, 1:1000, Cell Signaling Technology, #4436, USA); Anti-phospho-CaMKII (Thr286) antibody (rabbit monoclonal, 1:1000, Cell Signaling Technology, #12716, USA); Anti-β-actin antibody (mouse monoclonal, 1:5000, Proteintech, 60008-1-Ig, China). After washing with TBST, membranes were incubated with HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies for 1 h at room temperature: Goat anti-rabbit IgG-HRP (1:5000, Cell Signaling Technology, #7074); Goat anti-mouse IgG-HRP (1:5000, Cell Signaling Technology, #7076). Protein bands were visualized using enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL) reagents and quantified by densitometric analysis using ImageJ software (NIH, USA). Target protein expression levels were normalized to β-actin.

Quantitative Real-Time PCR

Total RNA was extracted from striatal tissues using TRIzol reagent according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA purity and concentration were assessed by measuring absorbance at 260 and 280 nm, and samples with an A260/A280 ratio between 1.8 and 2.0 were used for subsequent analysis. Complementary DNA (cDNA) was synthesized using a reverse transcription kit following the manufacturer’s protocol. Quantitative real-time PCR was performed using SYBR Green chemistry on a fluorescence quantitative PCR system. Each reaction was conducted in triplicate. The target genes analyzed included tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) and CaMKII, with β-actin used as an internal reference gene. Primer sequences were as follows: TH: Forward: 5′-GCTGACTTCTTCACCTACCA-3′, Reverse: 5′-GGATGAGGTGGTGATGTTGT-3′; CaMKII: Forward: 5′-TCCAGAAGAAGGACAGCAAA-3′, Reverse: 5′-CAGTGTCCATGTGGTGATGT-3′; β-actin: Forward: 5′-AGATTACTGCCCTGGCTCCT-3′, Reverse: 5′-ACAGAGTACTTGCGCTCAG-3′. Thermal cycling conditions were as follows: initial denaturation at 95°C for 30s, followed by 40 cycles of denaturation at 95°C for 5s, annealing at 58°C for 30s, and extension at 72°C for 30s. Relative gene expression was calculated using the 2−ΔΔCt method.

Statistical Analysis

All data were analyzed using SPSS version 26.0 (IBM, Armonk, NY, USA) and are presented as mean ± standard error of the mean (SEM). Data normality was assessed using the Shapiro–Wilk test. For data that met assumptions of normal distribution and homogeneity of variance, one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was performed, followed by the least significant difference (LSD) post hoc test for planned pairwise comparisons. The LSD test was applied because group comparisons were predefined and limited in number, thereby minimizing inflation of type I error. For data that did not conform to normal distribution, non-parametric Mann–Whitney U-tests were used for two-group comparisons. No missing data were observed; outliers were assessed using boxplot inspection and were retained unless biologically implausible. A value of P < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

ResultsChanges in Body Weight

Baseline body weight did not differ significantly among groups (p > 0.05). Following model induction, all SHR groups exhibited significantly lower body weight compared with the blank control group (p < 0.05). After 4 weeks of treatment, rats receiving low-, medium-, or high-dose Qiangzhi Fang showed significantly higher body weight than the untreated model group (p < 0.05), although values remained below those of blank controls.

Stereotype Behavior

No stereotypic behaviors were observed at baseline in any group. Model induction significantly increased stereotypic scores compared with both blank and sham-operated groups (p < 0.05). Qiangzhi Fang treatment significantly reduced stereotypic behavior in a dose-dependent manner, with medium- and high-dose groups showing greater reductions than the model group (p < 0.05).

Motor Activity

Motor behavior scores were comparable across groups at baseline (p > 0.05). Following model induction, motor activity scores were significantly elevated in the TD+ADHD model group relative to blank and sham groups (p < 0.05), indicating increased motor hyperactivity. Treatment with Qiangzhi Fang significantly reduced motor scores, with the greatest reduction observed in the high-dose group compared with the model group (p < 0.05) (Figure 1).

At baseline, SHR rats exhibited greater locomotor activity than blank controls (p < 0.05). Model induction resulted in a significant reduction in total distance traveled, central distance, central time, and mean speed compared with sham-operated rats (p < 0.05). Compared to the model group, Qiangzhi Fang treatment further reduced these parameters in a dose-dependent manner (p < 0.05), with the most pronounced effects in the high-dose group (Figure 2). These changes reflect reduced locomotor activity rather than motor impairment.

Western Blot Analysis

Compared with blank controls, the TD+ADHD model group did not show statistically significant changes in total TH or CaMKII protein expression (p > 0.05). Phosphorylated TH, phosphorylated CaMKII, and dopamine protein levels showed an increasing trend in the model group, although these differences did not reach statistical significance. High-dose Qiangzhi Fang treatment significantly reduced p-TH, p-CaMKII, and dopamine protein levels compared with the model group (p < 0.05), while total TH and CaMKII expression remained unchanged (Figure 5).

Quantitative PCR Analysis

The mRNA expression levels of TH, CaMKII, and dopamine-related transcripts were higher in the model group than in blank controls, but these differences were not statistically significant (p > 0.05). High-dose Qiangzhi Fang treatment significantly downregulated TH, CaMKII, and dopamine-related mRNA expression compared with the model group (p < 0.05), consistent with the protein expression findings (Figure 5).

Discussion

TD co-occurring with ADHD is the most common comorbidity pattern observed in children with tic disorders. The coexistence of involuntary motor tics, hyperactivity, impulsivity, and attentional dysregulation suggests shared but incompletely understood neurobiological mechanisms. The lack of well-characterized animal models that simultaneously reproduce both tic-like and ADHD-like behavioral domains has hindered mechanistic and pharmacological research. In this context, the present study adopted the striatal disinhibition strategy described by Francesca Rizzo et al, using bicuculline administration in spontaneously hypertensive rats (SHR), to establish a TD co-occurring ADHD rat model and to evaluate the behavioral and molecular effects of Qiangzhi Fang.16 Based on the previously reported model, we introduced a modified intracranial injection cannula by shortening the cannula length and adding a fixation nut. This modification was designed to improve experimental operability and facilitate repeated drug administration throughout the behavioral testing period. Although this optimization likely reduces handling difficulty and improves procedural stability, we acknowledge that no direct histological, biochemical, or stress-related markers were assessed to validate improvements in reproducibility or physiological burden.

From a behavioral perspective, stereotyped behavior scoring remains a widely accepted criterion for assessing tic-like phenotypes in rodent models. In this study, rats in the model group exhibited stereotypy and motor scores ≥2 following modeling, consistent with established thresholds for successful tic induction.17 These repetitive, non-goal-directed movements are thought to reflect impaired inhibitory control within cortico-striatal circuits and are commonly reported in pharmacological and genetic TD models.18–21 The significant reduction in stereotyped behaviors following Qiangzhi Fang treatment indicates that the intervention effectively attenuated this core tic-related phenotype.

Hyperactivity-related behaviors were evaluated using the open field test.22 Increased total distance traveled and elevated locomotor activity are well-established indicators of hyperactivity in SHR and other ADHD-related models, whereas average speed is often interpreted as reflecting impulsive or poorly regulated motor output.20,21,23 In the present study, model rats displayed increased locomotor activity compared with sham-operated controls, consistent with the known behavioral profile of SHR. Following Qiangzhi Fang administration, reductions in several locomotor parameters were observed, particularly in the high-dose group. However, not all open field indices reached statistical significance. Accordingly, these findings are interpreted as attenuation of hyperactivity-related behavior, rather than complete normalization, and claims of efficacy have been limited to domains supported by statistically significant results.

The elevated plus maze was employed to assess impulsivity-related behavioral tendencies under conditions of approach-avoidance conflict. In ADHD models, increased open-arm exploration is frequently interpreted as reduced risk evaluation or heightened impulsivity rather than decreased anxiety perse.24,25 In this study, modeled rats showed alterations in open-arm exploration compared with sham animals; however, these differences did not reach statistical significance. Similarly, Qiangzhi Fang treatment produced trends toward reduced open-arm entries and duration, but these changes were modest. Cognitive and attention-related behaviors were assessed using the Morris water maze. Although impairments in spatial learning and attention have been reported in SHR, such deficits are often mild and variable.26–28 In the present study, the model group showed reductions in platform crossings, target quadrant time, and target quadrant distance, but these changes were not statistically significant. This suggests that the model exhibits limited and inconsistent cognitive impairment, rather than robust learning or memory deficits. Taken together, the combined results of stereotypy scoring, open field testing, elevated plus maze, and Morris water maze indicate that the established model reproduces tic-like behaviors and ADHD-related hyperactivity and impulsivity, while cognitive alterations are relatively subtle. This behavioral profile is consistent with prior reports16 using SHR-based and striatal disinhibition models and supports the relevance of the model to TD co-occurring ADHD rather than to isolated cognitive dysfunction. Importantly, the use of multiple behavioral paradigms allows a more comprehensive and nuanced assessment of symptom domains, reducing reliance on any single behavioral outcome.

At the neurobiological level, TD has been widely associated with dopaminergic hyperactivity or dysregulated DA signaling within cortico-striatal circuits, whereas ADHD has traditionally been linked to dopaminergic hypofunction, particularly in prefrontal regions.29 The apparent coexistence of these conditions highlights the complexity of DA regulation in TD-ADHD comorbidity.30,31 CaMKII is a key mediator of intracellular Ca2⁺ signaling and regulates TH activity through phosphorylation, thereby influencing DA synthesis and release. Previous studies have reported correlations between CaMKII activation, TH phosphorylation, and synaptic DA levels.32–35 In the present study, no significant differences in TH, CaMKII, P-TH, P-CaMKII, or DA expression were observed between the model and control groups. We hypothesize that this may reflect offsetting effects between the baseline dopaminergic alterations in SHR and bicuculline-induced changes in striatal DA signaling; however, this interpretation remains speculative and requires confirmation using direct neurochemical approaches such as microdialysis or HPLC. Following Qiangzhi Fang treatment, reductions in P-TH, P-CaMKII, and DA protein levels, as well as decreased CaMKII, TH, and DA mRNA expression, were observed. These findings suggest that Qiangzhi Fang may modulate DA-related signaling pathways; however, we emphasize that these associations are correlative, and causal relationships cannot be inferred without further mechanistic experiments.

It is also important to note that TD and ADHD involve multiple neurotransmitter systems, including GABAergic, glutamatergic, and noradrenergic pathways. The present study focused primarily on dopaminergic signaling, which represents a limitation in mechanistic scope. Future studies incorporating multi-neurotransmitter analyses and circuit-level investigations will be necessary to more fully characterize the pharmacological profile of Qiangzhi Fang. Regarding safety, increased body weight observed in Qiangzhi Fang-treated rats suggests an absence of overt growth suppression; however, body weight alone is insufficient to establish toxicological safety. Comprehensive evaluations including organ function markers and histopathological analyses are required in future studies. Finally, although the present findings provide experimental support for the behavioral relevance of Qiangzhi Fang in a TD co-occurring ADHD model, translational limitations should be acknowledged. Differences in dose equivalence, pharmacokinetics, and the inherent constraints of extrapolating rodent behavioral paradigms to pediatric neuropsychiatric disorders warrant cautious interpretation. Further preclinical and clinical studies are needed to determine the therapeutic potential of Qiangzhi Fang in human TD-ADHD populations.

Conclusion

In summary, Qiangzhi Fang treatment in a TD co-occurring ADHD rat model was associated with improvements in tic-like behaviors and partial modulation of hyperactivity, while cognitive and attention-related performance showed limited or non-significant changes. At the molecular level, treatment was correlated with alterations in CaMKII and TH phosphorylation as well as DA-related mRNA and protein expression, suggesting a potential link with dopaminergic signaling pathways. These findings are correlative, and causal mechanisms cannot be inferred. Overall, this study provides experimental evidence supporting the behavioral relevance of Qiangzhi Fang and offers preliminary insight into its potential molecular effects. Further studies incorporating mechanistic, multi-neurotransmitter, and translational analyses are required to fully elucidate its therapeutic potential.
