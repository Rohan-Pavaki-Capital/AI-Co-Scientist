The objective of this study is to identify subgroups of pediatric CD who had differential responses to the infliximab treatment through trajectory cluster analysis of disease activity using data from electronic health records.

Methods

We conducted a retrospective study of 295 pediatric CD patients who had been treated with infliximab for a minimum of one year at the Center for Inflammatory Bowel Disease at The Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia between January 2010 and December 2017. The evolution of disease was described, and subgroups of patients were identified using trajectory analysis of longitudinal data of C-Reactive Protein (CRP). We compared patient characteristics, biomarker for disease activity, and long-term surgical outcomes across subgroups. Cox regression models were used to evaluate the added value of the subgroup classification to baseline phenotype and location in prediction of long-term surgical outcomes.

Results

We identified three subgroups of patients with differential relapse-and-remission profiles (n = 33, 65 and 197 from subgroup 1 to 3), which represented patients with a higher risk of infliximab non-response, with infliximab response but with occasional disease flares, and patients with long-term response. Patients with the best treatment response had a significantly lower frequency of complicated disease phenotypes (p = 0.01), including perianal involvement (p=0.05), lower baseline CRP (p < 0.01) and calprotectin (p = 0.01), and lowest risk of IBD-related gastrointestinal surgery within 10 years of starting treatment (p<0.01).

Conclusions

Readily available longitudinal data from electronic health records can be leveraged to provide deeper characterization of treatment response in pediatric CD.

SUMMARY

This study uses a trajectory analysis that incorporates modelling of longitudinal disease patterns and identification of subgroups to provide deeper characterization of pediatric Crohn disease. Subgroups identified are associated with, yet not fully explained by, baseline phenotype and treatment information.

Our study utilized longitudinal measures from the electronic health records to stratify pediatric Crohn disease patients who had been treated with infliximab by differential treatment response profiles based on patterns of disease evolution after the treatment. We found trajectories of well-controlled disease were associated with lower inflammatory burden as baseline and less complicating disease phenotypes, including less perianal involvement and lower disease activity at baseline. The identified classification improved risk prediction of long-term surgery comparing to models that only used baseline disease phenotype and location.

Pediatric Crohn disease (CD) patients have a heterogeneous clinical course and treatment response.1, 2 Infliximab can successfully induce and maintain remission in pediatric CD, but some children are non-responders and others develop secondary non-response. Classification of patients into subgroups based on their differential treatment response is critical for guiding treatment selection and improving disease management.

The Paris Classification categorizes pediatric CD patients based on age of diagnosis, location of disease along the gastrointestinal tract, and clinical behavior, such as stricturing and/or penetrating disease. The PIBD-classes criteria, which are the only validated classification of children with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), highlights isolated colonic CD as a distinct entity3. Phenotypic classifications are not static and may evolve over the disease and treatment course with the goal being to prevent the development of these complicated disease phenotypes 4–11. Despite many efforts on predicting the evolution to stricturing or penetrating complications, little has been done to characterize the patterns of treatment response over time. As most CD patients experience intermittent exacerbations of symptoms alternating with periods of quiescence, the longitudinal patterns of disease evolution after treatment contain a large amount of information on disease characteristics, which can be unique for individual patients. However, to study patterns of disease evolution is challenging in practice, requiring large longitudinal studies with a long follow up period and a substantial amount of chart review. In this study, we used a trajectory cluster analysis of data from electronic health records (EHR) to overcome the challenges. Specifically, we used trajectory analysis to describe the disease evolution, functional principal components analysis (FPCA) to quantify patterns of disease evolution, and, based on those patterns, cluster analysis to stratify pediatric CD patients after initiation of infliximab therapy.

MATERIALS AND METHODSStudy Design and Participants

We conducted a retrospective single-center study of 295 pediatric CD patients (younger than 21 years at diagnosis) who initiated treatment with infliximab and continued therapy for a minimum of one year at the Center for Inflammatory Bowel Disease at The Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP) between January 2010 and December 2017. Patients with ulcerative colitis were excluded. Participants needed to have a serum infliximab level drawn during the course of treatment. Demographics at treatment initiation and longitudinal data on disease activity, phenotype, pharmacological treatments, and surgical history within 10 years of treatment were extracted from EHR. The study was reviewed and exempted by the institutional review board at CHOP.

Trajectory Cluster Analysis of Disease Activity

The study outcome was longitudinal disease activity after the initiation of infliximab treatment based on laboratory test results of CRP. All eligible study participants were required to have at least two CRP measurements after the initiation of infliximab therapy to allow for estimation of disease evolution. We performed a two-step trajectory cluster analysis to identify unique patterns of disease evolution, and to classify patients to subgroups based on the patterns.

Trajectory Analysis of Disease Evolution

We estimated the trajectory of disease activity over 3.5 years after the initiation of infliximab therapy by smoothing over the longitudinal measures of CRP values via polynomial splines. Then we conducted functional principal components analysis (FPCA) to extract patterns of disease evolution. Like classical principal component analysis, the FPCA identifies a small number of trajectories (i.e. patterns) to explain the modes of heterogeneity among patients. Specifically, we extracted patterns that explained more than 80% of the overall variation in the study population. Together with the patterns of disease evolution, the FPCA also generated a set of scores that were associated with each pattern for each patient. These scores quantified how each patient’s observed disease trajectory was explained by the patterns of disease evolution identified in the population. That is, each patient’s observed disease trajectory can be viewed as a weighted sum of the patterns, patients with similar patient-specific scores had similar observed disease trajectory.

Cluster Analysis of Subgroup

We then conducted an unsupervised clustering analysis to classify patients, using the patient-specific scores obtained from the FPCA. Specifically, the Gaussian mixture model with an expectation-maximization algorithm was used for the classification, and the Bayesian information criteria was used to determine the optimal number of subgroups.

Sensitivity analyses were performed to evaluate the robustness of the results. First, the analysis was re-estimated using different number of functional principal components to examine whether the number of subgroups changed. The model fit was evaluated by using subsets of patients to see if the results were similar to clustering using the whole sample. Furthermore, we tested if using data from a smaller window of time affected the clustering results.

Association of disease trajectory with baseline characteristics and long-term surgical outcome

After the subgroups of study participates were identified, we examined the distributions of patient’s characteristics and long-term surgical outcomes across subgroups. We conducted Chi-squared test for categorical variables Kruskal-Wallis one-way analysis of variance for continuous variables to evaluate the associations between these variables and the subgroups. We also use Cox regression to evaluate if time-to-surgery is associated with our subgrouping beyond disease location and baseline phenotype.

All the statistical analyses were conducted using R version 3.6.1.

RESULTSPatient Characteristics

Demographics at time of infliximab treatment initiation are summarized using mean ±SD for continuous variables and count with percentage for categorical variables in Table 1. By excluding patients who initiated infliximab but continued therapy for less than one year at the Center for Inflammatory Bowel Disease at CHOP between January 2010 and December 2017, we did not include 102 patients who met our other inclusion criteria. These excluded patients consist of 59 males (58%), 86 patients with inflammatory CD (84%), and 33 patients on combined therapy (32%). Those excluded have lab results that indicate less severe disorder, with a median baseline CRP of 1.8 (IQR 0.9 – 4.1, n = 102) and median baseline calprotectin of 298 (IQR 83 – 627, n = 59).

Trajectory Cluster Analysis of Disease Activity

The first three patterns of disease evolutions were identified which explain more than 85% of the variabilities in the data. Figure 1 displays the average disease trajectory in black lines and the three patterns in blue and red lines. On average, disease activity was high at the time when treatment was started, decreased quickly within the first 6 month to achieve remission, stayed low between 6 and 18 months and increased again beyond 18 months. The first and third patterns explained more than 63% of variability in the data. They showed that the majority of between patient difference was observed around 1 year and between 2 to 2.5 years, with some patients having frequent flares during these time intervals. The second pattern illustrated that about 24% of variations were explained by between patient differences within a year (particularly within 6 months) after treatment, with different slopes of decline in disease activity during the early stage of treatment.

All three patterns were used in the subsequent cluster analysis using Gaussian mixture model, which classify patients to three subgroups. The average disease trajectory of each subgroup and the raw CRP values were shown in Figure 2. Of the three subgroups, subgroup 1 (n=33) had the highest CRP prior to starting infliximab treatment and frequent spikes in the CRP throughout 3.5 years after treatment initiation. Subgroup 2 (n = 65) had a lower CRP at baseline, but with intermittent rises in the CRP within 1 year and beyond 2.5 years after treatment initiation. Subgroup 3 (n = 197) was the largest group and with the lowest inflammatory burden at baseline and with rare disease flares within 3.5 years after treatment initiation.

We compared patient characteristics, biomarker for disease activity, received treatments and long-term surgical outcomes across subgroup and the results are shown in Table 2. The three subgroups had significantly different disease phenotypes (p = 0.01, n = 295) including perianal phenotype (p = 0.05, n = 295), CRP (p < 0.01, n = 295) and calprotectin levels (p < 0.01, n = 140) at treatment initiation. There was less complicating disease behavior and an overall lower inflammatory burden at baseline, as measured by CRP and fecal calprotectin levels, from subgroup 1 through 3. The risk of IBD-related gastrointestinal (non-perirectal) surgery within 10 years after treatment was also significantly different between groups (p < 0.001), with decreasing percentage of surgery from subgroup 1 to 3. Group 1, with the highest baseline disease activity and more frequent disease flares had a shorter treatment duration (p = 0.04, n = 295). This group did have a higher proportion of females (p = 0.08, n = 295, 52% female). We did not find statistical significance in the proportion on concomitant immunomodulator therapy, median serum infliximab level, antibodies to infliximab, and disease duration when infliximab was initiated. . The subgroup classification was shown to substantially improve the prediction of surgery risk within 10 years of treatment initiation in a Cox regression model (p < 0.01, n = 295) comparing to a model that used only baseline disease phenotype and location as risk predictors.

Subgroup 1: Severely active CD patients with a high rate of infliximab non-response

Patients in this group had substantially insufficient response to infliximab therapy within 3.5 years of infliximab treatment, which led to a higher risk of surgery in 10 years. Potential contributing factors to the poor disease control included higher disease activity at baseline with more complicated disease phenotypes, including perirectal disease.

Subgroup 2: Moderately active CD patients with infliximab response but with intermittent disease flares

The majority of patients in this subgroup had an inflammatory disease phenotype, responded well to therapies, and maintained low disease activity within 3.5 years of infliximab treatment. Compared with subgroup 1, the frequency of disease flares was lower, mainly within 1 year and beyond 2.5 years after treatment initiation. The risk of surgery within 10 years was also substantially lower—less than one third of the rate in subgroup 1— suggesting adequate response to infliximab therapy and desired disease control over time.

Subgroup 3: mild-to moderately active CD patients with infliximab response and ongoing well-controlled disease

This is the largest group of patients who tended to have mild disease activity at the baseline, quick decline of disease activity after infliximab initiation, and very low disease activity over the 3.5 years after treatment. Patients in this group had predominately an inflammatory phenotype without perirectal involvement, as well as the lowest rate of IBD-related gastrointestinal surgery (17.0%) out of all the subgroups within 10 years.

Sensitivity analysis

To evaluate the robustness of our findings, we examined how the results changed as we adjusted the number of functional principal components extracted. We compared results using four and five functional principal components, which explain 95% and 98% of the overall variance, respectively. We found that clustering with more functional principal components yields more clusters, but the results also suffered from overfitting to a small number of patients with extreme values, which led to clusters that were difficult to interpret.

We also evaluated the model using an internal validation. We a randomly selected 80% of the patients (n = 236) to train the clustering algorithm and performed the classification on the other 20% (n =59) of patients. Classification results of the 59 patients based on the 80% of the data were compared to the results based on the whole sample. We repeated the procedure for 50 times and found that the mean adjusted Rand index was 0.90 and the mean accuracy was 95%, which indicated that the current clustering results were robust to the sample of patients used.

DISCUSSION

In this study, we extracted unique patterns of disease evolution and classified patients into three subgroups based on their trajectory of response to infliximab treatment. The subgroups were different in terms of complicated disease phenotypes at the time of infliximab initiation and in terms of risk of IBD-related surgery within 10 years of treatment. Trajectories of long-term well-controlled disease were associated with a lower inflammatory burden at baseline, absence of complicated disease phenotypes, including perirectal involvement, and lower risk of surgery within 10 years of infliximab treatment. Our findings suggest that penetrating, stricturing and perianal disease phenotypes are risk factors infliximab non-response. We also found the identified subgroup classification substantially improved the prediction of 10-year surgery risk comparing to models that used only baseline disease phenotype and location.

In our study, concomitant therapy with an immunomodulator was not associated with disease trajectory. Serum infliximab levels, antibodies against infliximab and age at diagnosis were all also not significantly associated with the classification based on disease trajectory. However, there was variation in the dose and frequency of infliximab therapy. Although every participant in the study underwent therapeutic drug monitoring given the retrospective nature of the study it is unclear how much of this testing was proactive versus reactive drug monitoring. Of note, this cohort included very few patients with very early onset inflammatory bowel disease (VEO-IBD), defined as age at diagnosis < 6 years. Patients with VEO-IBD tend to have more colonic involvement and often carry the diagnosis of IBD-unclassified rather than CD or ulcerative colitis. Therefore, many VEO-IBD patients did not meet the study criteria. Compared to older children with IBD, children with VEO-IBD are more likely to have a monogenic cause for their disease and are often refractory to conventional therapies. Therefore, VEO-IBD might be expected to have a distinct disease trajectory compared to the three presented in this study.

We showed that trajectory clustering methods can be applied to longitudinal health data to define subgroups of patients with different disease activity and response to treatment over time. This unsupervised learning approach was shown to naturally separate patients into groups that have distinct disease progression patterns. Our investigation represents a novel perspective leveraging the patterns of longitudinal disease trajectory using data that are routinely collected in health care practice. We found the subgroups differ substantially in treatment response, which could inform clinical practice. The identified subgroup classification was associated with baseline disease subtypes. However, the demographic and phenotypic information were not sufficient to explain the heterogeneity across subgroups. The identified classification provided added value to prediction of long-term surgical outcomes and explained disease variation that was unaccounted for by existing phenotype classifications.

Our study has several limitations. The EHR data, which was not primarily collected for research purposes, suffered from the limitation of non-random sampling (or informative missing). The number and timing of lab tests were likely tied to patients’ disease activity and frequency of check-ups, which implied that sicker patients likely had more representation in the data and the analysis can be biased toward sicker patients due to this informative missing. Currently, methods to address missing data in the FPCA is not well developed 12. In our analysis, we used the weighted FPCA to account for the differential number of observations across patients by adjusting the sample sizes of available pairs of time points for the covariance estimate in the FPCA12. This method assumes the missing is completely at random which may not be idea for the EHR data, but extended statistical tools still need to be developed to correct bias due to missing at random and missing not at random for FPCA. We also expected the problem of informative missing is minor in our analysis as pediatric CD patients tended to have closer and more regular follow-up after infliximab treatment, with CRP closely monitored at infusions. Furthermore, truncation of the CRP measurements was not handled by the statistical methods employed, so it is possible that the full spectrum of disease activity was not observed. Such issues with the data were not entirely accounted for in this study and suggest further avenues of methodological research. With the method used though, the clustering was entirely unsupervised and could not be readily compared to any existing subtyping methods. It is thus difficult to assess whether these subtypes are correctly dividing the patients, or if there are better classification criteria that more accurately reflect separate groups of patients. Another limitation is that we used CRP as a measure of disease activity rather than fecal calprotectin, which has been shown to be an accurate surrogate marker for mucosal healing in pediatric CD 13. Yet, in clinical practice CRP is assessed more often than fecal calprotectin and may be the first test to alert the clinician that a patient has ongoing disease activity. Previous studies have used CRP as the primary measure of disease activity 14, 15. An elevated CRP may prompt a clinician to order a fecal calprotectin. While we found that fecal calprotectin differed significantly across our subgroups, our study population had few fecal calprotectin measurements and those that were measured may be affected by sampling bias. Importantly, the calprotectin levels measured in our cohort did seem to correlate with CRP as both these measures were more significantly elevated at baseline in subgroup with inadequate response with infliximab treatment.

Future studies could expand on these findings to provide deeper understanding of these pediatric CD subgroups. Additional clinical data, such as calprotectin measurements, are more accurate measures for disease activity but more sparsely measured. Novel methods for clustering of multivariate longitudinal data are needed to utilize such sparse measurements since they are likely insufficient on their own. Our trajectory subtyping approach could also be applied to different groups of patients, such as those being treated with novel CD therapies such as vedolizumab or ustekinumab. Furthermore, follow-up studies could look into how to classify patients without the need for data over the full observation period.

-What is known?

Pediatric Crohn disease (CD) patients represent heterogeneous disease course and differential response to treatment.

To study patterns of disease evolution is challenging in practice, requiring large longitudinal studies.

-What is new?

We stratified pediatric CD patients who had been treated with infliximab for a minimum of one year with differential relapse-and-remission risk profiles based on patterns of disease evolution using data from the electronic health records (EHR).

Trajectories of well-controlled disease activity were predicted by lower inflammatory burden at baseline, less complicated disease phenotypes, including less perianal involvement, and associated with lower risk of surgery.

Readily available longitudinal EHR data can be leveraged to provide deeper characterization of pediatric CD.
