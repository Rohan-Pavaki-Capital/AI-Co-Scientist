Cognitive disengagement syndrome (CDS), formerly termed sluggish cognitive tempo, is a set of symptoms characterized by excessive daydreaming, mental confusion, and slowed behavior/thinking. CDS is distinct from symptoms of attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) and other psychopathologies and uniquely associated with functional impairment. However, despite significant progress in developing and evaluating rating scale measures of CDS, no clinical interview of CDS exists with established psychometric properties that can facilitate a multi-method approach assessing CDS. Accordingly, the present study evaluated the psychometric properties of the semi-structured Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome-Clinical Interview (CDS-CI). The CDS-CI and the ADHD inattention (ADHD-IN) and hyperactive-impulsivity (ADHD-HI) modules from the Kiddie Schedule of Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia for School-Age Children (K-SADS) were administered to a sample of early adolescents (N = 341, ages 10-12 years) and their caregivers. Adolescents and caregivers also completed rating scale measures of CDS and ADHD symptoms. The CDS-CI demonstrated high internal consistency and inter-rater reliability. CDS-CI scores showed excellent same-source discriminant validity from K-SADS ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI scores and encouraging convergent and discriminant validity with rating scale measures, especially for caregivers. Above and beyond K-SADS ADHD-IN and ADHD-IN scores, higher parent and adolescent CDS-CI scores were both significantly associated with greater functional impairment. A threshold of ≥ 6 out of 15 symptoms was selected as an initial threshold for determining clinically-elevated CDS using the CDS-CI. Findings provide psychometric support for the CDS-CI as a tool in the multi-method assessment of CDS.

A growing number of studies demonstrate that cognitive disengagement syndrome (CDS) – a constellation of symptoms characterized by daydreaming, mental fogginess, drowsiness, and slowed behavior/thinking – is not only separable from symptoms of attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), depression/anxiety, and daytime sleepiness but also independently associated with a range of functional impairments (Becker et al., 2016). This set of symptoms was previously termed “sluggish cognitive tempo” (SCT) but a recent international Work Group recommended a change in terminology from SCT to CDS given (1) concerns about the potentially offensive and derogatory nature of the SCT term, and (2) to more accurately reflect the current state of the science (for a discussion, see Becker et al., 2023). The Work Group also concluded that CDS symptoms are associated with increased social problems and withdrawal/isolation, internalizing psychopathology (especially depression), and sleep difficulties, with indication that CDS may also be associated with more academic difficulties, emotion dysregulation, and suicide risk (Becker et al., 2023). Importantly, emerging research supports the longitudinal/predictive, cross-setting, and transcultural validity of the CDS construct (Becker, 2019; Becker et al., 2023; Sonuga-Barke et al., 2023). Given these findings, there is growing consensus that CDS is a construct of transdiagnostic importance, or perhaps a distinct psychiatric disorder (Barkley, 2014; Becker & Barkley, 2018; Becker et al., 2023; Kaçmaz et al., 2024).

As CDS research has advanced, one area of primary importance has been related to the measurement and assessment of CDS (Becker, 2021). Initially assessed with just two or three items (Frick et al., 1994; McBurnett, Pfiffner, & Frick, 2001), there has been substantial work in the past decade to develop and validate more comprehensive rating scale measures of CDS (Becker, 2021). While important, these rating scales represent only one method of assessment, and clinical interviews have long been considered valuable as part of a multi-method assessment of psychopathology. In particular, clinical interviews provide a comprehensive and systematic evaluation of symptom onset, presence, pervasiveness, and impairment (Ambrosini, 2000; Kaufman et al., 1997; Leffler, Riebel, & Hughes, 2015; Shaffer et al., 2000), and can also leverage clinical expertise in assessing and ensuring that symptoms are not better explained by another psychopathology (American Psychiatric Association, 1994; Kaufman et al., 1997). Accordingly, a clinical interview is needed to provide researchers and clinicians with a multi-method approach for assessing CDS. The Kiddie Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia for School-Age Children (K-SADS; Kaufman et al., 2013) is a well-established semi-structured diagnostic interview for assessing psychopathology in children and adolescents. An initial clinical interview for assessing CDS was created following the K-SADS format (McBurnett, 2010), though without published psychometric properties. Further, given major advancements in understanding the internal structure of CDS over the past decade, this interview was revised to include 15 CDS items currently identified as optimal for assessing the CDS construct (Becker, 2021; Becker et al., 2023). The current study evaluates the initial psychometric properties of the Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome – Clinical Interview (CDS-CI), a semi-structured clinical interview for assessing CDS in youth.

Study Objectives

The purpose of this study was to evaluate the reliability and validity of the scores from the parent and adolescent versions of the CDS-CI in a large sample of early adolescents (N = 341, ages 10-12 years). This is an important developmental period for assessing CDS as there is some indication that CDS symptoms may increase in adolescence (Leopold et al., 2016). Given the study also included the well-established K-SADS for assessing ADHD inattention (ADHD-IN) and hyperactivity-impulsivity (ADHD-HI) symptoms, the psychometric properties of the new parent and adolescent CDS-CI were also compared to the psychometric properties of the K-SADS ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI scores.

Objective 1: Reliability of CDS-CI scores

The first objective evaluated the reliability of CDS clinical interview scores. In order to be potentially useful measures of CDS, the parent and adolescent CDS clinical interview scores must demonstrate high levels of internal consistency (omega values > .90). The parent and adolescent CDS clinical interview scores must also demonstrate high inter-rater reliability (inter-rater correlations > than .90 when scored by a second a rater).

Objective 2: Convergent and discriminant validity of CDS-CI scores

The second objective used a multitrait (CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI) by multimethod (clinical interview and rating scale) by multisource (parents and adolescents) matrix with Pearson correlations to evaluate the convergent and discriminant validity of the CDS-CI scores (Campbell & Fiske, 1959; Eid, Koch, & Geiser, 2023). Figure 1 shows this matrix. We now describe the pattern of correlations within this matrix that are necessary to support the validity of the CDS scores from the parent and adolescent CDS-CI.

Same source-same method blocks.

These four blocks—the four blocks with the number 1 in the upper left corner (Figure 1)—involved the correlations of CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI within the (1) parent clinical interview, (2) parent rating scale, (3) adolescent clinical interview, and (4) adolescent self-report. Within source discriminant validity requires these correlations to be less than approximately .85 (Brown, 2015, p. 116; Kline, 2023, p. 56). Correlations less than approximately .85 are necessary to argue that CDS scores represent a distinct construct from ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI scores within the parent interview, within parent rating scale, within adolescent interview, and within adolescent self-report. Ideally such correlations should be much lower than .85.

Same source-different method blocks.

These two blocks involve the parent clinical interview with the parent rating scale correlations and the adolescent clinical interview with the adolescent self-report correlations. These two blocks have the number 2 in the upper left corner of each cell (Figure 1). These are the two most important validity blocks for the evaluation of the parent and adolescent CDS-CI scores. More specifically, the correlations within these two blocks provide the clearest evaluation of the convergent and discriminant validity of CDS-CI scores, given that the symptom information is collected from the same source with the two different methods (i.e., the interview and rating scale for parents and the interview and self-report measure for adolescents). We first define the ideal convergent and discriminant validity results for the correlations in these two blocks and then describe our expectations for these correlations.

Convergent validity ideally requires that the CDS with CDS, ADHD-IN with ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI with ADHD-HI correlations are large to substantial (e.g., .60 to .80; Brown, 2015, p. 188). Discriminant validity requires each convergent correlation to be significantly larger than its four discriminant correlations (the two in its row and the two in its column, Figure 1). For example, discriminant validity for the parent CDS scores requires that the parent CDS interview with parent CDS rating scale convergent correlation is significantly larger than the (1) parent CDS interview with parent ADHD-IN rating scale, (2) parent CDS interview with parent ADHD-HI rating scale, (3) parent CDS rating scale with parent ADHD-IN interview, and (4) parent CDS rating scale with parent ADHD-HI interview discriminant correlations.

We expected the parent interview with the parent rating scale block to provide strongest support for the convergent and discriminant validity of the parent CDS interview scores. We were less certain if the adolescent interview with the adolescent self-report block would yield as strong convergent and discriminant validity for adolescent CSD interview scores. This expectation was due to the much stronger validity for the parent CDS rating scale scores from earlier studies than for the adolescent CDS self-report scores (Becker, 2021).

Different source-same method blocks.

These two blocks involve the parent clinical interview with the adolescent clinical interview correlations and the parent rating scale with the adolescent self-report scale correlations. These two blocks have the number 3 in the upper left corner of the cell (Figure 1). Although the requirements for convergent and discriminant validity are the same for the correlations within these two blocks, we expected the convergent and discriminant validity results to be weaker for these analyses than the same source-different method analyses. The reason is that these analyses are across different sources for the same method while the previous analyses are across different methods for the same source (De Los Reyes et al., 2023).

Different source-different method blocks.

These two blocks involve the parent rating scale with the adolescent clinical interview correlations and the parent clinical interview with the adolescent self-report scale correlations. These two validity blocks have the number 4 in the upper left corner of the cell (Figure 1). Although the requirements for convergent and discriminant validity also remain the same for the correlations within these two blocks, we expected the weakest convergent and discriminant validity for these analyses. This expectation is due to the comparisons being across different sources and different methods (De Los Reyes et al., 2023).

Objective 3: Unique associations of CDS, ADHD-IN, ADHD-HI clinical interview scores with functional impairment

The third objective first determined the correlations of parent and adolescent CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI clinical interview scores with parent and adolescent global functional impairment scores from the BASC-3. It was expected that higher CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI interview scores would be associated with higher levels of global functional impairment. The second aspect of this objective, the more important question, determined if CDS interview scores still predicted higher levels of global functional impairment after controlling for ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI interview scores. We also evaluated the across source associations (parent interview CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI scores to adolescent functional impairment scores and adolescent interview CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI scores to parent functional impairment scores). We did not expect these across source first order and unique associations to be as strong as the within source.

Objective 4: Identifying a CDS symptom threshold for establishing clinically-elevated CDS

The fourth objective determined the percentage of adolescents who score at or above the various CDS symptom counts. This analysis also determined the level of impairment associated with different levels of CDS symptom occurrence. Our goal was to determine the CDS symptom threshold and the impairment threshold consistent with disordered behavior.

MethodsTransparency and Openness

This study’s design and its analysis were not preregistered. All data, analysis code, and research materials are available upon request from the corresponding author.

Participants

Participants were 341 early adolescents (ages 10-12 years). Characteristics of the adolescent participants and their caregiver/family are summarized in Table 1. There were approximately an equal number of female and male adolescents. Approximately half of the sample met diagnostic criteria for ADHD based on the K-SADS conducted with the adolescent’s caregiver (primarily biological mothers).

In addition to being in the 10- to 12-year-old age range, inclusion criteria included a standardized score ≥ 80 for overall intelligence on the Peabody Picture Vocabulary Test, 5th Edition (Dunn, 2019), willingness to discontinue stimulant medications for ADHD 24 hours prior to their research visit(s), and sufficient English language ability to complete the measures. In addition, children were excluded if the caregiver reported a previous diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder, bipolar disorder, or psychosis, or a significant visual, hearing or speech impairment precluding their ability to complete the measures.

Procedures

All procedures were approved by the Cincinnati Children’s Hospital Medical Center Institutional Review Board. Participants were recruited from a variety of sources, including media advertisements (e.g., television, Facebook, Instagram, Nextdoor), community flyers, e-mail distribution within a Midwestern children’s hospital, and letters to school and pediatrician partners. To assure a range of CDS symptomatology, several versions of advertising materials were generated (e.g., some specifying daydreaming, mental confusion, and slowed behavior/thinking, and some not targeting any attentional problems).

Interested caregivers completed a brief REDCap survey that included initial inclusion criteria (e.g., child’s age). Families meeting initial inclusion criteria were scheduled for an in-person research visit. After caregivers and adolescents provided informed consent and assent, respectively, study measures were collected. The CDS-CI and K-SADS were administered by trained graduate students, post-doctoral fellows, or clinical psychologists to caregivers and adolescents separately. Note that participants were offered the option to complete the measures at two separate visits. Participants taking stimulant medication for ADHD were instructed to withhold medication on the day of their research visit. This study’s design and its analysis were not preregistered. All data, analysis code, and research materials are available upon request from the corresponding author.

MeasuresDemographic Characteristics and Medication Use

Caregivers completed a demographic form to gather the information reported in Table 1. Medication use and psychosocial treatment was assessed with an adaptation of the Services Use in Children and Adolescents - Parent Interview (SCA-PI; Eaton Hoagwood et al., 2004).

CDS and ADHD Clinical InterviewsCognitive Disengagement Syndrome – Clinical Interview (CDS-CI).

The CDS-CI is a semi-structured diagnostic interview that assesses the presence and severity of CDS symptoms and was based on the Kiddie-Sluggish Cognitive Tempo Diagnostic Interview Module for Children and Adolescents (McBurnett, 2010). The CDS-CI consists of 15 items (e.g., “Does your child daydream a lot?,” “Does your child often get confused?,” “Is your child often sluggish or slowed down?”) in a format based on the K-SADS. Each item also includes additional follow-up probes that clinicians can use and adjust as needed to the developmental level of the child, the responses and language used by the interviewee, and the clinical and contextual information obtained.

Responses for each item were scored by the interviewer based on the frequency and associated functional impairment of the symptom in the past six months on a four-point scale (0 = no information, 1 = not present, 2 = subthreshold, 3 = threshold). In the present study, no items received a score of 0 (no information), as this response is only intended to be selected in rare instances when information is not available to provide sufficient information to rate (e.g., interviewing a caregiver regarding a foster child who just came into their care). Given this, any “no information” items should be treated as missing data (rather than as a “0” that contributes to a symptom mean or count score), and so the range of scores for the CDS-CI is 1 to 31. A symptom was scored as “subthreshold” if it occurred occasionally and had minimal to no impact on functioning, whereas a “threshold” score was indicated if the symptom occurred often or more frequently and negatively impacted functioning. Additionally, items for duration (coded yes/no for at least some symptom being present for at least 6 months), age of onset, and presence of domain-specific impairment (i.e., peers, school/academics, and family/home) were administered if the parent and/or adolescent endorsed the presence of a symptom occurring at the subthreshold or threshold level. The reliability of the CDS-CI has not previously been examined and is reported in the Results section. The CDS-CI is available to download online (Becker et al., 2024) and from the corresponding author.

Kiddie Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia for School-Age Children (K-SADS).

The K-SADS (Kaufman et al., 2013) is a semi-structured diagnostic interview based on the DSM-5 with good reliability and validity (Jarbin et al., 2017). The ADHD, conduct disorder (CD), generalized anxiety disorder, social phobia, panic disorder, post-traumatic stress disorder, major depression, and persistent depressive disorder modules of the K-SADS were administered to parents and adolescents separately. The oppositional defiant disorder (ODD) module was administered to parents only. The standard K-SADS format uses screening items and then supplemental items with remaining diagnostic criteria are asked based on the presence of screening symptoms. This format was used for all disorders in the present study except for ADHD, ODD, and CD. For ADHD, ODD, and CD, all items were asked to all participants regardless of whether any screening items were endorsed; thus, all participants were asked all 18 ADHD symptom items (in addition to all 8 ODD items and 15 CD items which are not examined in this study). Responses for each item were scored by the interviewer using the same four-point scale as described above for the CDS-CI. As with the CDS-CI, no items received a score of 0 (no information), and the range of scores for ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI in the current study was 1 to 3. Rating scale data support the concurrent validity of screens and K-SADS diagnoses (Kaufman et al., 1997). Inter-rater agreement in scoring screens and diagnoses is high (range: 93% to 100%) (Kaufman et al., 1997). For the current study, the reliability (omega for categorical indicators, see Flora, 2020) values for the parent interview ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI scores were .97 and .94, respectively. The omega values for the adolescent interview ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI scores were .94 and .91, respectively.

CDS, ADHD-IN, ADHD-HI, and Functional Impairment Rating ScalesChild and Adolescent Behavior Inventory (CABI).

Caregivers completed the 15-item CDS, 9-item ADHD-IN, and 9-item ADHD-HI scales from the CABI (Burns et al., 1987-2021). Each item was rated on a six-point scale for the past month (0 = almost never [never or about once per month], 1 = seldom [about once per week], 2 = sometimes [several times per week], 3 = often [above once per day], 4 = very often [several times per day], 5 = almost always [many times per day]). Scores on the 15 CABI CDS items (e.g., “gets lost in own thoughts,” “easily confused,” “daydreams”, “low level of activity”) have demonstrated strong structural validity (including discriminant validity from ADHD and other psychopathology symptoms including depression, anxiety, and oppositionality), excellent reliability (internal consistency, test-retest, inter-rater), invariance (across a one-month interval, sex of rater, community/clinical samples), and independent correlates relative to ADHD symptoms (e.g., Başay et al., 2021; Burns & Becker, 2021, 2022; Burns et al., 2023; Sadeghi-Bahmani et al., 2022; Sáez, Servera, Becker, & Burns, 2019; Servera et al., 2018; for a review, see Becker, 2021). Reliability (omega) for the current sample of participants for the CABI CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI scores were .97, .98, and .96, respectively. Mean CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI scores were used in the analyses in the current study.

Child Concentration Inventory, Second Edition (CCI-2).

Child self-report of CDS was measured using the CCI-2 (Becker, 2015). The CCI-2 items parallel the 15 items of the CABI parent-reported CDS scale (e.g., “I get lost in my own thoughts,” “I feel confused”). Items are rated on a four-point scale (0 = never to 3 = always), with higher scores indicating a higher frequency of CDS symptoms. Previous research indicates moderate correlations with parent-reported and teacher-reported CDS (rs = .29-.36) (Sáez, Servera, Burns, & Becker, 2019). Reliability (omega) was .91 for the CCI-2 scores in this study. A mean CCI-2 score was used in the analyses.

Behavioral Assessment System for Children, Third Edition (BASC-3).

The BASC-3 (Reynolds & Kamphaus, 2015) was completed by caregivers and children. The BASC-3 is a reliable and well-validated multidimensional measure to evaluate emotional and behavioral functioning in children. As in other studies examining ADHD symptom dimensions (Barden et al., 2023; Childs, Kaufman, & Olezeski, 2022), the BASC-3 Self-Report of Personality (SRP) Attention Problems and Hyperactivity clinical scales were used to have self-report measures of ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI. The BASC-3 parent and adolescent self-report scales of global functional impairment were used as our measures of impairment.

Results

SPSS (version 29) was used for descriptive information. R (Lavaan version 0.6-17) was used to calculate reliability coefficients (omega for categorical indicators; Flora, 2020) (Objective 1). The Mplus statistical software (version 8.8) was used for analyses examining the convergent and discriminant validity of the CDS interview scores, with the model constraint procedure used to test for differences between convergent and discriminant correlations (Objective 2). Specifically, the Mplus robust maximum likelihood estimator (MLR) was used for these analyses (see Raykov & Marcoulides, 2011, pp. 212-221, for an application of the Mplus software code to a multitrait by multimethod matrix with Pearson correlations). The Mplus software was also used to determine the correlations of the interview score as well as their unique associations with the global measure of functional impairment for the regression of the measures of functional impairment on the interview scores (Objective 3). Finally, SPSS was used for analyses to identify a CDS symptom threshold for establishing clinically-elevated CDS (Objective 4).

Descriptive Information and Reliability of CDS-CI Scores

For both parents and adolescents, the mean CDS symptom score was approximately halfway between the “not present” and “subthreshold” codes (parents: M = 1.45, SD = .06, range = 1 to 3; adolescents: M = 1.60, SD = .06, range = 1 to 3). Also based on both parents and adolescents, the average age of onset for any CDS symptom presence was in first/second grade (parents: M = 6.50 years, SD = 2.54; adolescents: M = 7.95 years, SD = 2.23). Parents and adolescents reported comparable lower rates (14.9% and 11.4%, respectively) of CDS-related peer impairment and higher rates (40.6% and 40.1%, respectively) of CDS-related school impairment. In contrast, the percentage with CDS-related family/home impairment was much higher based on the parent interview (44.2%) than the adolescent interview (27.8%). Reliability (omega) for parent and adolescence CDS symptom scores were .94 and .93, respectively, with the CDS impairment scores having omega values of .85 for parent and adolescent interviews. Inter-rater reliability was assessed with a second scoring of audio-recorded interviews, for feasibility reasons 20% of the parent and 20% of the adolescent interviews (n = 69) were randomly selected for inter-rater scoring. The inter-rater correlations were .99 for both parent and adolescent interviews. The mean scores between the original and the second independent scoring of the interviews also did not differ significantly.

Convergent and Discriminant Validity of CDS Interview Scores

Table 2 shows the Pearson correlations from the multitrait (CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI) by multimethod (clinical interview and rating scale) by multisource (parent and adolescent) matrix. We now summarize these results to evaluate the convergent and discriminant validity of the CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI scores with a focus on the new CDS interview measure.

Same source-same method blocks

For the CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI correlations within the parent clinical interview, within parent rating scale, within adolescent clinical interview, and within adolescent self-report, all the within source correlations showed discriminant validity (range = .34 to .77). For the parent interview, the correlations of CDS with ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI were .60 (SE = .03) and .34 (SE = .05), respectively. For the adolescent interview, the correlations were .66 (SE = .03) and .54 (SE = .04), respectively. The new CDS-CI scores thus showed good discriminant validity from ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI interview scores for the parents and adolescents.

Same source-different method blocks

For the parent clinical interview with the parent rating scale, the CDS, ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI convergent correlations were significant (ps < .001) and substantial (CDS = .85, SE = .02; ADHD-IN = .86, SE = .01; ADHD-HI = .88, SE = .01) with each convergent correlation being significantly larger than its four respective discriminant correlations (two in the row and two in the column for each convergent correlation). These results provided strong support for the convergent and discriminant validity of the parent CDS interview scores with the validity of the CDS interview scores similar to the validity of the ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI interview scores.

For the adolescent interview with the adolescent self-report scales, the three convergent correlations were significant (ps < .001) and large to substantial (CDS = .64, SE = .03; ADHD-IN = .75, SE = .02; ADHD-HI = .72, SE = .03), with the ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI convergent correlations being significantly (ps < .05) larger than their four respective discriminant correlations. The CDS convergent correlation was significantly (ps < .004) larger than its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-HI. However, the CDS convergent correlation was not significantly (ps >.10) larger than its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-IN. CDS scores showed weaker convergent validity than ADHD-IN scores (i.e., .64 vs. .75, respectively).

Different source-same method blocks

For the parent interview with the adolescent interview, the three convergent correlations were significant (ps < .001) and large to substantial (CDS = .50, SE = .05; ADHD-IN = .75, SE = .03; and ADHD-HI = .69, SE = .03). The ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI convergent correlations were significantly larger (ps < .004) than their four respective discriminant correlations. The CDS convergent correlation was significantly (ps < .007) larger than its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-HI. However, the CDS convergent correlation was not significantly (ps >.10) larger than its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-IN. CDS scores again showed weaker convergent validity than ADHD-IN scores (i.e., .50 vs. .75, respectively).

For the parent rating scale with the adolescent self-report scales, the three convergent correlations were significant (ps < .001) and medium to moderate (CDS = .38, SE = .05; ADHD-IN = .49, SE = .04; and ADHD-HI = .44, SE = .04). The ADHD-IN convergent correlation was significantly (ps < .007) larger than its four discriminant correlations, with the ADHD-HI convergent correlation being significantly (ps < .008) larger that three of its four discriminant correlations. The CDS convergent correlation was significantly (p < .001) larger than one of its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-HI. The CDS convergent correlation did not differ significantly (ps > .10) from its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-IN (CDS scores again showed weaker convergent validity than the ADHD-IN scores, .38 vs. .49, respectively).

Different source-different method blocks

For the parent rating scale with the adolescent interview, the three convergent correlations were significant (ps < .001) and large (CDS = .47, SE = .05; ADHD-IN = .66, SE = .03; and ADHD-HI = .64, SE = .03). The convergent correlations for ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI were significantly (ps < .001) larger than their four respective discriminant correlations. The CDS convergent correlation was significantly (ps < .005) larger than its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-HI. However, the CDS convergent correlation was not significantly (ps > .10) larger than its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-IN (CDS scores again showed weaker convergent validity than the ADHD-IN scores, .47 vs. .66, respectively).

For the parent interview with the adolescent self-report scales, the three convergent correlations were significant (ps < .001) and medium to large (CDS = .34, SE = .05; ADHD-IN = .52, SE = .04; ADHD-HI = .47, SE = .04). The ADHD-IN convergent correlation was significantly (ps < .003) larger than its four discriminant correlations. The ADHD-HI convergent correlation was significantly (ps < .001) larger than its two discriminant correlations with CDS and significantly (p < .05) larger than one of its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-IN. The CDS convergent correlation was significantly larger (p < .003) than one of its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-HI and not significantly (ps > .10) larger than its two discriminant correlations with ADHD-IN (CDS scores again showed weaker convergent validity than the ADHD-IN scores, .34 vs. .52, respectively).

Associations of CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI Interview Scores with Global Functional Impairment

The correlations of the parent CDS-CI, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI interview scores with the parent BASC-3 global functional impairment scale T-scores were .61 (SE = .03), .72 (SE = .03), and .55 (SE = .04), respectively (ps < .001). The partial standardized regression coefficients for the association of parent CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI interview scores with the impairment scores were .30 (SE = .04), .44 (SE = .05), and .15 (SE = .05), respectively (ps < .001). Higher parent CDS-CI scores were thus associated with higher global functional impairment, even after controlling for parent K-SADS ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI clinical interview scores.

The correlations of adolescent CDS-CI, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI interview scores with adolescent BASC-3 global functional impairment were .60 (SE = .03), .68 (SE = .03), and .60 (SE = .04), respectively (ps < .01). The partial standardized regression coefficients for the association of adolescent CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI interview scores with adolescent global functional impairment were .26 (SE = .06), .36 (SE = .07), and .18 (SE = .07), respectively (ps < .001). Higher adolescent CDS-CI scores were thus associated with higher global functional impairment, even after controlling for adolescent K-SADS ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI clinical interview scores.

We also examined the across source associations for the CDS-CI, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI interview scores with BASC-3 global functional impairment scores. The correlations of parent CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI interview scores with adolescent self-reported functional impairment were .30 (SE = .05), .46 (SE = .04), and .36 (SE = .05), respectively (ps < .001). For adolescent interview to parent-rated functional impairment, the correlations were .36 (SE = .05), .50 (SE = .04), and .40 (SE = .04), respectively (ps < .001). Although higher scores on the parent and adolescent clinical interview measures predicted higher scores for a different source on functional impairment, only ADHD-IN interview scores uniquely predicted higher levels of functional impairment for the different source after controlling for CDS and ADHD-HI (i.e., parent interview to adolescent functional impairment: partial standardized regression coefficient = .37, SE = .08, p < .001; adolescent interview to parent functional impairment: partial standardized regression coefficient = .43, SE = .08, p < .001)2.

Identifying a CDS Symptom Threshold for Establishing Clinically-Elevated CDS

Currently there are no criteria for identifying clinically-elevated CDS (e.g., number of symptoms and impairment) similar to DSM-5 diagnostic criteria. To provide initial information on the number of CDS symptoms to identify children with clinically-elevated and impairing behavior due to CDS symptom occurrence, Table 3 (parent CDS interview) and Table 4 (adolescent CDS interview) show the percent of the sample with 0 CDS symptoms, 1 or more, 2 or more, and so on to 10 more CDS symptoms along with the impairment associated with each level of symptom occurrence (see Barkley, 2012 for a similar procedure with adult self-report ratings of CDS symptoms).

Impairment was defined with two different measures. The first involved the three impairment questions asked during the CDS interview (CDS-related peer impairment, academic impairment, and home/family impairment). A threshold of a positive response on at least two of these three areas was used to determine the presence of CDS-related impairment. The second measure of impairment was a T-score > 65 on the BASC-3 global functional impairment scale. The tables show the mean impairment associated with each level of CDS symptom occurrence as well as the percentage of the sample that was impaired for each level of CDS symptom occurrence.

Six or more CDS symptoms identified 15.0% (n = 51) and 15.3% (n = 52) of the sample based on the parent and adolescent CDS interviews, respectively. For the parent interview, 86.3% of the 51 children (n = 44) with six or more CDS symptoms also met the impairment criteria of two of three areas of impairment due to CDS symptom occurrence. For the adolescent interview, 69.2% of the 52 (n = 36) with six or more CDS symptoms also met the impairment criteria of two of three areas. Thus, the percent of children that met the criteria of 6 or more symptoms and impairment in two or more areas was 12.9% and 10.56% of the sample for the parent and adolescent CDS interviews, respectively. In addition, approximately 50% of the children with six or more CDS on the parent and adolescent CDS interviews had BASC-3 global functional impairment T-scores > 65.

Discussion

There has been substantial progress over the past two decades in understanding CDS in children and adolescents, including the development of numerous CDS-specific rating scales with strong psychometric properties (Becker, 2021; Becker et al., 2023). However, there has yet to be a clinical interview with published psychometric support available for researchers and clinicians interested in using interview methods for assessing CDS in children and adolescents. Clinical interviews can be particularly important for assessing the developmental course, pervasiveness, and severity of symptoms as well as their associated impact on functioning, ensuring that symptoms are not better accounted for by another psychopathology and providing a richer clinical picture of the nature and lived experience of the symptom (Kaufman et al., 1997; Leffler, Riebel, & Hughes, 2015; Shaffer et al., 2000). Semi-structured interviews can be especially informative as they use a flexible yet systematic approach that incorporates the interviewer’s clinical expertise (Ambrosini, 2000; Leffler, Riebel, & Hughes, 2015). Accordingly, the current study provides the first psychometric support for the CDS-CI, a semi-structured interview based on the format of the frequently-used K-SADS that can be administered to youth and their caregivers.

Our first objective was to evaluate the reliability of the parent and adolescent versions of the CDS-CI. As expected, scores on both versions of the CDS-CI contained a large amount of true score variance (less than 8% measurement error) as well as high levels of inter-rater reliability (inter-rater correlations of .99 with no mean differences in CDS scores across raters). This information indicates CDS-CI scores are internally consistent and that different raters listening to recordings of the same clinical interview arrive at very similar conclusions regarding how the information obtained should be scored. Although future studies will be needed to evaluate other types of reliability (e.g., test-retest), the strong support for internal consistency and inter-rater reliability in both the parent and adolescent versions of the CDS-CI provides an important foundation for examining validity of CDS-CI scores.

Our second objective involved the use of a multitrait (CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI) by multimethod (clinical interview and rating scale) by multisource (parents and adolescents) matrix to determine the validity of CDS-CI scores. We first focus on the aspects of this matrix most relevant to our evaluation of the new CDS-CI (i.e., same source-same method and same source-different method correlations). We then briefly comment on the other blocks of the matrix (different source-same method and different source-different method correlations).

For the same source-same method correlations, parent CDS-CI scores showed excellent discriminant validity from parent ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI interview scores. Similarly, adolescent CDS interview scores showed excellent discriminant validity from adolescent ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI interview scores. Thus, within the parent and within adolescent interviews, CDS represented a different construct from ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI which is consistent with an established body of research based on rating scale data (Becker et al., 2016; Becker et al., 2023).

For the parent interview with parent rating scale correlations (same source-different method), the convergent correlation for CDS scores was substantial (.85) with CDS scores also showing significant discriminant validity with ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI scores. In addition, the validity results for CDS scores were as strong as the results for ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI scores. The parent interview with parent rating scale correlations thus provided excellent support for the convergent and discriminant validity of CDS-CI interview scores.

The adolescent interview with adolescent self-report correlations provided mixed support for the validity of CDS scores. Although the convergent correlations for CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI were large (CDS = .64) to substantial (ADHD-IN = .75), and CDS showed discriminant validity with ADHD-HI, CDS did not show discriminant validity with ADHD-IN (ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI showed discriminant validity with each other as well as CDS). If the convergent correlation for CDS had been as large as the convergent correlation for ADHD-IN for the adolescent interview with the adolescent self-report, CDS would have shown discriminant validity with ADHD-IN. This same pattern of results occurred in the four other validity analyses (i.e., [1] parent interview with adolescent interview correlations, [2] parent rating scale with adolescent self-report correlations, [3] parent rating scale with adolescent interview correlations, and [4] parent interview with adolescent self-report).

The weaker convergent and discriminant validity of the adolescent CDS-CI scores is not especially surprising, as the literature examining the discriminant validity of CDS from ADHD-IN is both larger and more consistent with parent (and teacher) ratings than with self-report ratings in either youth or adults (Becker, 2021). In fact, the parent rating scale of CDS used in this study is the most-frequently used measure of CDS, and scores on this CDS rating scale showed stronger convergent validity with scores on the adolescent CDS interview (.47) than with scores on the adolescent CDS self-report (.34) in our study. Our findings with the adolescent CDS interview are thus in line with the broader literature and further indicate the need for additional work in the assessment of CDS with adolescent clinical interview and, perhaps even more so, with adolescent self-report ratings. Historically, self-report measures of CDS were developed based on earlier parent and teacher items, and so it may be important to revisit the CDS item set to identify the nature and wording of items that may be optimal for assessing CDS with interview and self-report across the life span. Quantitative, qualitative, and mixed methods approaches would likely be useful toward this goal (Becker et al., 2022). Ideally, more careful development of content of the adolescent CDS interview and CDS self-report rating scale measures should result in a convergent correlation equal to the ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI convergent correlations.

This study also provides initial support for the external validity of the parent and adolescent CDS-CI. Both parent and adolescent scores on the CDS-CI were strongly associated with BASC-3 functional impairment. Furthermore, CDS-CI scores remained uniquely associated with functional impairment above and beyond K-SADS ADHD-IN and ADHD-HI scores. When across-source associations were examined, scores on the parent-reported CDS-CI were significantly correlated with adolescent-reported BASC-3 functional impairment, and vice versa. However, in regression models that examined across-source associations of CDS, ADHD-IN, and ADHD-HI symptoms in relation to other-informant functional impairment, only ADHD-IN symptoms were uniquely associated with functional impairment after controlling for the other two psychopathology dimensions. These findings are consistent with other studies that have found CDS symptoms to explain unique variance in functional impairment, though perhaps less strongly so than ADHD-IN symptoms (Barkley, 2013; Sáez, Servera, Becker, & Burns, 2019).

Finally, we aimed to identify a threshold for identifying clinically-elevated CDS based on symptoms endorsed on the CDS-CI and the presence of associated functional impairment. Of note, as CDS is not a recognized psychiatric disorder and there are currently no criteria for defining the clinical presence or absence of CDS (Becker et al., 2023), we sought to identify a threshold representing clinically significant and functionally impairing symptoms. This may be useful clinically (e.g., warranting further evaluation, tracking intervention response) or for research purposes (e.g., classification of participants, identifying participants for additional research procedures, aiming to understand the qualitative and lived experience of CDS symptoms). Given the stronger evidence for the parent CDS-CI than the adolescent CDS-CI, we prioritized the parent interview in determining a potentially useful symptom threshold. The presence of six or more symptoms was selected as an initial threshold for determining clinically-elevated CDS using the CDS-CI. In this sample, 15.0% and 15.3% of the sample had a threshold of ≥ 6 CDS symptoms based on the parent and adolescent interviews, respectively. Although this prevalence may initially appear to be too high, it is important to recall that the sample used in the present study included an overrepresentation of adolescents with attention problems (including approximately half meeting DSM-5 criteria for ADHD). CDS is estimated to occur in approximately 5-6% of the population, and approximately 27-45% of youth with ADHD have clinically-elevated CDS symptoms (Barkley, 2013; Burns & Becker, 2021; Burns et al., 2024; Servera et al., 2018). By extension to the current sample, a symptom count prevalence that identifies 15% of the sample is in line with what would be expected for a sample that is approximately split between youth with and without ADHD. In addition, among participants meeting this ≥6 symptom count threshold, the majority experience CDS-related functional impairment (86% and 69% based on the parent and adolescent CDS-CI, respectively), as well as high rates of global functional impairment on the (53% and 46% on the parent and adolescent BASC-3, respectively). As functional impairment can result from various sources, it is understandable that the rates of CDS-related functional impairment on the CDS-CI are higher than rates of functional impairment on the BASC-3. Future studies will be able to examine this symptom threshold in distinct and diverse samples, as well as evaluate its co-occurrence with psychiatric diagnoses, predictive validity with functional outcomes, and response to intervention.

Study Limitations and Constraints on Generality

Several limitations are important to note. First, this study included a sample of early adolescents (ages 10-12 years) and their caregivers, with approximately half of the sample meeting diagnostic criteria for ADHD. Findings for the CDS-CI cannot be assumed to generalize to younger children or older adolescents, nor to other clinical samples. Second, although our study used two methods for assessing CDS symptoms (rating scales and clinical interview), both methods are based on self-reported information and no procedures were used for possible response bias or atypical/random responding. Third, the only external validity examined in the current study was broad functional impairment, and it will be important to examine CDS-CI scores in relation to specific domains of functioning, including in the school context (e.g., Mayes et al., 2023). Fourth, only cross-sectional data were available in the present study, precluding determining any temporal associations. Longitudinal studies will be needed to examine the test-retest reliability as well as predictive validity of the CDS-CI. Finally, although our sample had an approximately equal number of female and male adolescents, as well as adolescents with diverse racial identities, they were part of families whose caregivers were highly educated (college degrees or higher) and reported higher family income (over $80,000/year). It will be important to examine the CDS-CI in more representative samples, with particular attention given to caregiver and youth understanding of the CDS-CI questions and any follow-up probes.

Conclusions

In conclusion, the current study provides the first psychometric support of a clinical interview for assessing CDS in youth. In a sample of young adolescents and their caregivers, the CDS-CI demonstrated strong reliability, encouraging convergent and discriminant validity with rating scale measures of CDS and ADHD symptoms, especially for caregivers, and preliminary support of external validity with functional impairment. The CDS-CI is thus a new tool in the multi-informant assessment of CDS.

Public Significance Statement:

Given major advancements in understanding cognitive disengagement syndrome (previously termed sluggish cognitive tempo) over the past decade, the current study provides initial support for the Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome – Clinical Interview (CDS-CI). The CDS-CI is a semi-structured clinical interview for assessing CDS in youth based on caregiver and/or youth report and offers a new tool in the multi-informant assessment of CDS.
