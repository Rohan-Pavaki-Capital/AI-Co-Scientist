Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) are a well‐established risk factor for a wide range of mental health conditions, including personality disorder (PD). Yet, few studies have explored the relationship between ACEs and dimensional models of PD using the ICD‐11 framework. The current study aimed to address this gap by establishing risk estimates and examining the potential dose‐response relationship between ACEs and PD severity. A total of 918 adults with a history of mental health treatment residing in the United States or United Kingdom completed self‐report questionnaires assessing ACE exposure and ICD‐11 PD severity. We found a positive relationship between ACE exposure and PD severity, where greater ACE exposure was associated with an increased risk of clinically significant personality dysfunction, and this relationship occurred largely in a dose–response manner. Given that individuals with four or more ACE exposures were most at risk of clinically significant PD, these findings highlight the need to investigate potential mechanisms underlying this vulnerability and the utility of targeted interventions to mitigate risk. Moreover, the ICD‐11 framework facilitated the examination of ACEs as a general risk factor relevant across PD presentations.

Introduction

Personality disorders (PDs) encompass enduring patterns of cognition, behaviour and affect that differ markedly from societal norms and persist across contexts (American Psychiatric Association [APA] 2022). Extensive research has focused on PD aetiology and treatment given their pervasive nature and significant impact on both the individual and the healthcare system (Volkert et al. 2018). In particular, adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) have been consistently implicated in the development and persistence of PDs (Afifi et al. 2011; Crișan et al. 2023). Nonetheless, prior research has typically been conducted using the traditional conceptualisation of PDs as 8–12 distinct categories (e.g., borderline PD and antisocial PD).

Growing concerns about the utility, validity, and heterogeneity of the categorical PD system prompted a dimensional reconceptualisation in the ICD‐11 (Hopwood et al. 2018; Tyrer et al. 2019). Within the ICD‐11 framework, a single PD diagnosis specified by severity of personality dysfunction (i.e., personality difficulty, mild PD, moderate PD or severe PD; World Health Organisation [WHO] 2022) replaces the 10 categorical PDs of the DSM‐5. PD severity considers impairments in self and interpersonal functioning, manifestations of personality disturbance across emotional, cognitive and behavioural domains, and the level of distress and impairment in personal, occupational, and social contexts (WHO 2022). Clinicians may further specify up to five maladaptive trait domains or a borderline pattern specifier; however, severity remains the sole diagnostic requirement and is the strongest predictor of prognosis and treatment needs (Bach and First 2018; Mulder and Tyrer 2019). Conceptually, ICD‐11 PD severity aligns with Criterion A of the hybrid categorical‐dimensional DSM‐5 Alternative Model of Personality Disorders (DSM‐5 AMPD; APA 2022), but extends this framework through its purely dimensional focus and greater emphasis on psychosocial impairment and observable manifestations of personality dysfunction. The present article focuses on ICD‐11 PD severity, reflecting its role as the key diagnostic component in the ICD‐11 PD classification system. Throughout this article, the term ‘dimensional’ is used to refer broadly to approaches such as the AMPD and ICD‐11, which conceptualise personality pathology on severity and trait continua rather than as discrete categories.

ACEs and PD

ACEs are associated with increased incidence of a wide range of mental health conditions. Indeed, an examination of world prevalence data ascertained that almost 30% of mental health conditions were preceded by adversity in childhood (Kessler et al. 2010). The relationship between ACEs and PD is well‐established; in a large cohort study, any type of childhood adversity was associated with a 3.8‐fold increased risk of any PD (Broekhof et al. 2024). Moreover, about 50% of individuals with PDs report general household dysfunction, and 35%–40% report physical neglect (Afifi et al. 2011). Beyond the effects of individual ACEs, research consistently demonstrates a dose–response relationship between ACEs and various health outcomes (Hughes et al. 2017; Morris and Hays‐Grudo 2023). This graded relationship suggests that the cumulative burden of childhood adversity exerts progressively greater effects on an individual's psychological functioning and distress (Morris and Hays‐Grudo 2023). Recent research indicates a relationship between cumulative trauma exposure and dimensional personality pathology in older adults (Stone et al. 2023), suggesting the potential presence of a dose–response relationship between ACEs and dimensional PD. Despite these indications, prior literature has predominantly examined relationships between specific ACEs (e.g., childhood sexual abuse) and specific categorical PDs (e.g., borderline PD; de Aquino Ferreira et al. 2018; Temes et al. 2020). Whether this dose–response pattern extends to ICD‐11 PD severity therefore remains relatively underexplored.

As such, emerging studies have focused on the association between childhood adversity and dimensional models of PD. Among a community sample, Borroni et al. (2019) found that childhood trauma predicted the majority of DSM‐5 AMPD maladaptive personality traits, though they did not consider Criterion A, the severity of personality dysfunction. Addressing this limitation, Lock et al. (2025) examined the temporal association between childhood trauma and ICD‐11 PD severity, finding significant associations with childhood sexual trauma and, to a lesser degree, recent sexual trauma. However, their small clinical sample (N = 75) restricts generalisability, particularly to broader community populations and to very specific types of ACEs. Moreover, to date no study has examined whether ICD‐11 PD severity exhibits a dose–response relationship with the number of ACEs experienced by an individual. Establishing such a relationship would clarify whether PD severity increases incrementally or reflects a threshold effect, thereby informing theoretical models of cumulative ACEs in PD and highlighting clinical thresholds for identifying individuals most in need of preventative interventions.

Current Study

The current study therefore aimed to examine the relationship between ICD‐11 PD severity and ACEs in a large community sample with a history of mental health treatment. First, we aimed to establish the association between ACEs and PD severity. Based on previous research highlighting a positive relationship (Lock et al. 2025), we hypothesised that increased ACEs would be associated with increased ICD‐11 PD severity. Second, we aimed to establish risk estimates (odds ratios and relative risk ratios) for the association between ACE exposure and clinically significant personality dysfunction. Reflecting prior research on categorical PDs (Broekhof et al. 2024), we hypothesised that increased incidence of ACEs would be associated with increased risk of having a clinically significant level of ICD‐11 PD severity. Third, we aimed to determine the presence of a dose‐response relationship between ACEs and PD severity. Given established dose–response patterns between ACEs and other mental health outcomes (Morris and Hays‐Grudo 2023), in addition to preliminary evidence for the effects of cumulative trauma exposure in older adults (Stone et al. 2023), we hypothesised that increased cumulative ACEs would be associated with increased PD severity in a systematic dose‐response pattern. Additionally, we explored whether this dose‐response relationship followed a linear pattern or exhibited non‐linear characteristics such as threshold or plateau effects.

Method

Our research questions and analysis plan were pre‐registered on the Open Science Framework (OSF) prior to analyses, which can be found here: https://osf.io/cw8xp/?view_only=12494057405448bba796992e7bb2621b.

The data for this study were obtained from two previous dimensional PD studies conducted by our research group at Monash University using identical recruitment methods (Martin et al. 2026). While the current project addressed novel aims and research questions not examined in the broader datasets, Martin et al. did examine the ACE‐Q in relation to overlapping symptoms of ADHD, autism, and PD in various latent variable models. Based on those findings, some association between ACEs and PD was expected; however, no detailed investigation of this relationship had been conducted and none of the planned analyses were completed at the time of the pre‐registration.

Participants

The present sample includes 918 participants. Of the final sample (N = 918, M age  = 40.29, SD age  = 12.58), 502 (54.7%) identified as female, 388 (42.3%) identified as male, 24 (2.6%) identified as non‐binary and 2 (0.2%) expressed their gender identity was not listed (specified transgender woman and transgender man). In terms of race/ethnicity, participants most commonly endorsed White/of European descent (79.1%), Black/of African descent (13.5%), Latino/a (White) descent (3.7%) and East Asian descent (2.8%). All participants had a history of mental health treatment, with 50.9% of the sample specifying that they were currently receiving such treatment, and 29.1% indicating that they had been previously hospitalised for their mental health.

Procedure

Recruitment was conducted via Prolific Academic (www.prolific.com) using their sampling criteria for United Kingdom (UK) and United States (US) residents, with English as a primary language and a historical or current mental health diagnosis. The latter inclusion criterion served to increase symptom response variability. Study advertisements were distributed via Prolific, which contained a link to the explanatory statement and a series of online self‐report questionnaires (described below). To maintain the anonymity of participants, consent was implied through the completion and submission of questionnaires. QualtricsXM, a secure web‐based application for research, was used to administer questionnaires online. The Monash University Human Research Ethics Committee approved the current data collection (approval numbers: 46937 and 47591).

MeasuresPersonality Disorder Severity Scale ICD‐11 (PDS‐ICD‐11; Bach et al. <xref rid="pmh70063-bib-0003" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>)

The PDS‐ICD‐11 is a 14‐item self‐report measure of personality dysfunction as defined by self and interpersonal dysfunction, emotional, cognitive and behavioural manifestations and psychosocial impairment/global distress. Respondents are asked to select the description that best fits their level of functioning; the first 10 items are rated on a 5‐point bipolar scale where the middle response score of zero reflects healthy personality functioning and dysfunction is represented in opposing directions at each pole, and the final four items are unipolar and scored on a 3‐point scale. Responses are summed to obtain a total where greater scores indicate greater severity of impairment. A cut‐score of ≥ 14 has the best support for indicating the presence of clinically significant personality pathology (i.e., mild, moderate or severe PD; Brown and Sellbom 2023). The PDS‐ICD‐11 has been validated for use in community and clinical samples with good internal consistency (e.g., Bach et al. 2021; Brown and Sellbom 2023; Martin et al. 2026). Internal consistency in the current sample was acceptable (α = 0.85).

Adverse Childhood Experiences Questionnaire (ACE‐Q; Felitti et al. <xref rid="pmh70063-bib-0012" ref-type="bibr">1998</xref>)

The ACE‐Q is a 10‐item self‐report scale originally adapted from the Childhood Trauma Questionnaire (Bernstein et al. 1994). Respondents answer yes/no [0–1] to each item, with higher scores indicating greater instances of ACEs. The ACE‐Q has been well‐validated across a range of clinical and community samples (Wingenfeld et al. 2011) and demonstrates acceptable internal consistency in the current sample (α = 0.81). In one of the datasets (N = 512), an administrative error resulted in the omission of item 4 (‘Did you often feel that no one in your family loved you or thought you were important or special? Or Your family didn't look out for each other, feel close to each other, or support each other?’). See data analysis section for more information.

Preliminary Data Processing Analysis

Prior to conducting the main analyses, we examined whether the administrative error resulting in one missing ACE‐Q item in dataset one (N = 512) created systematic differences in ACE‐Q total scores across the two samples. An independent samples t‐test compared mean ACE‐Q total scores across datasets, revealing no significant difference between dataset one (M = 3.32, SD = 2.55) and dataset two (M = 3.35, SD = 2.72), t(916) = −0.17, p = 0.87, 95% CI [−0.14, 0.12]. The missing item therefore did not meaningfully impact overall ACE exposure measurement. To further validate our sample equivalence, we examined the distribution of participants across ACE exposure levels. The proportion of individuals reporting up to four or more ACEs, a commonly used cut‐score in ACE research (Felitti et al. 1998; Hughes et al. 2017), was virtually identical across datasets (68% vs. 67%). These analyses demonstrate that the administrative error did not introduce systematic bias in ACE measurement, supporting the validity of merging the datasets for subsequent analyses.

Data Analysis Plan

All analyses were conducted using IBM SPSS Statistics v29 and RStudio v2024.12.0 + 467. Descriptive statistics were calculated across the total sample, including ACE‐Q and PDS‐ICD‐11 score distributions. Given missing data in the ACE‐Q for one dataset, Proportion of Maximum scores were calculated to ensure comparable ACE‐Q scaling across the merged sample. A zero‐order correlation between ACE‐Q scores and PDS‐ICD‐11 total scores was conducted to establish the dimensional association between childhood adversity and PD severity, using Spearman correlations given the non‐normality of the ACE‐Q data.

To establish risk estimates for the association between ACE exposure and clinically significant personality dysfunction, logistic regression analyses were conducted following the approach of Felitti et al. (1998). ACE‐Q exposure was entered as a single ordinal variable (0, 1, 2, 3, and 4+) into four separate logistic regression models with a binary present/absent clinically significant PD outcome (determined by PDS‐ICD‐11 scores ≥ 14 versus < 14; Brown and Sellbom 2023). Odds ratios and relative risk ratios for each ACE exposure level were then calculated to determine the risk of clinically significant PD at each cumulative level of ACE.

Finally, to determine the presence of a dose–response relationship between ACEs and PD severity, a regression model was conducted with ACE‐Q exposures entered as a single ordinal variable (0, 1, 2, 3, and 4+) and continuous PDS‐ICD‐11 scores entered as the dependent variable. As specified in our pre‐registration, as the relationships appeared non‐linear, polynomial terms (quadratic, cubic) were added to linear regression models and compared using F‐tests and AIC/BIC values for model comparisons.

ResultsDescriptive Statistics

As displayed in Table 1, ACE exposure was common within the sample, with 82% of participants reporting at least one ACE. This prevalence rate exceeds those reported in the general community (e.g., 22.4%; Madigan et al. 2023) and is consistent with the elevated adversity exposure commonly observed in individuals undergoing mental health treatment (Kessler et al. 2010). Notably, 42.8% of participants endorsed four or more ACEs, indicating substantial cumulative exposure within the present sample. PDS‐ICD‐11 scores ranged from 0 to 28 (M = 11.87, SD = 5.06, γ 1 = −0.19; κ = −0.009) and were approximately normally distributed. Overall, 28.4% (N = 261) of the sample met the criteria for clinically significant personality dysfunction (total scores ≥ 14). Correlations

To establish the association between ACEs and PD severity, we conducted a Spearman rank‐order correlation between PDS‐ICD‐11 total scores and ACE‐Q Proportion of Maximum scores. Results demonstrated a significant moderate positive correlation (ρ = 0.33, p < .001), indicating that increased ACEs are associated with increased PD severity.

Logistic Regression

A logistic regression analysis examined ACE exposure level as an ordinal predictor (0, 1, 2, 3, and 4 + ACEs) of clinically significant personality dysfunction (i.e., PDS‐ICD‐11 total scores ≥ 14). The overall model was significant, χ 2(1) = 28.28, p < 0.001, indicating that ACE exposure was significantly associated with clinically significant personality dysfunction. Each one‐unit increase in ACE exposure was associated with 1.30 times the odds of clinically significant personality dysfunction (b = 0.26, SE = 0.05, OR = 1.30, 95% CI [1.18, 1.44], p < 0.001). Separate logistic regression analyses then examined the risk of clinically significant personality dysfunction at each ACE exposure level relative to no ACE exposure. Table 2 contains the odds ratios and relative risk ratios for each level of ACE exposure. As shown in Table 2, compared to individuals with no ACEs (baseline risk = 14.5%), exposure to two ACEs and four or more ACEs was significantly associated with increased odds of clinically significant personality dysfunction, while exposure to one or three ACEs demonstrated no significant relationship with PD. A pattern of increased risk with greater ACE exposure emerged, where having 2 ACEs was associated with 2.92 times the odds of clinically significant PD, and having 4 + ACEs was associated with 3.41 times the odds of clinically significant PD.

Dose‐Response Relationship

To determine the presence of a dose‐response relationship between ACEs and PD severity, linear regression analysis was conducted with ACE exposure entered as an ordinal variable and continuous PDS‐ICD‐11 scores as the dependent variable. Linearity was firstly assessed through a locally weighted regression smoother, confirming a linear relationship between ACE‐Q exposures and PDS‐ICD‐11 total scores. We then conducted hierarchical regression analyses with nested models to further evaluate the linearity assumption underlying the dose–response relationship. ACE exposure was entered as a linear term in model one, with a quadratic term (ACE2) added in model two and a cubic term (ACE3) added in model three.

Results initially confirmed a significant dose‐response relationship (b = 1.19, SE = 0.11, p < 0.001), whereby each additional ACE exposure was associated with a 1.19‐point increase in PDS‐ICD‐11 total scores. The linear model explained 11% of the variance in PD severity (R 2 = 0.109, p < 0.001, AIC = 5662.43, BIC = 5676.90), and the addition of a quadratic term did not significantly improve model fit (ΔR 2 = 0.001, p = 0.324, AIC = 5663.47, BIC = 5682.75). Model fit was significantly improved with the addition of the cubic term in model three (ΔR 2 = 0.008, p = 0.004, AIC = 5657.08, BIC = 5681.19); however, the effect size was very small with less than 1% additional variance explained. Model comparison indices were mixed, with the AIC favouring the cubic effect model and the BIC favouring the linear model. These results thus suggest that the dose‐response relationship does not follow a perfectly linear pattern, which is illustrated further in Figure 1. A similar pattern was observed for the extended ACE categories (0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 and 7+), which is presented in Table S1 and Figure S1.

Discussion

The present study aimed to examine the association between ICD‐11 PD severity and ACEs, establish relevant risk estimates and determine the presence of a dose‐response relationship. Overall, there was a positive relationship between ICD‐11 PD and ACEs, and greater ACE exposure was associated with greater odds of experiencing clinically significant PD. A partial dose‐response relationship was also observed, whereby greater ACEs were associated with greater PD severity. Below we discuss the implications of these results.

First, we found a positive relationship between childhood adversity and ICD‐11 PD severity. These findings increment previous research highlighting associations between ACEs and categorical PD diagnoses (Afifi et al. 2011; Broekhof et al. 2024) by demonstrating that this relationship also exists when PD is conceptualised dimensionally. Our findings further contribute to the increasing literature on dimensional models of PD. Specifically, we replicate the results of Lock et al. (2025), who reported associations between childhood trauma and ICD‐11 PD severity in a small clinical sample, and extend them to a substantially larger community mental health sample. Additionally, we complement the findings of Borroni et al. (2019), who demonstrated that childhood trauma predicted maladaptive personality traits but did not examine the core diagnostic component of ICD‐11 PD severity. Taken together, our findings demonstrate that ACEs are associated not only with maladaptive traits of personality pathology but also with the central personality dysfunction common across all PD presentations.

In support of our second hypothesis, exposure to childhood adversity was associated with an increased risk of clinically significant personality dysfunction. Whilst the overall relationship between ACE exposure and clinically significant personality dysfunction was significant, only individuals exposed to two and four or more ACEs demonstrated significantly elevated risk compared to those with no ACE exposure. Given that exposure groups were of comparable size, this pattern may reflect qualitative differences in the specific types or combinations of adversities experienced at different exposure levels, suggesting that both quantity and type of exposure should be considered in future research. This counterintuitive finding notwithstanding, our findings corroborate prior research on categorical PDs (Broekhof et al. 2024) and extend this to the ICD‐11 framework. To our knowledge, this is the first study to quantify odds ratios for the relationship between ACEs and ICD‐11 PD severity, and indicates that ACE exposure may act as an important predictor of meeting diagnostic thresholds within dimensional PD models.

Finally, a partial dose‐response relationship was observed between cumulative ACE exposure and increasing continuous PD severity, supporting our third hypothesis. This finding aligns with previous research indicating that cumulative ACE exposure is associated with a greater risk of developing PD (Crișan et al. 2023). We do note that the dose‐response relationship observed was not perfectly linear; as stated earlier, individuals exposed to three ACEs demonstrated lower risk than individuals exposed to two. Despite this variability, we observed a general pattern of cumulative risk, whereby individuals exposed to four or more ACEs had the highest risk of clinically significant personality dysfunction, and individuals exposed to one ACE demonstrated the lowest risk. Further examination of this relationship in future studies is necessary to strengthen this conceptual understanding. It is worth noting that while ACEs explained only a small proportion of the variance in personality dysfunction, these results are consistent with prior research examining categorical PDs and reflect their complex and multifaceted aetiology (Winsper 2018).

The present study holds implications for the conceptualisation and treatment of PD. As individuals with two or four or more ACE exposures were most at risk of clinically significant PD symptoms, with the latter being associated with the greatest risk, future research may investigate the mechanisms underlying this vulnerability and whether targeted interventions for high‐exposure populations may mitigate personality pathology. Notably, the variability in risk across ACE exposure levels indicates that future research should examine not only the quantity of ACEs but also the specific types and combinations of adverse experiences. Certain adversities or patterns of co‐occurring adversities may confer particularly high risk for PD, and identifying such profiles of adversity may inform more targeted screening and early intervention efforts. The ICD‐11 PD model likely also offers greater utility than DSM‐5 categorical constructs for examining risk factors common to all PD presentations. Whereas previous research has established the relationship between ACEs and specific categorical PDs or maladaptive traits, using the ICD‐11 PD severity construct enabled us to highlight ACEs as a risk factor relevant to PD more generally. The ICD‐11 dimensional structure therefore supports the examination of risk factors for personality pathology that are either shared across presentations (via studies of severity) or specific to certain components (via studies of maladaptive traits). Future research should therefore consider using the ICD‐11 framework to elucidate such nuanced risk information.

The results of the current study should be considered in the context of several limitations. First, retrospective ACE reports were used, which may introduce recall bias and thereby invalidate our findings. However, retrospective reports are widely used in ACE research (Zarse et al. 2019) and have demonstrated validity (Fergusson et al. 2011). Nevertheless, future research should aim to replicate these findings in prospective, longitudinal cohorts, as in past categorical PD research (e.g., Broekhof et al. 2024). Second, one ACE‐Q item was omitted due to an administrative error, meaning scores were incomplete for dataset one, though reliability remained acceptable and total scores were comparable across samples, so this omission likely had little meaningful impact. Finally, the generalisability of our findings is limited by the largely White/Caucasian adult online sample. While we included attention checks, we acknowledge the risk of other forms of careless or deviant responding induced by online sampling (Brühlmann et al. 2020), and that participant context during survey completion could not be determined. Future research in more diverse samples is therefore encouraged to enhance generalisability.
