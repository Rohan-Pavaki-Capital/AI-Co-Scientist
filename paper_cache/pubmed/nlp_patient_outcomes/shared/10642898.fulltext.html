Treatment of non–muscle-invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC) is guided by risk stratification using clinical and pathologic criteria. This study aimed to develop a natural language processing (NLP) model for identifying patients with high-risk NMIBC retrospectively from unstructured electronic medical records (EMRs) and to apply the model to describe patient and tumor characteristics.

METHODS

We used three independent EMR-derived data sets including adult patients with a bladder cancer diagnosis in 2011-2020 for NLP model development and training (n = 140), validation (n = 697), and application for the retrospective cohort analysis (n = 4,402). Deep learning methods were used to train NLP recognition of medical chart terminology to identify seven high-risk NMIBC criteria; model performance was assessed using the F1 score, weighted across features. An algorithm was then used to classify each patient as high-risk NMIBC (yes/no). Manually reviewed records served as the gold standard.

RESULTS

The F1 scores after model training were >0.7 for all but one uncommon feature (prostatic urethral involvement). The highest area under the receiver operating curves (AUC) was observed for Ta (0.897) and T1 (0.897); the lowest AUC was for carcinoma in situ (CIS; 0.617). For high-risk NMIBC classification, positive predictive value was 79.4%, negative predictive value was 93.2%, and false-positive rate was 8.9%. Sensitivity and specificity were 83.7% and 91.1%, respectively. Of 748 patients manually confirmed as having high-risk NMIBC, 196 (26%) had CIS (of whom 19% also had T1 and 23% also had Ta disease); 552 tumors (74%) had no associated CIS.

CONCLUSION

The NLP model, combined with a rule-based algorithm, identified high-risk NMIBC with good performance and will enable future work to study real-world treatment patterns and clinical outcomes for high-risk NMIBC.

Bladder cancer is the sixth most common cancer in the United States, with an estimated 81,180 new diagnoses and 17,100 deaths in the United States in 2022.1,2 Over four times more common among men than women, and more common among White than Black or Hispanic individuals, bladder cancer is usually diagnosed at older ages (median age at diagnosis, 73 years).1-3 Approximately 70%-80% of patients with newly diagnosed bladder cancer have non–muscle-invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC), defined as tumor limited to the urothelium (stage Ta or carcinoma in situ [CIS]) or to the lamina propria (stage T1).4-6 Recurrence is common after initial treatment, with reported 5-year recurrence rates for NMIBC ranging from 50% to 70%, and an estimated 10%-30% of patients experiencing tumor progression within 5 years.6,7

CONTEXT

Key Objective

To develop and validate a natural language processing (NLP) model to identify patients with high-risk non–muscle-invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC) retrospectively from unstructured medical records of patients with bladder cancer.

Knowledge Generated

The positive and negative predictive values for identifying high-risk NMIBC were relatively high, possibly because of the redundant nature of the criteria whereby high-risk NMIBC could be identified from multiple features. The application of the NLP model and algorithm enabled us to identify and characterize a real-world population of patients with high-risk NMIBC, confirmed by manual chart review.

Relevance (F. Lin)

This study illustrates the successful deployment of NLP methods in the automated identification of high-risk NMIBC patient groups, guided by existing guidelines, using unstructured medical records. It highlights the methodological feasibility of developing phenotyping models using limited-sized training data sets. The performance metrics presented provide support for the application of these models in large-scale patient screening and observational research or registry development.*

*Relevance section written by JCO CCI Associate Editor Frank Lin, MBChB, PhD, FRACP, FAIDH.

The clinical approach to NMIBC is defined by the risk of recurrence and progression,4,5,8 with risk stratification outlined in American Urological Association (AUA)/Society of Urologic Oncology (SUO) guidelines according to clinical and pathologic criteria, including tumor characteristics and response to therapy.4,5 Currently, the clinical and demographic characteristics of real-world patients with high-risk NMIBC are not yet well defined with regard to the distribution of the high-risk criteria, and the AUA/SUO guidelines note the need to validate the risk groups in large, contemporary cohorts of patients with NMIBC.4,5,9

Electronic medical records (EMRs) can provide a rich source of information for understanding the real-world characteristics and clinical outcomes of patients with high-risk NMIBC. Identifying the AUA/SUO criteria for risk stratification using retrospective, real-world data is challenging, however, because the information is typically stored in an unstructured format, and there are no specific codes for NMIBC versus muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC). Natural language processing (NLP) models have been used to extract bladder cancer stage, grade, and evidence of muscle invasion from pathology reports10,11 and other types of clinical records,12 and NLP models have the potential to further enable AUA/SUO NMIBC risk stratification.

The objectives of this study were twofold: to develop and validate an NLP model that could identify patients with high-risk NMIBC retrospectively from unstructured medical records of patients with bladder cancer and to apply this model to retrospectively identify and describe characteristics of real-world patients with high-risk NMIBC from hospital-based health systems in the United States.

METHODSData Source and Patient Selection for NLP Model Development and Training

We drew on structured and unstructured data in patients' EMRs from 56 hospitals and 520 ambulatory clinics in the United States belonging to the Ciox Health network.13 Independent data sets were used for the three components of this study, namely, for (1) NLP model development and training; (2) NLP model validation; and (3) NLP model application for the retrospective cohort analysis. The training process included a testing component. With the validation data set, model parameters were tuned and model performance was tested. The study received approval from the WCG Institutional Review Board and was determined to be exempt from informed consent requirements (work order 1-1452901-1).14

For model development, training, and testing, we included patients who were age 18 years and older at diagnosis of bladder cancer recorded from January 1, 2011, to December 31, 2020, and who had evidence of bladder biopsy or transurethral resection of a bladder tumor (TURBT). The data set for training and testing (n = 140) was created by selecting eligible patient charts from the Ciox Discovery Lake (details are provided in the Data Supplement), with the goal of including at least 10 examples of different features, such as tumor stage, grade, histology, recurrence, tumor size and number, and degree of invasion, needed to classify patients with NMIBC according to the AUA/SUO risk criteria (Fig 1).4,5 After random selection of the first 100 patients, additional efforts were made to identify patients with less common data elements (eg, mention of lymphovascular invasion [LVI]) from key documents such as surgical pathology reports.

NLP Model Development and Training

Optical character recognition was used to generate machine-readable text from the medical records. Annotators labeled text within the training data, using tagtog-docs (v3.2021-W38.0),15 that could be used to map medical record terminology to seven of the high-risk NMIBC criteria: (1) high-grade (HG) stage T1, (2) recurrent HG stage Ta, (3) >3 cm or multifocal HG Ta, (4) CIS, (5) variant histology, (6) LVI, and (7) HG prostatic urethral involvement (Fig 14,5; the eighth criterion, failure of bacillus Calmette-Guérin [BCG] therapy in HG NMIBC, was not included in the model). Completed annotations were reviewed for accuracy by the lead data scientist, and ambiguous clinical scenarios were referred to a medical oncologist (D.S.) and a urologic oncologist (V.M.N.) for resolution. After annotations were completed, three charts contained no relevant clinical features, resulting in 140 cases included in the training and testing data set.

The final annotations were provided as inputs to two complementary NLP models developed to capture the 10 clinical and pathologic features from unstructured text (Fig 1): a general oncology NLP model to extract topology, histology, stage, behavior, and grade, and a bladder cancer NLP model specifically designed for identifying the AUA/SUO risk stratification factors, including recurrence, tumor size, multifocal tumor, LVI, and prostatic urethral involvement. Deep learning methods, including bidirectional long short-term memory and bidirectional encoder representations from transformers, were used in an ensemble to train NLP recognition of the relevant medical record terminology (see further details and URL in the Data Supplement). For example, if a human reviewer highlighted high grade in the patient chart, the NLP model learned to recognize reviewer highlighting and represented the matched text as a matched concept. The NLP-predicted highlights were then matched to the Unified Medical Language System16 with clinical features compiled for the International Classification of Diseases for Oncology.

The data set was split roughly 80:20 for training (n = 114) and testing (n = 26). Precision (positive predictive value [PPV]), recall (sensitivity), and F1 score (harmonic mean of precision and recall) for each feature and an overall weighted average were calculated based on the model predictions in comparison with the annotations from the clinical expert reviewers. The model performance (precision, recall, and F1 score) relative to sample size was evaluated by learning curves using training set sizes of 28, 56, 85, and 114 patients to determine whether additional training data were likely to improve model performance.

From the structured NLP model output data, each patient was then evaluated according to the seven AUA/SUO high-risk criteria as depicted in Figure 1.4,5 For each of the 10 medical chart features, a threshold was set that determined the minimum confidence score considered to be a positive result. A rule-based algorithm was used first to classify the patient's status as having NMIBC, MIBC, or unknown/no cancer from the binary values of the 10 features and second, if NMIBC, to classify the status as high-risk NMIBC (yes or no/unknown).

NLP Model Validation

For model validation, patients who were age 18 years and older with diagnosis of incident or recurrent bladder cancer (see the Data Supplement, Table 1, for listing of codes used) recorded from January 1, 2011, to December 31, 2020, were eligible for inclusion if their medical records included ≥1 year of available baseline data before the bladder cancer diagnosis, plus ≥1 year of data after the first bladder cancer diagnosis (unless death was recorded <12 months after the first bladder cancer diagnosis). Medical records of patients with bladder cancer from two groups of different Ciox Health facilities were used: collection 1 included 3,053 records, originating from approximately 50 hospitals and 450 outpatient centers, while collection 2 included 1,349 records originating from six hospitals and 70 outpatient clinics (Data Supplement, Supplemental Fig 1).

A total of 706 medical records pooled from randomly selected patients with bladder cancer in collection 1 (n = 296) and collection 2 (n = 410) were manually reviewed. Of the 706 records, NLP was successfully completed with 697 records (98.7%), which thus comprised the full validation set (Data Supplement, Supplemental Fig 1). Five-fold cross-validation was then completed on the criteria-level parameters.

Manually reviewed records were used as the gold standard for determining high-risk NMIBC, with the 296 records from collection 1 used also to analyze inter-rater reliability of the manual review process (see the Data Supplement for additional information and questionnaires used). For each of these charts sampled from collection 1, two trained abstractors completed a questionnaire with items related to the AUA/SUO high-risk NMIBC criteria.4,5 Inter-rater reliability was evaluated based on the binary classification of high-risk NMIBC (yes/no), with agreement analyzed using Cohen's kappa (κ). The testing of inter-rater reliability using collection 1 revealed only 13 disagreements regarding the binary classification of high-risk NMIBC (95.6% agreement; Cohen's κ = 0.884). With this high level of agreement between reviewers for collection 1, a single review was then conducted for the records in collection 2.

The high-risk NMIBC classification predictions from the model were compared with manual review results. The precision, recall, F1 score, and false positive rate (FPR) guided the parameter tuning and validation testing. Using the manually reviewed records as the standard of truth, the general oncology and bladder cancer–specific NLP model predictions were assessed at the feature level to generate a series of receiver operating characteristics (ROC) curves. During parameter tuning, disagreements between the model and manual review were examined by a data scientist, and inaccurate predictions by the model were minimized to optimize the model performance.

NLP Model Application to a Real-World Patient Data Set

For the retrospective cohort analysis, the validation data set was expanded to include the additional records from collections 1 and 2 (Data Supplement, Supplemental Fig 1). Patient eligibility criteria were the same as those for NLP model validation (see above), and the data cutoff date was December 31, 2021.

The NLP model was used to identify patients with bladder cancer with one or more of the seven AUA/SUO criteria for high-risk NMIBC,4,5 and the rule-based algorithm was then applied to classify individuals as having high-risk NMIBC (yes/no). Trained abstractors confirmed the classification of high-risk NMIBC using data from progress notes, operative notes, and pathology reports from TURBTs and biopsies.

Statistical Analyses

Model evaluation metrics included ROC, PPV, negative predictive value (NPV), sensitivity, specificity, and FPR. For the high-risk NMIBC classification, our goal was to achieve a point estimate of <5% for the FPR. A sample size of approximately 150 charts was needed for model validation with upper bound of 95% CI of ≤10%.

For the retrospective cohort analysis, descriptive statistics were used to summarize patient characteristics and the distribution of high-risk NMIBC criteria according to presence of CIS for the patient cohort identified by the model and confirmed by trained abstractors as having high-risk NMIBC.

Analyses were conducted using the R language for statistical computing, version 4.0.3 (R Core Team)17 and Python version 3.7.

RESULTSModel Training

The training and testing data set included the different features (ie, stage, grade, histology, recurrence, tumor size, and number) needed to classify patients with NMIBC according to the AUA/SUO risk criteria. Examples of the bladder cancer–specific features in annotations from the 140 patients in the training and testing data set included 86 patients with tumor size data, 58 with recurrence data, 28 with LVI data, 27 with multifocal tumor data, and 8 with prostatic urethral involvement data (patients could have multiple features).

Inspection of learning curves indicated that the NLP model performed poorly with only 28 training records (F1 scores ranging from 0.20 to 0.43; Appendix Fig A1). Performance improved substantially when we used 56 training records (F1 scores, 0.62-0.78); and performance leveled off between 56 and 114 training records (F1 scores, 0.70-0.79). Model predictions of tumor size had lower F1 scores compared with LVI, multifocal tumors, and recurrence (Appendix Fig A1). Prostatic urethral involvement could not be evaluated with a learning curve because we found only eight examples, of which five were included in the training set and three in the test set.

Manual Review and Model Validation

Manual review of collections 1 and 2 identified 325 patients with NMIBC. Overall, 203 of the 325 patients with NMIBC (62.5%) showed one or more criteria for high-risk NMIBC as confirmed through manual chart review (Table 1; Data Supplement, Supplemental Fig 1).

Of the 697 cases in the validation set, the NLP model and algorithm predicted that 214 cases were high-risk NMIBC. Of the 44 false positives, the most common were mistaken identifications by the model of CIS (n = 18) and variant histology (n = 15). Of the 33 false negatives, the majority were T1 disease (n = 15) that the model did not detect or muscle invasion (n = 8) detected by the model, which then incorrectly ruled out classification as high-risk NMIBC (Appendix Table A1).

Demographic and Clinical Characteristics of Patients With High-Risk NMIBC

Of 1,011 patients with high-risk NMIBC identified by the NLP model and algorithm, 748 patients (74%) were confirmed by manual chart review as having high-risk NMIBC (Data Supplement, Supplemental Fig 1). On biopsy or TURBT pathology reports of the 748 tumors, 196 (26%) contained CIS and 552 (74%) had no associated CIS.

Demographic characteristics of patients in the CIS and non-CIS cohorts were similar (Table 2). The median age at high-risk NMIBC diagnosis was 75 and 74 years, respectively, and the majority of patients in both cohorts were male (83% and 78%, respectively). Of those with known race, 92% and 88%, respectively, were White. Two thirds of patients in each cohort (64%-67%) were located in the South US Census Bureau region, 15%-16% in the Northeast, and 9%-10% in each of the West and Midwest regions.

Of the 196 tumors with associated CIS, 38 tumors (19%) also had T1 disease, 45 (23%) also had Ta disease, and 113 (58%) had no concomitant T1 or Ta disease (Fig 3). Of the 552 tumors with no CIS, 257 (47%) had T1 disease with or without Ta disease, and 295 (53%) had Ta disease. In the CIS cohort, the most common high-risk NMIBC criteria, present in 29%-51% of tumors, were HG disease, multifocal tumors, and a history of recurrence, as depicted in Figure 4. In the non-CIS cohort, the most common high-risk NMIBC criteria were HG disease, present in 99%, and multifocal, recurrent, or extensive (>3-cm) tumors, present in 31%-43%. DISCUSSION

The NLP model, combined with a rule-based algorithm, identified high-risk NMIBC with acceptable PPV and sensitivity (F1 scores, 0.70-0.79), particularly considering the relatively small number of training set charts. Although the ROC AUCs were <0.7 for three of the features (LVI, HG disease, and CIS), the PPV and NPV for predicting high-risk NMIBC were relatively high (79.4% and 93.2%, respectively), possibly because of the redundant nature of the criteria whereby high-risk NMIBC could be identified from multiple features. The application of the NLP model and algorithm enabled us to identify and characterize a real-world population of 748 patients with high-risk NMIBC, confirmed by manual chart review, among whom approximately one in four patients (26%) had tumors containing CIS, including, overall, the 11% of patients who had both CIS and concomitant T1 or Ta disease.

The percentage of patients with NMIBC who had high-risk disease as identified on manual review (63%) during model validation was somewhat greater than that reported in a previous retrospective study (56%) of 398 patients with NMIBC.9 Otherwise we found little other real-world data regarding high-risk NMIBC with which to compare our study results. In the retrospective cohort analysis, patient demographic characteristics of both CIS and non-CIS cohorts resembled those reported overall for bladder cancer,1-3 including diagnosis of NMIBC at a median age of 74-75 years and predominantly male patients.

A key limitation of the NLP model is the lack of a temporal component, thereby leading to the inability to make longitudinal assessments (either by abstractors or the NLP model). Therefore, the time course of bladder cancer progression could not be defined, and it is possible that a patient could have been incorrectly classified as having high-risk NMIBC, for example, if disease progression was from Ta (not high-risk) to MIBC with a risk factor (eg, LVI) captured after NMIBC progression to MIBC but not present earlier at the NMIBC stage. In that example, without a longitudinal perspective, both manual abstraction and the NLP model would result in an incorrect classification of high-risk NMIBC. Conversely, there could have been instances when true high-risk NMIBC was not identified, such as when less obvious examples of T1 were not captured. Another drawback of lacking the longitudinal relationship among tumor incidence, recurrence, persistence, and BCG treatment history was that we could not include the eighth AUA/SUO risk stratification criterion, the failure of BCG therapy,4,5 in the model.

This study demonstrates the creation and utilization of a new NLP model to identify patients with high-risk NMIBC tumors from unstructured real-world data. We drew on large data sets originating from numerous hospitals and ambulatory clinics located throughout the United States, although geographic diversity was somewhat limited, with two thirds of facilities located in the southern United States. The data generated could be explored further for predictive factors of disease progression, clinical treatment trends, and other variables associated with survival outcomes. The current NLP model considers all the available data for each patient to make an overall prediction of whether the patient ever had high-risk NMIBC. However, the natural history of bladder cancer can span many years, or even decades. Further work will include assessing high-risk NMIBC status longitudinally for each patient, once after every biopsy or TURBT.

In conclusion, the NLP model provides a promising opportunity to identify patients with high-risk NMIBC, enabling future studies to successfully use extensive collections of unstructured urology records. Future work will examine treatment patterns and clinical outcomes for these patients with high-risk NMIBC.
