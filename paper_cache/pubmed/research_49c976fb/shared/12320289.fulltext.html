Alzheimer's disease (AD) is accompanied by alterations in retinal vascular density (VD), but the mechanisms remain unclear. This study investigated the relationship among cerebral amyloid‐β (Aβ) deposition, VD, and cognitive decline. We enrolled 92 participants, including 47 AD patients and 45 healthy control (HC) participants. VD across retinal subregions was quantified using deep learning‐based fundus photography, and cerebral Aβ deposition was measured with 18F‐florbetapir (18F‐AV45) PET/MRI. Using the minimum bounding circle of the optic disc as the diameter (papilla‐diameter, PD), VD (total, 0.5–1.0 PD, 1.0–1.5 PD, 1.5–2.0 PD, 2.0–2.5 PD) was calculated. Standardized uptake value ratio (SUVR) for Aβ deposition was computed for global and regional cortical areas, using the cerebellar cortex as the reference region. Cognitive performance was assessed with the Mini‐Mental State Examination (MMSE) and Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA). Pearson correlation, multiple linear regression, and mediation analyses were used to explore Aβ deposition, VD, and cognition. AD patients exhibited significantly lower VD in all subregions compared to HC (p < 0.05). Reduced VD correlated with higher SUVR in the global cortex and a decline in cognitive abilities (p < 0.05). Mediation analysis indicated that VD influenced MMSE and MoCA through SUVR in the global cortex, with the most pronounced effects observed in the 1.0–1.5 PD range. Retinal VD is associated with cognitive decline, a relationship primarily mediated by cerebral Aβ deposition measured via 18F‐AV45 PET. These findings highlight the potential of retinal VD as a biomarker for early detection in AD.

We investigated the relationship between cerebral amyloid‐β (Aβ) deposition and retinal vascular density (VD) in Alzheimer's disease and their impact on cognitive impairment, using Aβ PET and fundus photography. We found that VD has the potential to serve as a biomarker for cognitive impairment, with Aβ deposition mediating this association.

Introduction

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a neurodegenerative disorder characterized by amyloid‐β (Aβ) and tau deposition in the brain, leading to cognitive dysfunction (Alzheimer's & Dementia 2024). Aβ is an extracellular aggregation of non‐soluble fibrils formed from the cleavage of amyloid precursor protein (Hardy and Selkoe 2002), while tau is an intracellular, microtubule‐associated protein that becomes hyperphosphorylated and forms neurofibrillary tangles in neurons (Wang and Mandelkow 2016). Aβ pathology is widely regarded as a hallmark for early diagnosis, whereas tau accumulation is more closely associated with disease staging and progression (Jack et al. 2024). Aβ pathological changes can be detected up to 20 years before clinical symptoms, making early screening particularly crucial (Jia et al. 2024). Positron emission tomography (PET) and cerebrospinal fluid assessments are the methods recommended by the National Institute on Aging and the Alzheimer's Association (NIA‐AA) for evaluating Aβ deposition. Among these, PET is less invasive, making it more acceptable to patients in clinical settings. Blood biomarkers have been incorporated into the diagnostic framework by the NIA‐AA but remain in the developmental stage (Jack et al. 2024). There is still a need for more cost‐effective and standardized non‐invasive assessment methods.

Aβ PET is a widely used diagnostic tool that allows for the observation of Aβ deposition distribution in the brain, effectively determining Aβ status. Its quantitative analysis provides a reliable biomarker for AD research, enabling the evaluation of treatment efficacy and monitoring of disease progression (Chen et al. 2023; Ciarmiello et al. 2019). Additionally, previous studies indicated that increased Aβ deposition was associated with reduced cerebral blood flow (CBF) measured by arterial spin labeling (ASL) (Falcon et al. 2024; Kapasouri et al. 2022). Correspondingly, vascular contributions to AD have received increasing recognition in recent years. In the Aβ clearance hypothesis, previous studies have suggested that Aβ transport across the microvascular endothelium (the location of the blood–brain barrier) is critical for controlling Aβ levels in the brain (Zlokovic et al. 2000; Mackic et al. 1998; Zlokovic et al. 1996). A guinea pig study revealed that a point mutation at codon 693 of the Aβ precursor protein gene impairs elimination of Aβ from the brain by reducing its rapid transport across the blood–brain barrier and the vascular drainage pathways (Monro et al. 2002). This mutation is associated with hereditary cerebrovascular amyloidosis and may result in the accumulation of Aβ peptides around blood vessels and in the brain. A prospective study demonstrated that vascular risk factors and their increase in midlife were significantly associated with elevated 18F‐florbetapir (18F‐AV45) PET standardized uptake value ratio (SUVR) (Gottesman et al. 2017). Subclinical vascular disease may mediate this association.

The retina, an extension of the central nervous system, shares a common embryonic origin with the brain (Sadda et al. 2022; Hinton et al. 2013; Patton et al. 2005). This anatomical link is reflected in their similar microvascular structures and blood barriers: the blood‐retinal barrier is analogous to the blood–brain barrier. This similarity supports the use of the retina as a visual window for exploring brain disease mechanisms, particularly to reveal the “retina‐brain” axis (Zhao et al. 2024; Kim et al. 2022; Ikram et al. 2012). An increasing number of studies have shown that the development of AD is accompanied by retinal manifestations (Ge et al. 2021; Xie et al. 2024; Gao et al. 2024; Alber et al. 2020). Fundus photography and optical coherence tomographic angiography (OCTA) are essential tools for measuring retinal vascular parameters (RVPs). A previous study demonstrated that transgenic mouse models of AD present more severe retinal capillary degeneration than the wild‐type (Shi et al. 2020). An OCTA study found that the healthy older adults at high risk for AD had significantly larger periarteriole capillary‐free zones compared to those at low risk (Arthur et al. 2023). This suggests that AD risk factors may contribute to intracerebral changes as well as retinal vasculopathy. Golzan et al. (2017), in their exploration of retinal vascular changes associated with Aβ deposition, further demonstrated a correlation between retinal arterial/venous pulsations and Aβ deposition in both HC and preclinical AD. Vascular density (VD) is a stable and reproducible indicator of retinal microvascular circulation, directly reflecting changes in retinal vascular degeneration (Curro et al. 2024; Wang et al. 2020). Another OCTA study showed that participants with Aβ PET positivity exhibited lower retinal VD (Bermudez et al. 2024).

Fundus photography, as the most widely used retinal imaging method that is convenient and cost‐effective, combined with deep learning (Cheung et al. 2022), can be an accurate measurement tool suitable for mass screening individuals at risk for AD. Frost et al. (2013) were the first to confirm abnormalities in RVPs in AD patients using fundus photography. Among these parameters, the asymmetry factor venule showed significant differences not only between AD and HC groups but also between Aβ‐positive and Aβ‐negative healthy control (HC) groups. Consequently, RVPs may provide another inexpensive tool to aid in the early warning of AD. However, the association of RVPs with brain Aβ deposition in AD and the effects on cognition remain to be further elucidated.

This study aimed to investigate the relationship among cerebral Aβ deposition, VD, and cognitive decline, in order to elucidate the potential of VD as a biomarker for detection in patients with AD.

Materials and MethodsStudy Participants

A total of 92 subjects were enrolled from July 2023 to July 2024 at Xuanwu Hospital, Capital Medical University, China, including 47 patients with AD and 45 HC participants. The patients with AD were diagnosed based on the National Institute of Neurological and Communicative Diseases and Stroke/Alzheimer's Disease and Related Disorders Association criteria for probable AD (McKhann et al. 1984 ). The HC participants were age‐ and gender‐matched to patients and had no cognitive decline complaints, depression, or anxiety, with the Mini‐Mental State Examination (MMSE) score ≥ 26. Participants with current or previous psychiatric or neurological disorders, such as major depression, stroke, brain tumor, head injury, and a diagnosis of high myopia, glaucoma, severe cataracts, history of intraocular surgery, and uncontrolled hypertension were excluded.

Data Acquisition

All participants were examined using simultaneous PET/magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) 3.0‐Tesla system (uPMR 790, United Imaging Healthcare, Shanghai, China; Signa, GE Healthcare, Waukesha, WI, USA) within 3 days of receiving color fundus photography using fully automated nonmydriatic fundus camera (Kestrel 3100 m, SYSEYE, Chongqing, China) and clinical neuropsychological scores, including MMSE and Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA). Specifically, imaging data from 20 patients with AD and 21 HC participants were acquired using GE PET/MR scanner, whereas data from the remaining 27 patients with AD and 24 HC participants were acquired on the United Imaging PET/MR scanner.

PET images were acquired 50 min after an intravenous injection of 370 MBq (10 mCi) of 18F‐AV45, with an acquisition time of 20 min on both scanners. For the GE PET/MRI scanner, MRI‐based PET attenuation correction was performed using a two‐point Dixon sequence. PET images were reconstructed with a matrix size of 192 × 192, a field of view of 350 mm, and a voxel size of 1.8 × 1.8 × 2.8 mm3. For the United Imaging PET/MRI scanner, MRI‐based PET attenuation correction was performed using Dixon water‐fat separation images, in which soft tissue, fat, and air were automatically segmented via a deep learning model trained on labeled Dixon images. PET images were reconstructed with a matrix size of 192 × 192, a field of view of 250 mm, and a voxel size of 1.3 × 1.3 × 2.8 mm3.

High‐resolution T1‐weighted imaging (3D T1WI) was acquired on both scanners. For the GE PET/MRI, the imaging parameters included repetition time (TR) of 8.5 ms, echo time (TE) of 3.2 ms, flip angles of 15°, and voxel size of 1.0 × 1.0 × 1.0 mm3. For the United Imaging PET/MRI, TR was 7.2 ms, TE was 3.0 ms, flip angle was 10°, and voxel size was 1.0 × 1.0 × 1.0 mm3.

ASL was performed with the following acquisition parameters for each scanner. For the GE PET/MRI, the parameters included a post‐labeling delay of 2.0 s (Alsop et al. 2015), TR of 4854 ms, TE of 10.7 ms, and voxel size of 3.7 × 3.7 × 4.0 mm3. For the United Imaging PET/MRI, the post‐labeling delay was 2.0 s, with TR of 5000 ms, TE of 14.0 ms, and voxel size of 3.5 × 3.5 × 4.0 mm3. Raw ASL data from GE PET/MRI were processed on the Advantage Workstation 4.7 using ReadyView software (version 10.3.67) within the FuncTool environment to generate whole‐brain CBF maps. For the United Imaging PET/MRI, data were processed on the brain perfusion analysis workstation using uWS‐MR software (version R006).

Brain Imaging Processing

MATLAB 2018b (The MathWorks Inc.) and Statistical Parametric Mapping (SPM12, Wellcome Department of Imaging Neuroscience, London, United Kingdom) were used for processing 18F‐AV45 PET, 3D T1WI, and CBF data. First, the 18F‐AV45 PET and CBF images were registered to their corresponding 3D T1WI images. The 3D T1WI images were segmented into gray matter, white matter, and cerebrospinal fluid. Subsequently, the 3D T1WI images were normalized to the Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) space using affine transformation. The resulting transformation matrix was then applied to transform 18F‐AV45 PET and CBF images into MNI space. The 18F‐AV45 PET images were converted to SUVR maps with reference to cerebellar cortex (Li et al. 2023). The CBF images were converted to relative CBF (rCBF) maps with reference to putamen (Bi et al. 2024).

The cortical region segmentation was manually drawn in MNI space using PMOD software (PMOD 4.002, Zurich Technologies Ltd., Switzerland) based on an MRI template. These region of interest (ROI) templates were previously developed in the Department of Radiology, Johns Hopkins University, and were validated in our former studies (Yan et al. 2021; Liu et al. 2019; Zhou et al. 2021; Li et al. 2025; Li et al. 2022). 18F‐AV45 PET SUVR and rCBF in each cortical region and global cortex were extracted for ROI‐wise analysis. A total of 14 cortical regions were included, namely Orbitofrontal Cortex (ObiFronCo), Prefrontal Cortex (PreFrontal), Superior Frontal Cortex (SupFronCo), Lateral Temporal Cortex (LatTemporal), Parietal Lobe, Posterior Precuneus (PosPrecuneus), Anterior Cingulate Cortex (AntCingulate), Posterior Cingulate Cortex (PosCingulate), Occipital Lobe, Postcentral Gyrus, Medial Temporal Lobe (MedTemporal), Inferior Temporal Cortex (InfTemporal), Entorhinal Cortex, and Fusiform Gyrus, as detailed in Figure S1 and Table S1.

Fundus Photography

The fundus photographs were processed using EVisionAI software (EVision Technology (Beijing) Co. Ltd.) to automatically identify retinal vessels and calculate VD (Figure 1). The preprocessing steps included ROI extraction, denoising, normalization, and enhancement. A deep learning semantic segmentation network, ResNet101‐Unet, was then used to segment the retinal vessels (He et al. 2016; Ronneberger et al. 2015). VD was calculated as the ratio of retinal vessel area to fundus area.

AI‐based workflow for extracting retinal vascular parameters. The preprocessing steps for the original fundus photograph included region of interest extraction, denoising, normalization, and enhancement. VD (total) referred to the VD of the entire retina, while VD (0.5–1.0 PD), VD (1.0–1.5 PD), VD (1.5–2.0 PD), and VD (2.0–2.5 PD) were extracted using the minimum enclosing circle of the segmented optic disc as the diameter (papilla diameter, PD).

To enable more precise measurement of VD, the optic disc was segmented. The deep learning object detection network (Single Shot MultiBox Detector) was utilized to locate the optic disc and generate a detection box. The center point of this box served as the origin for applying a polar coordinate transformation to the image. In this system, a vision attention‐based edge extraction algorithm was employed to determine the boundaries of the optic disc. Subsequently, using the minimum enclosing circle of the segmented optic disc as the diameter (papilla‐diameter, PD), VD was calculated at distances of 0.5–1.0 PD, 1.0–1.5 PD, 1.5–2.0 PD, and 2.0–2.5 PD from the center of the optic disc (Frost et al. 2013; He et al. 2024).

Statistical Analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using R (version 3.6.1) and SPSS (version 26). Group differences (15 tests in cerebral Aβ deposition; 15 tests in rCBF) were assessed using independent sample t test, Mann–Whitney U test, or chi‐squared test between AD and HC groups. Pearson partial correlation analyses (230 tests) were conducted to assess associations among 22 variables, including 5 retinal VD‐related parameters, Aβ deposition in 15 brain regions, and 2 cognitive scores (MMSE and MoCA). Multiple linear regression models were used to examine the association between VD and global cortical Aβ deposition. In these models, VD‐related parameters were treated as the independent variable, Aβ deposition in the global cortex as the dependent variable. Mediation analysis was further performed to explore whether Aβ deposition mediated the relationship between VD and cognitive performance. In these models, VD‐related parameters were treated as the independent variable, MMSE or MoCA as the dependent variable, and Aβ deposition in the global cortex as the mediator. Total effect, average direct effects (ADE), and average causal mediation effects (ACME) were calculated.

For all analyses, age, gender, education, hypertension, diabetes, and hyperlipidemia were included as covariates. p values from group comparisons and correlation analyses were adjusted for multiple comparisons using the false discovery rate (FDR) method, which was applied to each set of tests separately. In addition, the above analyses were validated using rCBF as a substitute for VD. All statistical tests were two‐sided, and significance was set at p < 0.05.

Mediation Analysis of <styled-content style="fixed-case" toggle="no">VD</styled-content>, Cerebral Aβ Deposition, and Cognitive Impairment

Mediation models were utilized to delve deeper into the contribution of VD to cerebral Aβ deposition and cognitive impairment (Figure 5). The ACME of VD on cognitive impairment (measured respectively by MMSE and MoCA) was significant for VD (total), VD (0.5–1.0 PD), VD (1.0–1.5 PD), VD (1.5–2.0 PD), and VD (2.0–2.5 PD) via 18F‐AV45 PET SUVR of the global cortex. Specifically, the ACME was 84.413, 76.663, 96.527, 91.484, and 68.562 for MMSE, and 96.648, 83.599, 111.085, 104.520, and 78.380 for MoCA. While ADE of all other VD parameters was not significant, ADE of VD (1.0–1.5 PD) was significant for MMSE and MoCA, and mediation proportion was 49.7% and 51.6%, respectively. This indicated that VD across all ranges has a distinct impact on cognitive impairment, with VD (1.0–1.5 PD) being the most critical.

Discussion

This study explored the association between VD and cerebral Aβ deposition, and its impact on cognitive impairment in AD. VD was generally lower in different regions of AD patients compared to HC participants, and it was negatively correlated with 18F‐AV45 PET SUVR in the global cortex and most cortical regions. Notably, VD (0.5–1.0 PD) and VD (1.0–1.5 PD) showed stronger correlation with 18F‐AV45 PET SUVR in the global cortex compared to VD (total), VD (1.5–2.0 PD), and VD (2.0–2.5 PD). VD proved to be a reliable indicator, demonstrating consistent associations with cognitive performance across multiple subregions. Further mediation analysis confirmed this relationship. The ACME of VD in each region on MMSE and MoCA was mediated through 18F‐AV45 PET SUVR in the global cortex, with VD (1.0–1.5 PD) exhibiting the strongest effect and a significant ADE. These findings highlight the potential of VD as a biomarker for detecting cognitive decline in AD and emphasize its key areas of focus, supported by the analysis of rCBF.

In recent years, AD research has expanded beyond the brain to include extra‐cerebral organs. The retina, as part of the central nervous system and directly connected to the brain, has drawn significant attention, particularly due to the frequent retinal vascular abnormalities observed in patients with AD (Gaire et al. 2024; Gupta et al. 2021; Zou et al. 2023). Therefore, our study focused on analyzing VD as a metric that directly reflects retinal vascular damage. A previous OCTA study confirmed that VD of the superficial capillary plexus was significantly decreased in AD compared to HC (Yoon et al. 2019). Another OCTA study showed that the VD of both the superficial and deep vascular plexus was lower in the AD group compared to the HC group (Jiang et al. 2021). However, the imaging field of OCTA typically focuses on the central retinal region. To comprehensively analyze retinal vascular changes, our study utilized fundus photography combined with deep learning‐based AI software for precise analysis of the overall retina and its subregions. We found that while there were no significant differences in overall retinal VD between groups, VD in each retinal subregion was lower in the AD group compared to the HC group. Furthermore, the VDs of the overall retina and each subregion were negatively correlated with Aβ deposition in the global cortex. This suggests a potential link between abnormalities in certain RVPs and cerebral Aβ deposition. This is consistent with O'Bryhim et al.'s study (O'Bryhim et al. 2018), which found that the foveal avascular zone was larger in the Aβ‐positive HC group compared to the Aβ‐negative HC group, suggesting that Aβ influences vascular dropout. Our study also indicated that VD (0.5–1.0 PD) and VD (1.0–1.5 PD) had stronger associations with Aβ deposition in the global cortex compared to VD (total), VD (1.5–2.0 PD), and VD (2.0–2.5 PD).

The mediation analysis of this study further revealed a significant and stable association between VD, cerebral Aβ deposition, and cognitive impairment. Importantly, this result illustrated for the first time the “retina‐brain” pathway through which VD influences cognitive impairment, and found that VD (1.0–1.5 PD) had the most critical ACME on cognitive impairment, with a significant ADE. The pathology of AD is often associated with cerebrovascular abnormalities, including vascular non‐perfusion, reduced blood flow, and vascular inflammation. Among these, the disruption of the blood–brain barrier is considered a key driver of cerebral Aβ deposition, involving impaired Aβ clearance and inadequate removal from the circulation (Sweeney et al. 2018; Taylor et al. 2024; Reas et al. 2024). Previous studies confirmed that specific retinal changes were associated with cognitive impairment, yet the pathway of this association remains unclear (Mei et al. 2021; Santangelo et al. 2020; Mutlu et al. 2018). Our data demonstrated that a reduced VD was associated with declines in MMSE and MoCA scores, significant mediated by cerebral Aβ deposition. As previously mentioned, damage to the cerebral microvascular system affects the clearance of Aβ in the brain, which subsequently leads to cognitive impairment. Based on the theory of homology between the retina and the brain, RVPs can serve as indicators of the status of the cerebral microvascular system (Chan et al. 2024; Crair and Mason 2016; Zhang et al. 2021). Therefore, changes in RVPs, represented by VD, may reflect cognitive impairment in AD via this “retina‐brain” pathway. Additionally, VD (1.0–1.5 PD) exhibited an ADE on both MMSE and MoCA, indicating that it can directly influence cognitive performance. We speculate that this may be related to retinal Aβ deposition, requiring further research to clarify this relationship. However, this also emphasizes the importance of VD (1.0–1.5 PD).

The ASL‐based CBF map could reflect changes in cerebral microvasculature to some extent. Previous studies confirmed that reduced CBF occurred in the early stages of AD and was associated with Aβ deposition (Falcon et al. 2024; Callewaert et al. 2024), supporting the hypothesis that vascular factors played a crucial role in the progression of AD. In this study, we replaced VD with rCBF for mediation analysis and found that rCBF also influenced cognitive performance through its mediation of cerebral Aβ deposition. This relationship further suggests that vascular changes in both the brain and retina are interlinked with amyloid pathology, supporting the feasibility of using VD as an indicator of cerebral microvascular conditions.

This study has several limitations. First, larger sample sizes and longitudinal follow‐up are needed to validate the current findings and to assess the longitudinal relationships among VD, cerebral Aβ deposition, and cognitive impairment. Second, this study did not utilize retinal imaging methods capable of detecting retinal Aβ deposition during data collection. Retinal Aβ deposition has emerged as a recent research focus (Dumitrascu et al. 2024), and the relationship between retinal vascular parameters and Aβ deposition, along with the underlying mechanisms linking these changes to cerebral alterations, merits further exploration.

Conclusions

In summary, we utilized 18F‐AV45 PET to explore the “retina‐brain” axis and demonstrated that reduced retinal VD is associated with cognitive decline. This is the first study to reveal the mediating role of cerebral Aβ deposition in the association between retinal VD and cognitive decline, and to delineate the distinct effects of the whole retina and its subregions, highlighting the critical role of VD (1.0–1.5 PD). These findings advance the understanding of the relationships among cerebral Aβ deposition, retina alterations, and cognitive decline, providing evidence supporting VD as a non‐invasive, cost‐effective, and reliable biomarker for early AD screening.
